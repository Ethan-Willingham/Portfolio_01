The Worst Pain in Medicine

Ten of the most agonizing conditions people live through, counted down. The famous chart that ranks the world's worst pains is a fake, so this one shows its work, and says where ranking pain stops being possible.

A ranked countdown / 33 sources / 13 min read

Four parts The fake chart The ten, worst last Also brutal Sources

Before the list

The chart you have seen is made up

The most-shared chart of the world's worst pains is made up. You have seen it, or one like it: causalgia 42, amputation 40, childbirth 35, all scored neatly out of 50. It looks like science. It is folklore.

The graphic borrows the name of a real tool, the McGill Pain Questionnaire, which doctors have used since the 1970s. But the real questionnaire scores one person's pain in one moment, on a scale that tops out at 78, not 50. It was never built to hand each disease a single fixed number. Nobody ever measured "causalgia equals 42." The giveaway is the scoring: every version of the viral chart is marked out of 50, a denominator that appears nowhere in the actual instrument, and the same condition gets quoted as 42, 47, and 48 on different sites. Melzack 1975 good

There is a real finding buried under the myth. In 1984 Ronald Melzack, who built the questionnaire, did measure labor pain against a few other conditions and found it sat near the very top, with causalgia. Melzack 1984 What got lost is everything around it: a later meta-analysis of 51 studies found that even severe-condition patients average only a quarter to half of the maximum score. Wilkie 1990 The honest version of the chart is blurry and full of error bars. The version that spreads is crisp and wrong.

Here is the uncomfortable part for a ranked list like this one. You cannot really rank pain across people. There is no thermometer for it. Pain is intensity and duration and dread and loneliness all at once, and my 8 is not your 8. Any top ten is a judgment call wearing a lab coat. PainScience So this one shows its work, and tells you where the work runs out.

How this is ranked

Four ingredients, in order of trust. One, the rare studies that actually compared conditions, above all the cluster headache survey in the chart up top. Two, suicide and suicidal-thought rates, a grim but real proxy for pain people cannot live with. Three, how constant it is, so conditions people live inside rank above one-off agonies that end, even when the one-off peaks just as high. Four, whether anything helps. The top three are close enough that the order is mine, not the data's, and I will say so when we get there.

One last caveat, because it is the question you actually asked if you came here from a search box. If you wanted the most common pain, this would be a duller list, topped by ordinary low back pain, which afflicts 619 million people and is the leading cause of disability on Earth. GBD 2021 Common and worst are not the same thing. This is a list about the worst: the conditions that, when the people who have felt them try to describe them, break the scale.

The countdown

Ten, worst last

Counting down to the one that fits the brief best: not the worst single moment, but the worst pain to be alive inside.

10

Gout

Comes in attacks~4% of adults

Start with the one almost everyone underrates. A gout attack is a genuine 10 out of 10 that you can usually stop with a cheap daily pill, which is the only reason it sits at the bottom of this list instead of higher.

Gout is uric acid going solid. When there is too much of it in your blood, it crystallizes into microscopic needles inside a joint, classically the base of the big toe, and your immune system attacks the crystals as if they were a germ, firing the same alarm (the NLRP3 inflammasome) it uses on real infections. The toe goes red, hot, and so tender that the weight of a bedsheet is unbearable, usually starting in the dead of night. It peaks in under 12 hours. StatPearls

It is also the closest thing to solved on this whole list. A urate-lowering drug like allopurinol dissolves the crystal supply, and the attacks simply stop. About 4 percent of American adults, roughly 8 million people, get gout. For most of them it is the worst pain they will ever feel, and it is almost entirely preventable. GBD gout

9

Kidney stones

Acute~11% of men, ~7% of women

The famous one, and it earns it. A stone is a hard pebble that forms in the kidney. The agony, called renal colic, begins when it drops into the ureter, the narrow tube down to the bladder, and plugs it. Urine backs up, pressure climbs, and the stretched nerves in the kidney fire in waves. People in renal colic cannot lie still; they pace and writhe, which is the tell that separates a stone from appendicitis, where you freeze and hold your breath.

In the cluster headache survey, kidney stones scored 6.9 out of 10. But a smaller study asked women who had been through both a stone and childbirth which was worse, and 63 percent said the stone, rating it about 9.8 out of 10. Only 19 women had experienced both, so hold that one loosely, but it is a striking number. 2017 study small sample The cruel twist: the pain tracks how blocked you are, not how big the stone is, so a tiny moving stone can hurt more than a large still one.

The mercy is that it almost always ends, often within a day, and it is very treatable. Agony with a happy ending.

8

Phantom limb pain

Constantup to ~8 in 10 amputees

Pain in a part of your body that is not there anymore. After an amputation, most people, by some counts 8 in 10, feel pain that seems to come from the missing limb: burning, stabbing, or the sense that an absent hand is locked in a cramp that can never be unclenched. Limakatso 2020

The pain is not in the stump. It is in the brain's map of the body. When a limb is gone, neighboring regions of the cortex creep into its vacated territory, and the more scrambled that map gets, the worse the pain. The proof runs in reverse, too. Mirror therapy, putting the intact limb beside a mirror so the brain "sees" two whole limbs moving together, can partly un-scramble the map and take the edge off. StatPearls

For most who get it, it never fully leaves. It resolves on its own in only about one in six.

7

Postherpetic neuralgia

Constant~1 in 10 shingles cases

The reason your doctor keeps nagging you about the shingles shot. Shingles is the chickenpox virus, which never actually leaves your body, waking up decades later to inflame a single nerve. In most people the rash heals and that is the end of it. In some, mostly older, the damaged nerve keeps firing for months or years after the skin clears.

That is postherpetic neuralgia: a deep constant burn, plus a vicious trick called allodynia, where the lightest touch turns to pain. A shirt. A breeze. The weight of a bedsheet. Some sufferers cut holes in their clothing to keep fabric off the skin. PHN review

It is one of the very few entries here you can largely prevent. The Shingrix vaccine stops most cases of shingles, and no shingles means no aftermath. The risk climbs steeply with age, which is exactly why the shot is aimed at people over 50. CDC

6

Chronic pancreatitis

Constant or recurrentorgan self-digestion

Your pancreas makes the enzymes that digest your food. In pancreatitis, those enzymes switch on too early and start digesting the pancreas itself. The organ is, in a real sense, eating itself, and that autodigestion is what makes the pain so savage. Cleveland Clinic

The pain is a deep, boring pressure high in the abdomen that drills straight through to the back. In the chronic form it becomes a near-constant companion: about 9 in 10 chronic patients live with pain, and many slide into opioid dependence trying to manage it. pancreatitis pain review In the cluster survey, acute attacks scored 7.0 out of 10, just behind childbirth.

The last-resort cure is as drastic as the disease. Surgeons remove the entire pancreas, harvest its insulin-making cells, and inject them into the liver to keep them alive, trading the pain for a diabetes you run by hand for the rest of your life.

5

Sickle cell crisis

Recurrentinherited, lifelong

An inherited disease whose signature is sudden, crushing pain, and a textbook case of that pain being disbelieved. In sickle cell disease, one tiny change in hemoglobin makes red blood cells stiffen into crescent shapes. The rigid cells jam in small vessels like cars wedged in a tunnel, and the tissue downstream is abruptly starved of oxygen.

That is a vaso-occlusive crisis: deep, grinding bone pain, often in the back, chest, and limbs, severe enough that guidelines tell emergency rooms to give strong intravenous pain relief within 60 minutes of arrival. AAFP It is the leading reason people with the disease are hospitalized.

They often do not get that 60-minute relief. Sickle cell patients, most of them Black, are routinely undertreated and quietly suspected of drug-seeking while in genuine agony. It is one of the better-documented racial gaps in American medicine, and it belongs on this list as much as the biology does. VOC opioid review good

4

Erythromelalgia

Recurrent to constant"man on fire"

They call it man on fire, and it is exactly what it sounds like. Attacks of burning so intense that patients describe their feet as scalded, packed in hot coals, or sliced with razor blades, while the skin turns visibly red and hot to match. Cleveland Clinic

The inherited form is one of the cleanest stories in all of pain science. A single misspelled gene, SCN9A, jams open a sodium channel called Nav1.7 in your pain nerves, so the alarm is stuck near "on." The same channel switched fully off produces people who feel no pain at all, which is why that one tiny protein is now a prime target for a new kind of painkiller. StatPearls good

Heat sets it off, so cold brings relief, and that is the trap. Patients plunge their feet into ice water for hours, and the prolonged soaking rots the very tissue it was meant to save, sometimes badly enough to cost the limb. Doctors specifically warn against it. The instinct to stop the pain becomes its own injury.

3

Trigeminal neuralgia

Comes in attacks, for yearsthe original "suicide disease"

The cruelty here is in what sets it off. Trigeminal neuralgia is a sudden electric shock through one side of the face, the cheek, jaw, or around the eye, lasting seconds, and it can fire dozens of times a day. What triggers it is the ordinary business of having a face: chewing, talking, shaving, brushing your teeth, a cold breeze. People stop eating and stop speaking to keep the attacks away. StatPearls

The usual cause is a blood vessel pressing on the trigeminal nerve where it leaves the brainstem, slowly wearing away the nerve's insulation until a gentle touch signal crosses into the pain wiring. The tell that this is electrical, not tissue damage, is the treatment: it responds to the anti-seizure drug carbamazepine and barely to ordinary painkillers or opioids.

It earned the name "suicide disease" long before anyone measured why. In a 2025 study, more than a third of patients had thought about suicide in the previous two weeks, over five times the rate found in ordinary back pain. J Pain Res 2025 good

2

Cluster headache

Comes in bouts~1 in 1,000

The worst pain anyone has actually measured. The word "headache" undersells it so badly that sufferers resent it. A cluster attack is a hot poker driven through one eye, strictly one-sided, that same eye going red and streaming, for 15 minutes to 3 hours, often at the exact same time each night, in bouts that run for weeks. Where a migraine makes you lie still in the dark, cluster pain makes people pace, rock, and bang their heads against the wall. Cleveland Clinic

This is the entry with hard numbers, the same ones in the chart at the top. Across 1,604 patients, people rated an attack 9.7 out of 10, and 72 percent gave it a flat 10, scoring it above their own memories of childbirth, kidney stones, and pancreatitis. Burish 2021 The nickname "suicide headache" is literal: in one study, 36 percent had active suicidal thoughts during attacks, falling to almost none between them. Lee 2019

It is about as common as multiple sclerosis, roughly 1 in 1,000, and skews heavily toward men. The strangest mercy on this whole list: breathing pure oxygen through a mask can stop an attack within minutes, a clue that the problem is the brain's wiring, not any wound.

1

Complex regional pain syndrome

Constant, often for lifestarts from a minor injury

Number one can start with a sprained wrist and never end. Complex regional pain syndrome, once called causalgia, is what happens when the body's pain alarm breaks in the "on" position. After an injury, sometimes a trivial one, a needle stick, a small fracture, the pain does not fade as the injury heals. It grows, spreads, and digs in: a constant burning in one hand or foot, with skin so sensitive that a puff of air or the brush of a sleeve is torture. The limb can swell, change color and temperature, sweat on its own, and grow hair and nails wrong. It can jump to other limbs. Cleveland Clinic

By definition, the pain is wildly out of proportion to whatever started it. There is no reliable cure, and it carries the bleakest numbers here: in one small study, 46 percent of patients had considered suicide and 20 percent had attempted it. Lee 2014 small sample

Honesty time, as promised. By the only contest anyone actually ran, the cluster headache one slot down scores higher in the moment. CRPS takes the top for a different reason: it does not come in attacks you can wait out. People live inside it, day and night, sometimes for the rest of their lives. That is the kind of pain this list was really about.

The honest ranking

If you ranked by the single worst moment, cluster headache would be number one. I ranked by the worst life, so a disease with no off switch wins. Reasonable people would order the top three differently, and the data does not settle it.

If you are the one in pain

Several conditions here are linked to suicide for a plain reason: unrelenting pain is dangerous on its own, separate from any sadness. If you are struggling, in the US you can call or text 988 for the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, any hour. Refractory pain is a medical problem with real treatments, and worth pushing a doctor hard about.

Also brutal

Five that didn't make the cut

A different day, a different ten. These were the closest to the line, each left off for a reason worth naming.

  • Endometriosis. Tissue like the uterine lining grows where it should not, then bleeds and scars on schedule. Women with it report a physical quality of life on par with cancer patients, and it still takes an average of 7 to 9 years to get diagnosed. Left off only because its pain is harder to compare head to head, not because it is milder. endometriosis QoL
  • Metastatic bone cancer. A tumor tricks the body's own bone-dissolving cells into eating the bone from the inside, and the dissolving bone releases acid straight onto the pain nerves. A single targeted dose of radiation can quiet it within days. Here radiotherapy is a painkiller as much as a cancer treatment. bone cancer pain
  • Fabry disease. An inherited enzyme fault that makes burning hand and foot pain start in childhood, set off by heat, fever, or exercise. Because those are just the ingredients of being a kid, it is often waved off as growing pains while it quietly damages the kidneys and heart. GeneReviews
  • SUNCT. A rare cousin of cluster headache: stabbing jolts around one eye that each last seconds, but can strike up to 200 times a day. Each one is brief; together they can swallow a life. StatPearls
  • Necrotizing fasciitis. The "flesh-eating" infection. Its earliest red flag is pain that makes no sense, far worse than the small, almost normal patch of skin above it, because the tissue is dying deep underneath. It can go from first symptom to fatal in under a day and a half. Left off as acute, not a life lived in pain, but few things hurt more while they last. CDC

The fine print

Sources, and how I checked them

I went in wanting a clean number for each condition. There isn't one, and the most-shared "pain index" turned out to be invented. So the ranking is a declared judgment, not a measurement, and the soft spots are flagged in the text on purpose: the small samples, the one recall-based survey doing a lot of work, and the top three that no data can separate. Every load-bearing claim was run through a fact-check whose job was to refute it.
