the world on **managing professionals** (knowledge workers and experts) so they go
fully **"all in."** Educational and impersonal: no names, no company. Manual labor
appears only as a contrast that sharpens the difference. By direction, this dossier
**excludes military-leadership material.**

Built from ~16 parallel research streams. Each section carries: core thesis, key
ideas, stats (with sources), the best quotes, case studies, and takeaways. Source URLs
are preserved verbatim for citation.

## 1. The frame: managing labor vs. managing professionals

**Core thesis.** The architecture of 20th-century management, built by Frederick Taylor
to squeeze productivity from repetitive physical labor, is structurally incompatible with
knowledge work. Knowledge workers own their means of production (their minds), cannot be
supervised in detail, and routinely know more about their work than the people above them.
Unlocking their full engagement requires replacing command-and-control with autonomy,
purpose, and a partnership model where managers lead by persuasion, not direction.

**Key ideas.**
- **Taylor's Scientific Management (1911):** replace rule-of-thumb with a scientifically
  derived "one best way"; match workers to tasks; supervise closely; split thinking
  (managers) from doing (workers). It worked *because* the task was simple, repetitive,
  and fully visible to an outsider with a stopwatch.
- **McGregor's Theory X / Theory Y (1960, "The Human Side of Enterprise"):** managers hold
  one of two self-fulfilling assumption-sets. Theory X: people dislike work, need coercion,
  avoid responsibility. Theory Y: effort is as natural as rest, people self-direct toward
  goals they're committed to, creativity is "widely distributed." Treat people as X and you
  manufacture the passivity you then cite as proof.
- **Drucker's knowledge worker (1959 on):** the manual worker is told the task and asked
  "how?"; the knowledge worker must first answer "what is the task?" Because the worker
  defines the job, autonomy is a structural requirement, not a perk. Drucker's six factors
  for knowledge-worker productivity are "almost the exact opposite" of what raises manual
  productivity. Manage professionals as **partners** and near-**volunteers**: partners
  can't be ordered, they must be persuaded.

**Killer stats.**
- Knowledge workers grew from **37% to 59% of the US workforce, 1950 to 2000** (≈3.1%/yr). https://www.researchgate.net/publication/220427053_The_growth_of_information_workers_in_the_us_economy
- Drucker's nurse case: stripping non-nursing chores **nearly doubled** bedside productivity, **more than doubled** patient satisfaction, and nurse turnover **almost vanished** in four months. https://newsletter.getdx.com/p/knowledge-worker-productivity
- Taylor's pig-iron experiment: **12.5 → 47 tons/day** (≈3.75x) under scientific management. https://www.masterclass.com/articles/understanding-taylorism-the-history-of-scientific-management-theory
- Services reached **79% of US employment** in 2023. https://www.statista.com/statistics/270072/distribution-of-the-workforce-across-economic-sectors-in-the-united-states/

**Best quotes.**
- "Knowledge workers own the means of production. It is the knowledge between their ears.
  And it is a totally portable and enormous capital asset." — Drucker, *Management
  Challenges for the 21st Century* (1999). https://edbatista.com/2008/10/drucker-1.html
- "Increasingly, 'employees' have to be managed as 'partners,' and ... partners cannot be
  ordered. They have to be persuaded." — Drucker. https://edbatista.com/2008/10/drucker-1.html
- "Once beyond the apprentice stage, knowledge workers must know more about their job than
  their boss does — or they are no good at all." — Drucker.
- "The essential task of management is to arrange organisational conditions ... so that
  people can achieve their own goals best by directing their own efforts toward
  organisational objectives." — McGregor (1960). https://yukaichou.com/gamification-analysis/theory-x-theory-y-mcgregor-management-assumptions/

**Case study.** *Drucker's hospital floor:* nurses listed paperwork, phones, flowers, and
routing calls as what impeded them — none requiring a nursing credential. A floor clerk
absorbed those duties; within four months bedside hours nearly doubled, satisfaction more
than doubled, turnover collapsed. Lesson: you unlock professionals not by speeding them up
but by stripping away everything that is *not* the work.

**Takeaways.** Audit your assumptions (X vs Y) before redesigning any process. Let the
professional define the task. Lead by persuasion. Treat people as assets to develop, not
costs to minimize. Protect the core work from administrative drift. Provide challenge and
mission, not just pay. Respect the expertise asymmetry: "I create the conditions; you
deliver the expertise."

## 2. Engagement and what "all in" really means

**Core thesis.** Professionals have a huge gap between their minimum required output and
their possible output. Engagement is whether a person chooses to close that gap with
initiative, creativity, and care. Because that "discretionary effort" gap is enormous in
knowledge work, the manager's behavior is the single largest lever on performance.

**Key ideas.**
- **Gallup Q12:** twelve statements (from one million interviews) that predict team
  performance. They climb four rungs: basic needs → individual contribution → belonging →
  growth. From Q3 up, every item is meaning, relationship, growth — not pay or conditions.
- **First, Break All the Rules (1999):** great managers select for talent, define outcomes
  (not steps), build on strengths, and fit the role to the person. "People leave managers,
  not companies."
- **"All In" (Gostick & Elton, 2012):** a "culture of belief" via seven steps — burning
  platform, customer focus, agility, share everything, partner with talent, root for each
  other, clear accountability. (The "Believe/Belong/Become" label appears in their related
  writing but is not the verified formal framework of *All In*; the seven steps are.)
- **Discretionary effort:** the bridge between engagement and performance — the extra
  energy a person *chooses* to give. For a manual worker the min-to-max gap is bounded; for
  a professional it is vast (a disengaged engineer ships working code; an engaged one solves
  the problem you didn't know you had).

**Killer stats.**
- Global engagement fell to **20%** in 2025 (64% not engaged, 16% actively disengaged) — lowest since 2020. https://www.gallup.com/workplace/349484/state-of-the-global-workplace.aspx
- Low engagement cost the world economy **>$10 trillion (≈9% of GDP)** in 2025 (supersedes the older $8.8T figure). https://www.mangoapps.com/articles/gallup-2026-state-of-the-global-workplace
- **Managers account for ≥70% of the variance** in team engagement (2.5M teams, 195 countries). https://news.gallup.com/businessjournal/182792/managers-account-variance-employee-engagement.aspx
- Top- vs bottom-quartile engaged teams: **+23% profitability, +14-18% productivity, -63% safety incidents, -51% turnover, -78% absenteeism, +10% customer loyalty** (Q12 meta-analysis: 736 studies, 3.35M employees). https://www.gallup.com/workplace/321725/gallup-q12-meta-analysis-report.aspx
- People who use their strengths daily are **6x more likely** to be engaged. https://getlighthouse.com/blog/gallup-marcus-buckingham-first-break-rules/
- Only about **1 in 10** people have the natural talent to manage well, yet most are promoted on tenure/prior performance. https://news.gallup.com/businessjournal/182792/managers-account-variance-employee-engagement.aspx

**Best quotes.**
- "Gallup has discovered that managers account for at least 70% of the variance in employee
  engagement scores across business units." — Gallup, *State of the American Manager* (2015).
- "People leave managers, not companies." — Buckingham & Coffman, *First, Break All the Rules*.
- "Good business is always commonsense, ... uncommonly practiced." — Chester Elton, on *All In*. https://www.cbsnews.com/news/get-engaged-why-your-company-should-go-all-in/

**Case study.** Gallup's 736-study meta-analysis across 3M+ employees: the engagement →
profitability and engagement → quality links are *especially* pronounced where output is
judgment, code, analysis, design, or client relationship — precisely because the
discretionary-effort gap in knowledge work is wider than in process-defined roles.

**Takeaways.** The job is conditions, not supervision. Clarity (Q1) before motivation.
Recognition is feedback (proof you're paying attention), not a reward program. Build on
strengths over fixing deficiencies. Draw the line from each person's work to the mission.
Transparency is a retention tool. And note the leading indicator: manager engagement fell
to 22% — managers can't give what they don't have.

## 3. The manager's craft: adapt, give feedback, coach

**Core thesis.** Managing professionals is an adaptive craft: read where each person stands
on a given task, flex your style to match, and spend your highest-leverage hours on
conversations, feedback, and coaching rather than on doing the work yourself.

**Key ideas.**
- **Grove, "High Output Management":** *A manager's output = the output of his organization
  + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence.* Leverage is the
  multiplier; the 1:1 is the highest-leverage hour. **Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM):** adjust
  style to the person's maturity *on that task* (low → directive; medium → two-way support;
  high → delegate). "Don't confuse TRM with general competence."
- **Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership:** D1 enthusiastic beginner → S1 Directing; D2
  disillusioned learner → S2 Coaching; D3 capable but cautious → S3 Supporting; D4
  self-reliant achiever → S4 Delegating. Seasoned pros are D4 in their domain but reset to
  D1/D2 on new responsibilities.
- **Goleman, "Leadership That Gets Results" (HBR 2000):** six styles — Coercive,
  Authoritative/Visionary, Affiliative, Democratic, Pacesetting, Coaching. Climate drives
  ≈⅓ of results; style is the primary driver of climate. Coercive and Pacesetting (both
  over-used by high achievers) hurt climate; the best leaders fluidly use 4+.
- **Kim Scott, "Radical Candor":** Care Personally × Challenge Directly. Failure modes:
  Ruinous Empathy (most common), Obnoxious Aggression, Manipulative Insincerity. Softening
  every message deprives professionals of the signal they need to grow.
- **Google Project Oxygen:** 10 behaviors of great managers; #1 *is a good coach*, #2
  *empowers, doesn't micromanage*; technical skill ranked **last**.
- **GROW coaching model:** Goal → Reality → Options → Will. Ask before telling; the pro who
  builds their own plan executes with more commitment.

**Killer stats.**
- Climate accounts for ~⅓ of financial results; leadership style drives **up to 30%** of bottom-line profitability (3,000+ executives). https://hbr.org/2000/03/leadership-that-gets-results
- Project Oxygen (10,000+ data points, 100+ variables): **technical skill ranked dead last**; coaching/empower/communicate ranked 1, 2, 5. https://rework.withgoogle.com/intl/en/guides/following-the-data-the-research-behind-great-managers
- "**Ninety minutes** of your time can enhance the quality of your subordinate's work for two weeks." — Grove. https://getlighthouse.com/blog/andy-grove-quotes-leadership-high-output-management/
- Managers who know employees' strengths are **71% more likely** to have engaged people. https://www.betterup.com/blog/project-oxygen

**Best quotes.**
- "A manager's output = the output of his organization + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence." — Grove.
- "The best leaders ... are skilled at several [styles], and have the flexibility to switch ... as the circumstances dictate." — Goleman.
- "Ruinous empathy is by far the most common mistake I see managers make." — Kim Scott. https://www.hrdconnect.com/2023/09/21/ruinous-empathy-kim-scott-on-dismantling-the-most-common-form-of-ineffective-feedback/

**Case study.** *Grove's TRM trap:* a high performer (high TRM) was promoted into a new role
(low TRM there); his manager *reduced* meetings instead of increasing support; he felt
abandoned and resigned. TRM resets at role boundaries — promotion doesn't carry it.

**Takeaways.** Measure yourself by team output, not personal busyness. Diagnose TRM before
choosing a style. Make the 1:1 the highest-ROI hour (report sets the agenda). Widen your
style range to 4+. Make peace with challenge — care personally, challenge directly. Ask
before telling (GROW). Coach daily, not quarterly.

## 4. Trust, psychological safety, and team effectiveness

**Core thesis.** High performance from professionals is *enabled* by conditions, not
extracted by incentives or oversight. The interpersonal environment is the mechanism, not a
soft precondition.

**Key ideas.**
- **Edmondson — psychological safety:** "a belief that one will not be punished or
  humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes." A group-level
  climate. Her hospital paradox: better teams reported *more* errors — they were safer to
  surface them. The challenge of knowledge work is safety **plus** high standards (the
  "learning zone").
- **Google Project Aristotle:** five keys, in order — **psychological safety**
  (foundational), dependability, structure & clarity, meaning, impact. "How the team worked
  together" mattered more than who was on it.
- **Lencioni — Five Dysfunctions:** absence of (vulnerability-based) trust → fear of
  conflict → lack of commitment → avoidance of accountability → inattention to results.
- **Coyle — "The Culture Code":** build safety (belonging cues), share vulnerability (it
  *precedes* trust), establish purpose.
- **Greenleaf — servant leadership:** "servant first." Best test: "Do those served grow as
  persons ... become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous?"
- **Collins — Level 5 leadership & "first who, then what":** humility + indomitable will;
  get the right people on the bus and motivation "largely goes away."

**Killer stats.**
- **85% of employees** have withheld important information from their manager out of fear. https://getleda.com/psychological-safety/edmondson-research
- Project Aristotle: high-safety sales teams beat targets by **17%**; low-safety missed by up to **19%**. https://rework.withgoogle.com/intl/en/guides/understand-team-effectiveness
- Leaders rated 9-10 by employees averaged **84%** psychological-safety scores vs **36%** for those rated ≤6. https://www.niagarainstitute.com/blog/psychological-safety-at-work
- **100% of 11 good-to-great companies** had Level 5 leadership during transformation. https://jimcollins.com/concepts/level-five-leadership.html
- Toxic culture was the **#1 predictor of attrition** in the Great Resignation — 10x more powerful than pay (MIT Sloan). https://businessleadershiptoday.com/the-culture-code-summary/

**Best quotes.**
- "Vulnerability doesn't come after trust — it precedes it." — Coyle, *The Culture Code*.
- "If you have the right people on the bus, the problem of how to motivate and manage people largely goes away." — Collins. https://www.jimcollins.com/concepts/first-who-then-what.html
- "Do those served grow as persons?" — Greenleaf, *The Servant as Leader* (1970). https://greenleaf.org/what-is-servant-leadership/

**Case study.** *Edmondson's hospital teams (1999, ASQ):* the best teams looked *worse* on
medication-error records — because they reported honestly while weak teams suppressed the
signal. "You cannot manage what people are afraid to report."

**Takeaways.** Safety is infrastructure, not mood. The best teams report *more* problems.
Vulnerability goes first, from the top. Fix the base of Lencioni's pyramid before
goal-setting. Serve the professional's growth (Greenleaf's test). Hire before you
strategize. Belonging cues are cheap and constant (don't glance at the laptop in a 1:1).

## 5. Autonomy cultures and the new manager

**Core thesis.** Professionals are not managed into engagement; they are *enabled* into it.
They hit their ceiling in high-talent, high-trust, low-friction environments where the
manager clears the path rather than directs the traffic.

**Key ideas.**
- **Netflix talent-density flywheel ("No Rules Rules"):** build talent density → increase
  candor → remove controls (in that order). **Context, not control:** give so much strategic
  context that people decide well without asking. **The Keeper Test:** "If this person were
  leaving, would I fight to keep them?"
- **Peopleware (DeMarco & Lister):** "The major problems of our work are not so much
  technological as sociological." Coding War Games: a **10:1** spread between best and worst,
  uncorrelated with language/experience/pay, correlated with *workspace*. Beware
  "teamicide." Protect **flow** (an interruption costs ~20 minutes).
- **Google "Work Rules!" (Bock):** default to open; top performers leave at **⅕** the rate
  when valued, doing meaningful work, with friction removed.
- **The new manager's redefinition (Zhuo, Fournier, Watkins):** your output is now the
  team's output plus the team's future capacity — half results, half team health.
- **Dweck — growth mindset as infrastructure:** a manager's mindset is contagious;
  growth-mindset managers give stretch work and treat failure as information.

**Killer stats.**
- Coding War Games: **10:1** best-to-worst productivity gap; no correlation to experience or pay. https://www.runn.io/blog/peopleware-book-summary
- Interruption tax: ~**20 minutes** of lost productive time per disruption; ~15 min to reach flow. https://www.shortform.com/blog/peopleware-productive-projects-and-teams/
- Google: top performers who feel valued leave at **1/5** the rate of the lowest. https://youexec.com/book-summaries/work-rules-laszlo-bock
- Microsoft growth-mindset shift (131,000 employees): growth-mindset favorability ~78-80%, "primary driver" of culture; market cap grew from stagnation to **$700B+**. https://www.neuroleadership.com/articles/how-microsoft-overhauled-its-approach-to-growth-mindset
- Replacing a person costs ~**4.5-5 months** of salary (Peopleware). https://www.shortform.com/blog/peopleware-productive-projects-and-teams/

**Best quotes.**
- "The major problems of our work are not so much technological as sociological in nature." — DeMarco & Lister, *Peopleware*.
- "If you dummy-proof the system, only dummies want to work there." — Reed Hastings.
- "The learn-it-all does better than the know-it-all." — Satya Nadella. https://fortune.com/2024/05/20/satya-nadella-microsoft-culture-growth-mindset-learn-it-alls-know-it-alls/
- "Your job as a manager is to get better outcomes from a group of people working together." — Julie Zhuo.

**Case study.** *Netflix, 2001:* forced to lay off a third of staff after the dot-com bust,
the remaining (keeper-grade) team moved faster with *less* process — the accidental
experiment behind talent density and the keeper test.

**Takeaways.** Redefine your job (team output + future capacity). Raise the talent floor.
Give context, not control. Protect flow. Practice candor as a system (the 4A model:
aim-to-assist, actionable, appreciated, accept-or-discard). Invest in growth; praise effort
and strategy. Eliminate teamicide behaviors.

## 6. Managing brilliant, creative professionals

**Core thesis.** The compliance-and-process playbook quietly destroys professionals. To get
a brilliant person all in, *reduce* management: strip the apparatus of control and replace
it with candor, team quality, meaningful challenge, and real trust.

**Key ideas.**
- **Catmull (Pixar) — the Braintrust:** senior peers give utterly candid feedback with
  **zero authority** over the director. The moment feedback carries power, candor dies.
  Protect the "ugly baby" (every film starts ugly) from the "hungry beast" (budgets,
  schedules). **People over ideas:** "Give a good idea to a mediocre team and they'll ruin
  it; give a mediocre idea to a great team and they'll fix it or throw it out."
- **McCord (Netflix) — freedom & responsibility:** most HR exists to manage the median and
  insults the excellent. "We hire fully formed adults." Dream team, not family. "A company's
  job isn't to empower people; it's to remind people that they walk in the door with power."
- **Horowitz — people, then products, then profits:** culture matters most when things go
  wrong. Good people leave because (1) they hate their manager or (2) they stop learning.
  "Being too busy to train is the moral equivalent of being too hungry to eat." Peacetime vs
  wartime leadership.

**Killer stats.**
- Companies with honest-feedback cultures showed **270% higher 10-year returns** (CEB). https://review.firstround.com/humans-hate-being-spun-how-to-practice-radical-honesty-from-the-woman-who-defined-netflixs-culture/
- **70% of employees** stay silent about issues that could compromise performance (Deloitte).
- In creative/inventive work the best are ~**10x** their peers (vs ~2x in procedural work). https://www.levels.fyi/blog/netflix-unique-culture.html
- *Toy Story 2* was **rebuilt from scratch in four months** after a Braintrust verdict → 100% on Rotten Tomatoes, $511M. https://mindsetonline.com/ed-catmull-pixar-30-years-braintrust-ugly-work-early/

**Best quotes.**
- "If you give a good idea to a mediocre team, they will screw it up. If you give a mediocre idea to a brilliant team, they will ... come up with something better." — Catmull.
- "Part of our job is to protect the new from people who don't understand that ... there must be phases of not-so-greatness." — Catmull.
- "A company's job isn't to empower people; it's to remind people that they walk in the door with power and create conditions for them to exercise it." — McCord.
- "Being too busy to train is the moral equivalent of being too hungry to eat." — Horowitz.

**Case study.** *Toy Story 2:* four months out, the Braintrust judged the story flat; the
team rebuilt almost the whole film. Candor stripped of authority made it possible to hear
the verdict without defensiveness.

**Takeaways.** Separate feedback from authority. Hire for team quality first. Remove the
apparatus of distrust (every monitoring policy is a signal). Tell the hard truth. Protect
the new idea long enough to judge it fairly. Train them or lose them. Know whether you're in
peacetime or wartime. Use the keeper test honestly.

## 7. The motivation canon (the intellectual backbone)

**Core thesis.** The assumption that professionals work harder for more pay/incentives is
empirically wrong for cognitive, creative, expert work. Extrinsic controls *crowd out* the
intrinsic motivation that drives professional excellence. What produces full engagement:
meaning, autonomy, and visible progress. The manager's job is to clear the fuel away, not
light the fire.

**Key ideas.**
- **Herzberg's two factors:** *hygiene* (pay, policy, conditions, supervision) only prevents
  dissatisfaction; *motivators* (achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility,
  growth) are the only things that motivate. Fix via **vertical job loading** (enrich depth,
  not add tasks). KITA ("kick in the pants") makes people *move*, not *motivated*.
- **Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan):** three innate needs — **autonomy, competence,
  relatedness.** The **overjustification effect:** contingent rewards for already-interesting
  work *reduce* intrinsic motivation. Controlling management signals distrust and depletes.
- **Pink's "Drive":** Motivation 3.0 = **Autonomy, Mastery, Purpose.** Carrot-and-stick fits
  routine/algorithmic work and backfires on heuristic/creative work.
- **Maslow:** once basics are met, professionals manage toward **self-actualization** — "What
  a man can be, he must be."
- **Amabile & Kramer, the Progress Principle:** the largest diary study of work motivation
  (238 workers, ~12,000 entries). The #1 daily motivator is **progress in meaningful work.**
  Catalysts (clear goals, autonomy, resources, help) + nourishers (respect, encouragement).
  "Small everyday hassles hold more sway than small everyday supports."
- **Csikszentmihalyi's Flow:** clear goal + immediate feedback + challenge-skill match. Below
  → boredom; above → anxiety; the corridor between is peak performance.
- **Ariely on meaning:** ignoring someone's work is *almost as demotivating as destroying it.*

**Killer stats.**
- Herzberg's "One More Time..." had **1.2M reprints** by 1987 — the most-reprinted HBR article ever. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_Herzberg
- Deci/Koestner/Ryan 1999 meta-analysis (**128 experiments**): tangible engagement-contingent rewards undermined intrinsic motivation **d = -0.40**; positive verbal feedback **enhanced** it **d = +0.33**. https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/A-meta-analytic-review-of-experiments-examining-the-Deci-Koestner/8ad9801baea65b40fbbe6fc56e34b2b7be47d0ba
- Glucksberg candle problem: the incentivized group took **~3.5 minutes longer** on the creative version. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candle_problem
- Ariely's Bionicle/"Sisyphus" study: meaningful condition built **11 vs 7** and persisted to a **42% lower** per-unit wage. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/222404618_Man's_Search_for_Meaning_The_Case_of_Legos
- Ariely acknowledgment study: acknowledged workers stopped at **15¢/page** vs **27.5¢** (ignored) vs **29¢** (shredded). https://www.ted.com/talks/dan_ariely_what_makes_us_feel_good_about_our_work

**Best quotes.**
- "I can charge a man's battery, and then recharge it ... But it is only when he has his own generator that we can talk about motivation." — Herzberg.
- "If you want people to do a good job, give them a good job to do." — Herzberg.
- "Of all the positive events that influence inner work life, the single most powerful is progress in meaningful work." — Amabile & Kramer. https://hbr.org/2011/05/the-power-of-small-wins
- "The best moments usually occur when a person's body or mind is stretched to its limits in a voluntary effort to accomplish something difficult and worthwhile." — Csikszentmihalyi.

**Case studies.** *Herzberg/AT&T job enrichment:* letting correspondents sign their own
letters and own accuracy raised a service index from ~25% to ~95% in three months, no pay
change. *Atlassian "FedEx Days" / Google 20%:* a day of pure autonomy "to ship something"
produced signature products.

**Takeaways.** Hygiene first, but only first. Kill if-then rewards for creative work. Enrich
the work, not the perks. Protect autonomy (micromanagement reads as "I don't trust your
competence"). Make progress visible (small wins). Never ignore work product. Match challenge
to skill. Connect to purpose.

> **Data reconciliation note (engagement figures).** Sources cite Gallup variously: ~20-23%
> engaged globally (report year dependent) and lost productivity of ~$8.8T historically, with
> newer framings near $8.9-10T (and a one-year *drop* costing ~$438B). For the essay, use the
> robust, defensible framing: **"about 1 in 5 employees worldwide are engaged"** and
> **"disengagement costs roughly $8.8 trillion, about 9% of global GDP"** (Gallup), plus the
> very robust **"managers account for ~70% of the variance in team engagement."** Avoid
> over-precise contested year figures.

## 8. For Your Improvement (FYI) — the centerpiece module

**Core thesis.** FYI is a practitioner's development-and-coaching reference built on one
premise: **leadership competencies are learnable.** For each named competency it gives a
diagnosis plus ~10 concrete development actions you can take without leaving the job. People
plateau not from what they know but from fixable skill gaps and derailers.

**What it is / history.** Authors **Michael M. Lombardo & Robert W. Eichinger** (both ex-Center
for Creative Leadership). Founded **Lominger** (1991); built the **Leadership Architect**
library (67 competencies). FYI is the development guide that sits beside 360 feedback and
**individual development plans (IDPs)**. **Korn Ferry acquired Lominger in 2006** ($24M). In
2014 Korn Ferry released the **Korn Ferry Leadership Architect (KFLA)** — **38 competencies**
in **4 factors / 12 clusters** (the 6th edition of FYI, 2014, is built on this). Note the
edition difference: 5th ed = 67 competencies (Lominger); 6th ed = 38 (Korn Ferry).

**The current KFLA model (38 competencies, 4 factors, 12 clusters):**
- **THOUGHT** — *Understanding the business:* Business Insight, Customer Focus, Financial
  Acumen, Tech Savvy. *Making complex decisions:* Manages Complexity, Decision Quality,
  Balances Stakeholders. *Creating the new & different:* Global Perspective, Cultivates
  Innovation, Strategic Mindset.
- **RESULTS** — *Taking initiative:* Action Oriented, Resourcefulness. *Managing execution:*
  Directs Work, Plans & Aligns, Optimizes Work Processes. *Focusing on performance:* Ensures
  Accountability, Drives Results.
- **PEOPLE** — *Building collaborative relationships:* Collaborates, Manages Conflict,
  Interpersonal Savvy, Builds Networks. *Optimizing diverse talent:* Attracts Top Talent,
  Develops Talent, Values Differences, Builds Effective Teams. *Influencing people:*
  Communicates Effectively, **Drives Engagement**, Organizational Savvy, Persuades, Drives
  Vision & Purpose.
- **SELF** — *Being authentic:* Courage, Instills Trust. *Being open:* Demonstrates
  Self-Awareness, Self-Development. *Being flexible & adaptable:* Manages Ambiguity, Nimble
  Learning, Being Resilient, Situational Adaptability.
- Plus **10 Career Stallers & Stoppers** (derailers) in 3 clusters.

**Each competency entry contains:** an opening quote; **behavioral definitions** (Skilled /
Less Skilled / Talented / Overused); **Some Causes** (root causes of a gap); **The Map** (why
it matters); **~10 development tips/remedies** (the signature feature, practice-oriented);
**Job Assignments** (on-the-job stretch); **Suggested Readings.** The 6th ed adds a
developmental-difficulty rating and competency connections.

**Development philosophy.** (1) Competencies are learnable. (2) **70-20-10:** ~70% of
development from challenging on-the-job experience, ~20% from relationships/feedback, ~10%
from coursework — so the tips skew toward what you *do at work.* (3) **Learning agility**
(Lombardo & Eichinger's construct: the willingness and ability to learn from experience and
apply it to new, tough, first-time situations; dimensions: mental, people, change, results)
— a better predictor of potential than IQ or past performance. (4) **IDP discipline:** work
on **one or two** priority competencies, not ten.

**Most relevant to managing professionals / engagement:** **Drives Engagement** (successor to
classic "Motivating Others"); **Develops Talent** ("Developing Direct Reports & Others");
**Builds Effective Teams**; **Directs Work** (clear *outcomes*, minimal process prescription —
the key for experts); **Instills Trust** (the competency most tied to professional
engagement); **Courage** (honest developmental feedback); **Situational Adaptability** (shift
between directing/coaching/delegating); listening (folded into Communicates Effectively).

**Representative FYI-style development tips** (constructed to represent the approach; not
verbatim from the book — flag as such): *Drives Engagement* — before delegating any task for
a week, write one sentence on why it matters and say it aloud when assigning; meet the two
least-engaged reports and give them more of the work they find meaningful. *Develops Talent* —
if anyone has done the same work >1 year, design one stretch task this quarter (cross-functional
project, exec presentation, temporary lead).

**Best quotes (secondary-sourced).**
- "The number one developer of competence by far is stretching, challenging jobs — not feedback, not courses, not role models." — FYI development philosophy.
- "For development to make a lasting difference, people must be clear on what skill ... they need to improve, be motivated to make the change, and know what steps to take."
- "Anyone who has not yet recognized and accepted a need or limitation ... will not be helped by what's in this book." — FYI introduction.

recoverable online; "Drives Vision & Purpose" cluster placement varies by source (People vs
Results); competency numbers vary by source; the example tips are representative, not verbatim.

## 9. Recognition, ownership, and purpose

**Core thesis.** Three deeper-than-pay drivers turn compliance into "all in": **recognition**
(specific, frequent, in the form the person values), **ownership** (transparency + a real
stake → owner-grade judgment), and **purpose** (a legible "why" that makes work vocational).
They fail independently and compound together.

**Key ideas.**
- **The Carrot Principle (Gostick & Elton):** recognition *amplifies* goal-setting,
  communication, trust, and accountability. The "wrong language" failure mode.
- **5 Languages of Appreciation (Chapman & White):** words of affirmation, quality time, acts
  of service, tangible gifts, (appropriate) touch. Recognition in the wrong language lands as
  no recognition.
- **Great Game of Business / open-book (Jack Stack):** teach everyone the numbers → follow the
  score → give a stake. Financial literacy converts compliance into stewardship.
- **Job crafting / purpose (Wrzesniewski):** Job / Career / Calling split ~evenly regardless of
  role; the hospital cleaners who saw themselves as part of healing behaved differently doing
  identical work.

**Killer stats.**
- Not-adequately-recognized employees are **3x more likely** to say they'll quit next year (Gallup). https://www.gallup.com/topic/recognition.aspx
- Top-quartile-recognition firms: **ROE 8.7% vs 2.4%** bottom quartile (10-yr, 200,000-person study). https://www.mindtools.com/a2wtxlt/the-carrot-principle/
- S-corp ESOP voluntary quit rates run ~**1/3 of the national average.** https://www.nceo.org/research/research-findings-on-employee-ownership
- Employees whose **purpose is fulfilled by work are 6x more likely to want to stay** (McKinsey, 2021). https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/help-your-employees-find-purpose-or-watch-them-leave
- Recognition delivery gap: **51% of managers** think they recognize well; only **17% of employees** agree. https://www.shortform.com/summary/the-5-languages-of-appreciation-in-the-workplace-summary-gary-chapman-and-paul-white
- SRC open-book: revenue **$16M → $83M** in a decade; equity ~$100K → $25M; hourly ESOP balances ~$35K → $400K. https://www.greatgame.com/jack-stack

**Best quotes.**
- "The best, most efficient, most profitable way to operate a business is to give everybody ... a voice in saying how the company is run and a stake in the financial outcome, good or bad." — Jack Stack.
- "When people think and act like owners, it changes everything." — Jack Stack.
- "If you think 'I'm an ambassador to the hospital,' it changes what you do." — Amy Wrzesniewski.

**Case studies.** *SRC Holdings:* opened the books, taught shop-floor workers to read a P&L;
owner-grade decisions followed. *Hospital cleaners:* same duties, a different story about "why,"
produced unprompted comfort, water, company for lonely patients.

**Takeaways.** Recognize frequently, specifically, in the right language. Open the books and
teach the game. Give a *real* stake (not hypothetical). Surface and reinforce the "why." Find
each person's shortest leg (recognition / ownership / purpose) and lengthen it first.

## 10. Managing professional-service firms (Maister)

**Core thesis.** Professionals (who sell expertise, not time) cannot be managed, only led. Treat
a lawyer/consultant/engineer/physician like a supervised employee and you sever the motivational
engine. David Maister's PSF research is the most rigorous case that **autonomy, mastery,
challenge, and peer respect** are the real levers, and that **engagement causes profit** (not the
reverse).

**Key ideas.**
- **Three goals (causally sequential):** great client service, satisfying careers, financial
  success — engage people → they serve clients → profit follows.
- **Finders / Minders / Grinders** (leverage pyramid) and **Brains / Grey-hair / Procedure**
  project types — staffing must match the work, and the **apprenticeship covenant** (seniors
  develop juniors) must be honored.
- **Dynamos / Cruisers / Losers:** most pros are *Cruisers* (coasting). The job isn't firing the
  bottom; it's **re-igniting the middle** with genuine stretch + visible investment.
- **Consent-based leadership ("First Among Equals"):** experts "must agree to be influenced."
  Title-authority is worthless among peers; influence comes from caring more about their success
  than your own.
- **"Practice What You Preach":** a measured causal chain from coaching/high standards → employee
  satisfaction → client quality → revenue.

**Killer stats.**
- **65-85% of professionals are "Cruisers"** at any time; only 10-20% Dynamos. https://www.bookey.app/book/true-professionalism
- Maister surveyed **5,589 professionals, 139 offices, 29 firms, 15 countries**; engagement
  **causes** measurable financial gains. https://www.amazon.com/Practice-What-You-Preach-Achievement/dp/0743223209
- Leader **autonomy support → job satisfaction r = .56, turnover intention r = -.40** (meta-analysis, 72 studies, N=32,870). https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6133074/
- Losing a senior professional ≈ a **seven-figure** hole (partner cash comp ~$672K in 2023; replacement 50-200% of salary). https://www.heidrick.com/en/insights/professional-technology-services/2024-us-professional-services-partner-compensation-survey

**Best quotes.**
- "For professional work, both productivity and quality are highly correlated with the degree to which the professional worker is engaged and committed to the task at hand." — Maister.
- "Managers should be hassle absorbers, not hassle creators." — Maister.
- "Leaders will best enable their people to achieve peak performance not by managing them, not by leading them, but by inspiring them." — Maister & McKenna.

**Case study.** *McKinsey's "obligation to dissent":* every professional, regardless of tenure,
is expected to speak up against flawed analysis; partners invite challenge; week-long
uninterrupted training signals development is non-negotiable.

**Takeaways.** Stop managing, start developing (reward developing others). Honor the
apprenticeship contract. Stretch or stagnate. Leadership is earned by consent. Invest in the
coasting middle. Prove you practice what you preach. Autonomy is freedom over *method* with
full accountability for *results* — not permissiveness.

## 11. Coaching as the core mode of leading professionals

**Core thesis.** The manager who asks, listens, and develops systematically outperforms the one
who directs and advises. For professionals, **coaching is the job**, not an add-on.

**Key ideas.**
- **Bill Campbell ("Trillion Dollar Coach"):** "It's the people." Free-form listening + a
  4-part 1:1 (performance / peer relationships / leading their own team / innovation). The "five
  words on a whiteboard" agenda ritual. **Team first** ("work the team, not the problem"). Candor
  delivered privately, immediately, with genuine care. "Believe in people more than they believe
  in themselves."
- **"The Coaching Habit" (Bungay Stanier):** tame the **Advice Monster** (Tell-it / Save-it /
  Control-it). The **7 questions** — Kickstart ("What's on your mind?"), AWE ("And what else?"),
  Focus ("What's the real challenge here for you?"), Foundation ("What do you want?"), Lazy ("How
  can I help?"), Strategic, Learning ("What was most useful for you?"). "Stay curious a little
  longer."
- **Project Oxygen:** "is a good coach" is the **#1** behavior of great managers.

**Killer stats.**
- ~**70%** of engagement variance is the manager (27M employees). https://news.gallup.com/businessjournal/182792/managers-account-variance-employee-engagement.aspx
- Google manager favorability rose **83% → 88%** after coaching-based development; worst managers improved most. https://hbr.org/2013/12/how-google-sold-its-engineers-on-management

**Best quotes.**
- "Coaching is no longer a specialty; you cannot be a good manager without being a good coach." — Schmidt/Rosenberg/Eagle.
- "A coach is someone who tells you what you don't want to hear, who has you see what you don't want to see, so you can be who you have always known you could be." — *Trillion Dollar Coach*.
- "Your title makes you a manager. Your people make you a leader." — Bill Campbell.
- "Tell less and ask more. Your advice is not as good as you think it is." — Bungay Stanier.

**Case study.** *Campbell at Google:* opened team meetings with weekend "trip reports" to build
the psychological safety that makes honest professional exchange possible — deliberate
infrastructure, not icebreaker theater.

**Takeaways.** Open 1:1s with "What's on your mind?" and don't fill the silence. Kill the advice
monster. Use a 4-dimension 1:1. Build the team before the problem. Candor + care, private,
immediate. Close with "What was most useful for you?" (their insight sticks better than yours).

## 12. The wider HBR / management classics

**Core thesis.** The more control you exert over a skilled professional, the more they disengage.
Full engagement needs authenticity, delegated initiative, calibrated expectations, attention to
what *works*, direct presence, and cultures that reward honest self-examination.

**Key ideas + best lines.**
- **Goffee & Jones, "Why Should Anyone Be Led by You?"** authentic leadership; selectively reveal
  a (tangential) weakness; "tough empathy" (give what they need, not what they want). "Be
  yourselves, more, with skill." (McKinsey Award winner.)
- **Oncken & Wass, "Who's Got the Monkey?"** never accept the "next move" off a report's back;
  require a recommendation, not a raw problem. "Why is it that managers are typically running out
  of time while their subordinates are typically running out of work?" https://hbr.org/1999/11/management-time-whos-got-the-monkey
- **Manzoni & Barsoux, "The Set-Up-to-Fail Syndrome":** a boss's low expectations are
  self-fulfilling; tighter oversight → withdrawal → "confirmation." "Bosses cannot hide their
  convictions with words; underlying beliefs surface." https://hbr.org/1998/03/the-set-up-to-fail-syndrome
- **Buckingham & Goodall, "The Feedback Fallacy" / "Nine Lies":** excellence is idiosyncratic;
  name what works rather than diagnose deficiency. "Learning rests on our grasp of what we're
  doing well, not what we're doing poorly." https://hbr.org/2019/03/the-feedback-fallacy
- **Peters & Waterman, "In Search of Excellence":** "productivity through people"; **MBWA**
  (managing by wandering around). "Treat people as adults ... partners ... with dignity ... with
  respect."
- **Argyris, "Teaching Smart People How to Learn":** smart pros are often the worst learners
  (defensive reasoning); need **double-loop** learning (question the assumptions), safe only when
  leaders model it. https://hbr.org/1991/05/teaching-smart-people-how-to-learn

**Killer stats.**
- **~62%** of a performance rating reflects the *rater*, not the ratee (idiosyncratic rater effect). https://hbr.org/2019/03/the-feedback-fallacy
- Positive attention is roughly **30x** more powerful than negative feedback in driving performance (Gallup, via "Nine Lies"). https://www.runn.io/blog/nine-lies-about-work-summary
- Peters & Waterman "excellent" firms: **14.1%** annual returns (1982-2002) vs 11.3% Dow / 10.1% S&P. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_Search_of_Excellence

**Takeaways.** Lead with earned authenticity. Return the monkey (require recommendations). Audit
the label you've put on a "weak" performer. Catch people doing things right. Get out of the
office. Model double-loop honesty about your own failures.

## 14. Brand-name org case studies (CEO-grade)

**Core thesis.** Professionals produce more, stay longer, and innovate harder when treated as the
most valuable thing in the building, not the most expensive risk to manage. The lever is removing
what *extinguishes* intrinsic motivation: surveillance, rigid hierarchy, "we don't trust your
judgment."

**The cases.**
- **SAS Institute (Jim Goodnight):** "the principal asset walks out the door every night." 35-hr
  norm, trust, on-site clinic/childcare. Voluntary turnover **3-4%** vs an industry **17-25%**,
  saving an estimated **~$60-100M/yr**; profitable **44 straight years**. https://www.cbsnews.com/news/working-the-good-life/
- **W.L. Gore:** the **lattice** (no bosses, "associates," "sponsors" not managers; self-made
  commitments); the **~150-person plant cap** (Dunbar). "Waterline" rule. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._L._Gore_%26_Associates
- **Semco (Ricardo Semler):** employees set their own hours and even salaries with full financial
  transparency; turnover fell to ~2%; revenue $4M → $212M. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricardo_Semler
- **Morning Star:** no managers; **Colleague Letters of Understanding (CLOUs)** negotiated with
  the colleagues your work touches; peer-set pay. Gary Hamel, HBR: "First, Let's Fire All the
  Managers." https://www.corporate-rebels.com/blog/morning-star-pioneering-self-management-in-manufacturing
- **Spotify squads/tribes/chapters/guilds:** the cautionary tale — the *nouns* spread worldwide;
  the culture (psychological safety, real autonomy) didn't. Spotify itself moved on. "20%
  structure, 80% culture." https://agilepainrelief.com/blog/the-spotify-model-of-scaling-spotify-doesnt-use-it-neither-should-you/
- **Google 20% time:** produced Gmail, News, Maps features; eroded under deadline pressure (only
  ~10% actually used it; "120% time"). Symbol that exploration is legitimate. https://9to5google.com/2013/08/16/googles-20-percent-time-birthplace-of-gmail-google-maps-adsense-now-effectively-dead/
- **Hawthorne studies:** the canonical "attention raises output" story — *but* Levitt & List's
  reanalysis (NBER 15016) found the dramatic effect "entirely fictional" in the actual data. Use
  with care. https://www.nber.org/papers/w15016

**Takeaways.** Turnover cost is your most legible finance argument. Autonomy must be *real*
decision authority (pros detect theater). Accountability follows voluntary, public commitment.
Scale + anonymous bureaucracy kills it (keep units small and named). Copying structure without
culture yields vocabulary, not results.

## 16. Build spec — learning science (how to teach it so it sticks)

(Applies to the essay's structure and pacing. Full design-system spec to follow from stream 15.)

- **Mayer's multimedia principles:** coherence (cut anything not load-bearing), signaling (cue
  the key idea), segmenting (one chunk at a time), pre-training (define terms first), spatial
  contiguity (labels next to the diagram), personalization (conversational "you/I").
- **Cognitive Load (Sweller):** working memory ≈ 4 items; minimize *extraneous* load (jargon,
  clutter, split attention) to free *germane* load. **Worked-example effect:** show the situation
  + the principle applied + the outcome before asking the reader to apply it.
- **Dual coding (Paivio) + concreteness fading:** start concrete (a named scene), then strip to
  the abstraction. Pair every abstraction with a visual.
- **Make It Stick:** **retrieval practice** (self-test prompts; ~80% recall after a week vs ~36%
  for re-reading), **spacing/interleaving** (revisit each idea later in a new context),
  **generation** (ask "what would you do?" before revealing), **desirable difficulty.**
- **Curse of knowledge:** experts skip steps; "tappers" think 50% will get the tune, ~3% do.
- **Story & analogy:** facts in a narrative ~**20x** more memorable than a list.
- **Explorable explanations (Victor, Nicky Case) + 3Blue1Brown:** never start with a definition;
  motivate before explaining; visuals carry ownership; bring the reader to the edge of the
  conclusion before naming it.

**The 10 highest-leverage moves:** (1) open every section with a concrete scenario, not a concept
name; (2) one idea per section, hard limit; (3) name and number every set of ideas; (4) a visual
or comparison table beside every abstraction; (5) motivate before explaining (open with a
question/puzzle); (6) conversational "you/I" register; (7) close each section with one line:
"The manager's move here is..."; (8) pre-define key terms up front; (9) revisit each core concept
later in a new context; (10) one unified through-line / protagonist the whole essay returns to.

## 13. Multipliers and the mechanics of delegation

**Core thesis.** The central error with professionals isn't neglect, it's the wrong kind of
presence: leaders who need to be the smartest in the room suppress the very intelligence they're
paying for. Fix: shift from broadcasting intelligence to extracting it (Multipliers), and make
the transfer of decision authority **explicit and graduated** (delegation levels + decision rights).

**Key ideas.**
- **Multipliers vs Diminishers (Liz Wiseman):** Multipliers access **70-100%** of a team's
  capability; Diminishers **20-50%** (≈2x gap). Five disciplines: **Talent Magnet, Liberator,
  Challenger, Debate Maker, Investor** (vs Empire Builder, Tyrant, Know-It-All, Decision Maker,
  Micromanager).
- **The Accidental Diminisher:** ~2/3 of diminishing is *unintentional* by well-meaning leaders.
  Archetypes: Rapid Responder, Pace-Setter, Rescuer, Idea Guy, Always-On, Optimist.
- **Appelo's 7 Levels of Delegation:** Tell, Sell, Consult, Agree, Advise, Inquire, Delegate, set
  *per decision area* and made visible on a **delegation board** (calibrated with "delegation
  poker"). Most autonomy breakdowns are invisible mismatched assumptions (manager thinks
  "Advise," team hears "Tell").
- **RAPID (Bain):** name one **D**(ecider) per decision (Recommend/Agree/Perform/Input/Decide).
  Autonomy without role clarity = re-litigation and stall.

**Killer stats.**
- Multipliers ≈ **2x** output from the same people; the average manager uses only **66%** of team capability. https://mindsetonline.com/liz-wiseman-multipliers-150-leaders-twice-output/
- A Fortune 500 Multiplier program: **+28%** team output, no new headcount. https://www.advantageperformance.com/turning-your-managers-into-multipliers/
- Empowered + well-coached orgs are **3.2x** more likely to report decisions that are both high-quality and fast (McKinsey). https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/for-smarter-decisions-empower-your-employees

**Best quotes.**
- "It isn't how much you know that matters. What matters is how much access you have to what other people know." — Wiseman.
- "The people around a Multiplier get smarter and more capable over time. The people around a Diminisher get quieter and more cautious." — Wiseman.
- "To lead on purpose, we must understand how we diminish by accident." — Wiseman.

**Case study.** *Intel, George Schneer:* spoke ~10% of the time in meetings; his engineer Vikram
gave "100%." Under a brilliant but directive boss, the same Vikram self-rated 50%. Same person,
different leadership, half the output.

**Takeaways.** Find your Accidental-Diminisher pattern first. Ask, don't tell. Name each person's
"native genius" and assign work that needs it. Map a visible delegation board. Name one Decider
per decision. Raise the delegation level as capability grows. Make mistakes safe.

*(Accuracy: the 2x and +28% figures are practitioner self-report/assessment, not blinded trials;
the McKinsey 3.2x is survey correlation. Frame as findings/surveys, not lab results.)*

## 15. Build spec — best long-form web design (the design system)

(How the page should look. Pairs with stream 16, the teaching moves.)

**Exemplars to emulate:** Bartosz Ciechanowski (interactivity that *is* the argument), NYT
"Snow Fall" (scroll-triggered media, no clicks), Distill.pub (book-width prose, full-width
figures, define-on-hover), The Pudding (data-first, spare prose), Nicky Case (hook-then-explain,
one sandboxed interactive), Works in Progress / Asterisk + Stripe Press (serious register =
restraint, generous space), Bloomberg (the big-stat anchor), Our World in Data (show, then
explain, then cite), Datawrapper/IiB (figures titled with the *conclusion*).

**Concrete specs (map onto the site's tokens):**
- **Measure:** prose `max-width: ~64ch` (the site's descent uses `--col: 62ch`); never exceed ~66.
- **Body:** ~20px / line-height ~1.6 in Century Supra (the site body is 1.2-1.28rem; the descent
  reads at ~1.14rem `--d-lead`). Keep mobile ≥17px.
- **Type scale, 4 levels max:** cover headline `clamp(2.4rem,7vw,4rem)`; section opener
  ~1.5-2rem; subhead ~1.25rem; body ~1.14-1.25rem. Big stats live *outside* this scale at
  `clamp(56px,8vw,96px)`.
- **Color:** warm cream ink on the dark green; **one accent** (`--accent #D4C4A0`) restricted to
  ~3 roles (stat numerals, section numbers, drop cap/links). No decorative accent washes.
- **Spacing:** 8px base; **64-96px between sections**, 8-16px within. Figures escape the prose
  column (wider wrapper) so they read as evidence, not shrunken images.
- **Figures:** title states the conclusion; annotate on the chart; caption is a standalone
  sentence with source; chart labels ≥13px, tabular figures, muted gridlines on the dark canvas.
- **Big stat:** display weight, accent, followed immediately by the comparison in body size; units
  are part of the number; generous space around it.
- **Motion:** wrap *everything* in `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: no-preference)`; fine-pointer
  gate hover effects; only figure fade-in on enter + the one interactive's state changes;
  200-350ms ease-out; no parallax/scroll-jacking/sticky-on-mobile.
- **One interactive,** placed at the argument's climax, following the paragraph that poses the
  exact question it answers; surrounded by prose that processes the result.
- **Mobile-first:** test at 375px; `clamp()` the stats; full-bleed figures via negative margin;
  44px touch targets.
- **A11y:** body contrast ≥7:1; alt text describes the trend; one `<h1>`, sections `<h2>`; `rem`
  font sizes; reduced-motion honored.

**10 highest-leverage moves:** (1) lock prose to ~64ch; (2) body ~20px/1.65 Century Supra;
(3) big stats `clamp(56px,8vw,96px)` + immediate comparison; (4) accent in only 3 roles;
(5) every figure titled with the conclusion; (6) all animation behind reduced-motion; (7) 64-96px
between sections vs 8-16px within; (8) figures escape the prose column; (9) one interactive at the
climax; (10) test 375px before desktop.

**Sources:** Ciechanowski via CSS-Tricks https://css-tricks.com/bartosz-ciechanowskis-interactive-blog-posts/ ; Distill interactive articles https://distill.pub/2020/communicating-with-interactive-articles/ ; line length/height https://pimpmytype.com/line-length-line-height/ ; Butterick https://practicaltypography.com/summary-of-key-rules.html ; WCAG contrast https://www.allaccessible.org/blog/color-contrast-accessibility-wcag-guide-2025
