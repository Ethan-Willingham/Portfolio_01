Why you have a different age on each planet

A "year" is just one trip around the Sun, and every planet takes a different amount of time to make the trip. Mercury, hugging the Sun on the inside lane, finishes a lap in only 88 days. Neptune, far out on the edge, needs almost 165 of our years for a single orbit.

So your age depends on whose calendar you use. The number of birthdays you have had is really the number of times Earth has carried you around the Sun. Stand on Mercury and you have gone around many more times; stand on Neptune and you may not have finished a single lap. Someone who is 80 on Earth has never once had a birthday on Neptune.

This is also why the outer planets feel almost frozen in human terms. A person born when Pluto was discovered, in 1930, still has not lived a single Plutonian year, which runs about 248 of ours.

How this is calculated

It takes the exact time between your birthday and right now, then divides it by each world's orbital period, the time for one trip around the Sun. For Earth it uses the calendar year you already count by; for the other planets it uses the orbital periods from NASA's planetary fact sheet.

The Space Station is not a planet, so it has no year in the usual sense. Instead it laps the whole Earth roughly every 93 minutes, about 16 times a day, so its "age" is simply how many of those laps have happened since you were born. The number gets large quickly.

  • Mercury88.0 days
  • Venus224.7 days
  • Earth365.25 days
  • Mars687.0 days
  • Jupiter11.86 years
  • Saturn29.45 years
  • Uranus84.01 years
  • Neptune164.8 years
  • Pluto247.9 years
  • ISS (one orbit)92.7 min

Everything runs in your browser. Your birthday is never sent anywhere, and the link you share opens an empty calculator, never your date. Orbital periods are sidereal (measured against the stars); the Space Station's orbit decays and is periodically reboosted, so its period is an approximation.
