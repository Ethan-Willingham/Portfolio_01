    A few more things

    A "year" is one trip around the Sun, and every planet takes a different amount of time to make it. Mercury rounds the Sun in 88 days; Neptune needs almost 165 of our years. So your age depends on whose calendar you keep, and someone who is 80 on Earth has never once had a birthday on Neptune.

    The orbital periods, and the math ↓

    Your age on a world is the exact time since your birthday divided by that world's orbital period, the time for one lap of the Sun. Earth uses the calendar year you already count by; the rest use the sidereal periods from NASA's planetary fact sheet. The Space Station is not a planet, so its "lap" is one orbit of Earth, about 93 minutes.

    • Mercury88.0 days
    • Venus224.7 days
    • Earth365.25 days
    • Mars687.0 days
    • Jupiter11.86 years
    • Saturn29.45 years
    • Uranus84.01 years
    • Neptune164.8 years
    • Pluto247.9 years

    Time itself runs a touch differently up on the Station. Its speed makes its clocks tick about 25 microseconds a day slower than ours, while its altitude (weaker gravity) speeds them up a little; the speed wins. Astronaut Scott Kelly came home from a year in orbit a few milliseconds younger than his identical twin.
