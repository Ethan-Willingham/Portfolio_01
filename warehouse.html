On Warehouses, Factories & the Software That Runs Them

The Brain Behind the Warehouse

Behind every "your order has shipped" is a building you have probably never seen, anywhere from a spare back room to a place the size of a small town, and a piece of software that knows where every item inside it is. This is how that software works, from the receiving dock to the factory line, explained from the ground up, so the next time it comes up you can follow every word.

scroll

You tap "buy," and a few hours later a phone buzzes in a building you will never see. Somewhere in a county you have never driven through, in a space that might be a back room behind a shop or might be longer than a runway, a person you will never meet walks to a shelf, scans a barcode, drops your thing in a tote, and a screen somewhere updates by one. That building might hold a few hundred kinds of item or a few million, and it knows, to the shelf, where every single one of them is sitting right now. That knowing is not a binder and it is not a person's memory. It is a piece of software, and this is a tour of how it thinks.

The software is a Warehouse Management System, a WMS, and an entire industry of companies sells it, installs it, and keeps it running. If you want to understand the modern economy of stuff, the factory that makes it and the warehouse that ships it, the fastest way in is through the system that runs the floor. By the end of this you should be able to stand in a distribution center, follow a box from the truck to the door, and understand every step, well enough to be the person a warehouse calls when their software starts misbehaving.

Five words before we start

1

WMS (Warehouse Management System)

The software that runs the floor: it knows what you have, where it sits, and what each worker should do next. The brain of the building.

2

SKU (stock keeping unit)

One distinct sellable item. A blue medium tee and a blue large tee are two SKUs. A big warehouse carries tens of thousands. Say it "skew."

3

LPN (license plate)

A barcode on a pallet, case, or tote that the system treats as one object. Scan the plate and the system knows everything riding on it, in one beep.

4

ERP vs WMS

The ERP (the company's financial brain) knows you own 500 units and what they are worth. The WMS knows those 500 are in bin A-12-03, on this pallet, in this lot. Books versus the floor.

5

Directed work

The system tells the worker where to go and what to do, instead of the worker deciding. The single feature that separates a real WMS from a spreadsheet.

Act I
The building and its brain

01

The building behind the buy button

First, the scale of the thing, so the rest makes sense.

A modern distribution center is not a stockroom. It is a machine for moving objects, and it runs at a tempo that is hard to picture from outside. A single large e-commerce warehouse can hold a hundred thousand kinds of things and ship hundreds of thousands of items in a day, each one going to a different doorstep. The work of finding, grabbing, boxing, and shipping all of it is the whole game, and it happens against a clock set by a customer who was promised the package tomorrow.

None of that is possible by memory or paper. The instant the building gets bigger than what one foreman can hold in his head, you need a system that holds the map for everyone. That system is the WMS. It is the difference between a warehouse and a pile.

$1.19 trillionWhat Americans spent online in 2024. Almost every one of those orders was picked, packed, and shipped out of a building run by a WMS.US Census Bureau, 2024

The WMS pro's read

You are not selling software that helps a warehouse. For a real operation you are selling the thing the warehouse cannot run without. That is why these deals are big, slow, and sticky, and why "it went down" is a five-alarm call.

02

How the software sees the room

The whole warehouse, rebuilt inside a database. Get this and you get everything that follows.

The WMS keeps a digital twin of the physical building, and it is built from a few simple objects. There is the item master, the record of every SKU: its size, its weight, whether it ships in eaches or cases, whether it needs a lot or a serial number. There is the location master, a record for every addressable spot on the floor, usually coded by aisle, bay, level, and position, so A-12-03 means a specific shelf you can walk to. And tying them together is the inventory record: this item, in that location, this quantity, in this status. That last word, status, is doing enormous work, and we will come back to it.

Here is the catch that sinks more projects than any bug. The system is only as true as that data. Tell it a case weighs 8 pounds when it weighs 80 and it will cheerfully send a worker to stack it on a top shelf. Leave out a box's dimensions and it cannot choose a carton or rate a carrier. The unglamorous work of clean item and location data is the foundation the whole building stands on, and the most common reason a go-live turns into a nightmare.

Every warehouse is a snowflake. The software is the same; the rules each one runs are not.The first thing anyone in this industry learns

The WMS pro's read

When a customer says "the system is wrong," your first suspect is almost never the software. It is the data feeding it. Knowing how to run a quick data-quality audit is half the job.

03

Directed work and the holy scan

The difference between a WMS and a clipboard is who decides, and one beep.

On a paper system, a worker gets a list and figures out their own route, their own order, their own shortcuts. The inventory gets updated later, or never, and errors surface days after they happen, when they are expensive. A WMS inverts this. It hands the worker the next task on a scanner: go to A-12-03, scan the location, scan the item, confirm the count. The worker does not choose. The system chooses, because the system can see the whole board.

And the scan is the point. In a well-run warehouse the barcode scan is the atomic unit of truth: no scan, no change. The worker cannot skip it, and the moment they do it, the inventory updates in real time and a line is written to an audit trail with a timestamp, a device, and a name. This is what people mean when they say a warehouse is "on the system." Reality and the record move together, beep by beep.

99.9%Pick accuracy a scan-confirmed operation can hold, where a worker must scan the item before the system accepts it. Paper and memory top out a full point or two lower, and you find the errors much later.Industry benchmark, directional

The WMS pro's read

If a customer is still working a zone "off the system" because it is faster, that zone is where their accuracy goes to die. The whole value of what you sell is the discipline of the scan. Defend it.

04

The dock

Receiving is where the system meets reality, and where most retail fines are born.

A truck backs in. Before it arrives, a good supplier has already sent an ASN, an advance ship notice, an electronic manifest that says exactly what is on the trailer and on which pallet. The receiver scans the pallet's license plate, the system says "expected: 48 of SKU 00193, on PO 4451," the worker counts, and a match turns the screen green. A mismatch goes to an exception queue. Anything missing, broken, or extra gets logged then and there as OS&D, over, short, and damaged, because once the driver leaves, the claim is much harder to win.

The clock that matters here is dock to stock: how long from the trailer touching the door to the goods being findable and sellable on the system. Stretch it out and you create phantom shortages, stock that is physically in the building but invisible to every order. Sometimes the system never stores it at all: if an outbound order is already waiting for those exact units, the WMS can cross-dock them straight from the inbound door to an outbound truck, and the box never sees a shelf.

< 2 hoursDock-to-stock at a sharp operation, against the day or more it takes a struggling one. The gap is pure availability: hours your inventory does not exist yet.Warehouse benchmark surveys

The WMS pro's read

The ASN is where the big retailers live. Walmart and Target fine suppliers for a late or wrong one. If your customer ships to those giants, a clean, on-time ASN out of your system is not a feature, it is them not losing money.

05

Putaway, and the quiet art of where

Picking a shelf for a box sounds trivial. It is the single highest-leverage decision in the building.

When the box is received, the WMS tells the worker where to put it, and it is not random. The system runs directed putaway against rules: keep frozen with frozen, heavy down low, and put the fast movers where they are easy to reach. The deepest of these rules is slotting, the deliberate placement of each SKU to cut the one cost that dominates a warehouse: walking. The fastest 20 percent of SKUs drive 80 percent of the trips, so they belong in the golden zone, between your knees and your shoulders, close to packing. The slow stuff goes to the top shelves and the back forty.

There is a two-tier trick underneath this. The full pallets live in high reserve racking, and a small, dense forward pick face holds a few days of the busy items at hand height. The WMS quietly runs a replenishment loop, topping up the pick face from reserve before it runs dry, so the picker never has to climb. Get slotting wrong and you pay for it on every single pick, forever.

20–40%How much a serious slotting pass can cut the distance pickers walk. Walking is roughly half of all pick labor, so this is real money, found by moving boxes, not hiring anyone.Slotting case studies, directional

The WMS pro's read

A customer whose pickers walk a little farther every quarter has stale slotting and does not know it. That is a concrete, dollarized win you can bring to a review, and often an upsell to a slotting module.

06

The count that never stops

Everything downstream is built on one number being true: do the books match the bins?

Inventory accuracy is the foundation, and it decays on its own. A box gets moved without a scan, one gets crushed and tossed, a count gets fudged under time pressure, and slowly the record drifts from the shelf. The old fix was to shut the building once a year and count everything, a miserable snapshot that is stale the next morning. The modern fix is cycle counting: count a small, smart slice every day, count the fast movers most often, and chase the root cause of every miss, so accuracy is maintained continuously instead of rescued annually.

Two ideas earn their keep here. The first is the order in which you sell: FIFO ships the oldest stock first, and FEFO, first expired first out, ships whatever dies soonest, which is the law of the land in food and pharma. The second is the word status. A unit on hand is not the same as a unit you can sell. It might be available, or it might be spoken for in one of two very different ways. Allocated means it is reserved for a specific order and about to ship. On hold means it is blocked from shipping because something is wrong with it, like damage or an expired date. That gap is behind a complaint you will hear a hundred times: "the system says I have 500, but I can't ship any." All 500 are real; they are just all allocated, or all held. On hand is never the same as available to promise.

99%+Where a WMS holds inventory accuracy. On paper it is often 80 to 90, which means one pick in ten goes wrong. The leap from "ten" to "ninety-nine" is the system's plainest payoff.Warehouse benchmark surveys

The WMS pro's read

Half the "your software is broken" tickets you ever get will be a status confusion or a process that skipped a scan, not a bug. Learn to read the inventory record out loud, item, location, quantity, status, and you can solve most of them on the call.

07

The pick

The heart of the building, the biggest labor cost, and where automation is rewriting the rules.

Picking is the act of pulling the right things for one order, and it is where most of the warehouse's hours go. The dirty secret is that a picker spends as much as half their shift just walking, which is why the WMS obsesses over how to organize the trip. It can send one worker for one order (discrete), or batch many orders into one loop and sort them after (batch and cluster), or split the floor into zones and pass a tote down the line (zone), or release a timed wave of orders to hit a truck's cutoff (wave). Each is a different answer to the same question: how do we touch the most orders per step?

How the worker is guided matters too. A scanner screen is the baseline; pick-to-light flashes the bin and the count; voice talks the picker through it hands-free, big in freezers and groceries. And then the radical move, goods to person: instead of the human walking to the shelf, a robot brings the shelf to a standing human. That single inversion deletes the walking entirely, which is why Amazon spent 775 million dollars to buy the company that invented it and then kept it for themselves.

10–15 milesHow far a picker can walk in one shift in a big building. Goods-to-person robotics exists to drive that number toward zero, and to turn a half day of walking into a half day of picking.Industry reporting

The WMS pro's read

The pick is where your ROI story is won. When you talk picks-per-hour and travel saved, you are speaking the operations director's native language, and that is the number that renews the contract.

08

Pack, label, and out the door

The last hundred feet, where the warehouse hands off to the carrier and closes the loop.

At the pack station the picked items get verified one more time by scan, then the system does something clever called cartonization: it picks the smallest box that fits, because shipping air is expensive. How expensive? Carriers bill on dimensional weight, charging by the box's size or its actual weight, whichever costs more, so a big light box of pillows is billed as if it were heavy. The WMS weighs and measures, then rate shops across carriers, choosing parcel for a small box, LTL (less than truckload) for a pallet, FTL for a full trailer, and prints the label.

Then it closes the loop, and a quiet stack of paperwork leaves with the order. Four documents do the real work, and they split cleanly in two: two are printed for a person to read, and two are messages fired between machines.

Paper · rides in the box
Packing slip
The plain list of what is in the carton: items, quantities, and the order number, with no prices. It is the page a person checks the shipment against, tucked inside the box or in a pouch stuck to the outside.
Paper · rides with the driver
Bill of lading
Only for freight, the pallets that move by truck. It is at once the receipt for the goods and the contract with the carrier, so the driver signs it at pickup. One page: pallets, weight, class, addresses, and who pays.
Electronic · sent ahead to the buyer
Outbound ASN
The advance ship notice, an EDI 856 fired to the customer before the truck arrives, describing every pallet and carton down to the barcode. The packing slip's machine twin: they scan one label and their system already knows what is inside.
Electronic · sent up to your ERP
Ship confirmation
The message that tells your own business the order is gone, often an EDI 945. Invisible, and the one that matters most: it relieves the inventory, closes the order, and finally lets finance send the invoice.

That last one, the ship confirmation, is the one to watch. Until it fires, the order hangs in processing: the stock is never relieved, the customer is never billed, and the warehouse gets the angry call, because the WMS is the last system in the chain, so it is blamed first.

The WMS pro's read

This is the seam between the warehouse system and the transportation system (the TMS). Knowing exactly where one ends and the other begins, and that the ship confirm is what closes the money loop, makes you the rare WMS pro who can actually triage a stuck shipment.

Act III
The work that never stops
The work that keeps inventory true

Click any station, or step through with the arrows. Replenish, count, hold, returns: four jobs that never finish, running at once, all day. This is the quiet loop behind a shelf you can trust.

The work that keeps inventory true runs in a loop, all day, all at once: replenishment refills the pick shelves from reserve, cycle counting verifies the records a slice at a time, holds pull damaged or expired stock out before it can ship, and returns bring goods back to be inspected and re-entered or retired. Then it begins again.

09

The upkeep that never sleeps

The journey you just followed assumes the shelves are stocked and the counts are honest. Keeping them that way is not a step. It is a loop that runs all day, forever.

Four jobs circle the building without end. Replenishment keeps refilling the small pick face from the big reserve in back, before a picker ever reaches an empty slot. Cycle counting checks a smart slice of the inventory every day, so the record and the shelf are reconciled in days instead of once a year. Those two you have already met. The loop adds two more that quietly decide whether a customer ever gets burned.

The first is the hold. When stock is damaged, expired, recalled, or simply awaiting inspection, the system pulls it out of the available pool and parks it in quarantine, where it physically cannot be picked, so it can never ship by mistake. The second is returns, the whole reverse journey: goods come back from customers, get inspected one at a time, and are restocked, sent back to the vendor, or written off. It is slow and expensive precisely because almost none of it can be done in bulk. And every job in this loop feeds the next: a return that passes inspection becomes replenishment stock, a count that finds a gap can trigger a hold, and around it goes.

2–3× costWhat it takes to process one returned item versus shipping it in the first place, because each one must be inspected by hand instead of handled in bulk. Returns run a fifth or more of everything sold online, and nearly half of all apparel.NRF and industry estimates, directional

The WMS pro's read

Half of "the system is broken, it shows stock I cannot ship" tickets are a hold or an allocation doing its job, not a bug. And a customer drowning in returns is not just complaining, they are asking for a reverse-logistics module. Both are this loop, and reading it on the call is the whole game.

Act IV
The factory next door
How raw materials become a product

How raw materials become a product: components arrive and are checked in, wait in the stockroom until a work order calls for them, are gathered into one kit per build by the bill of materials, and at the assembly line many parts become one finished good. 10

From raw to finished

A factory is a warehouse with a transformation in the middle. The WMS feeds the line and catches what comes off it.

In manufacturing the same building logic runs, but the goods change shape on the way through, and the jargon multiplies fast. Here is the key that unlocks it: nearly everything orbits one idea, the bill of materials, or BOM. It is simply the recipe for one unit, the exact parts it takes to build a single product, anywhere from a dozen for a toy to tens of thousands for a car.

Bill of materials · BOMthe recipe for one unit
  • 1× Steel frame
  • 1× Motor
  • 1× Control board
  • 6× Fasteners
  • 1× Trim kit
= 1 finished product

A work order says to build, say, two hundred of them. After that, almost every other manufacturing term is just a verb you apply to that recipe.

Kitting
Gather one recipe's worth of parts into a single bin and stage it at the line, so the assembler never leaves the station to hunt for a screw.
Line replenishment
Keep the recipe's parts stocked right at the line as it eats them. The classic signal is Kanban: an empty bin means send another.
Backflushing
When a finished unit is reported, deduct the whole recipe from inventory in one stroke, rather than scanning each part out. A wrong BOM quietly miscounts your stock.
Work in process
The recipe half-assembled: units on the line that are no longer loose parts and not yet finished goods. Let WIP pile up and it hides problems.

When the unit is finished, it is received back into the warehouse as a new item, exactly the handoff in the diagram above. The stakes on the line, though, are different. In distribution a slow pick means a late package. On a car line, a part that arrives late or out of sequence stops the line, and a stopped automotive line is measured in many thousands of dollars per minute. That is why high-end plants run just in sequence, parts arriving in the exact order the line will use them.

$10–22 k / minWhat a stopped assembly line can cost at a major automaker. In a plant, the warehouse system is not shipping packages. It is keeping a very expensive machine from going quiet.Widely cited industry figure

The WMS pro's read

A manufacturing customer does not care about "orders shipped." They care about the line never starving. Reframe every conversation around feeding production on time, and you are speaking to their actual fear.

11

The system zoo

Five three-letter systems claim a piece of the floor. The day you can draw the stack is the day you stop sounding like an outsider.

The most confusing thing for a newcomer is that the WMS is not alone. It lives in a stack of systems, each minding its own altitude, and they are forever blamed for each other's failures. At the top is the ERP, the financial brain that decides what to make and buy and what it is worth. Below it the WMS runs the physical floor. On the factory side the MES tracks what is happening on the machines right now. And where there is automation, a WCS drives the conveyors and sorters in millisecond real time, with a newer layer, the WES, orchestrating robots and humans together. This layered map is old enough to have a name, the ISA-95 pyramid.

Who owns what, top to bottom
level 4ERP
The ledger. What to make, what to buy, what it is worth. Knows you own 500; not which shelf.
level 3WMS ★
The floor. Where every unit physically sits, and what each worker does next. Your world.
level 3MES
The line. Which machine is running what, right now, and the quality check at each step.
level 2WES / WCS
The machines. Orchestrates robots, then drives conveyors and sorters in real time.
level 1PLC
The motors and sensors. A valve opens, a belt turns. No idea what an order is.

They talk constantly, mostly through a decades-old language called EDI. The two messages that matter most: a 940 tells the WMS to ship something, and a 945 tells the ERP it shipped. That handshake is the heartbeat of the whole stack.

And this is exactly where projects die. The software vendor does its part, the data flows between systems get underestimated, and the connection between WMS and ERP becomes the thing nobody owns. When the 945 silently fails to send, three different teams point at each other while the customer's orders pile up unbilled.

~45%Of warehouse software projects that name integration as the main cause of going live late and over budget. Not the software itself. The wiring between the software and everything around it.Implementation surveys, directional

The WMS pro's read

Most "WMS bugs" are integration bugs. The WMS pro who can tell whether the WMS sent the message, the middleware dropped it, or the ERP rejected it is worth their salary three times over. Learn the 940 and the 945 cold.

Act V
What the customer measures

12

The tyranny of the multiplied nines

Every warehouse lives and dies by a handful of numbers. The cruelest one is the product of the others.

Ask an operator how good they are and they will quote you a metric: inventory accuracy, on-time shipping, pick accuracy, dock-to-stock time. The one the executives actually watch is the perfect order rate, the share of orders that go out right on every dimension at once. The industry standard breaks it into four: on time, complete (in full), undamaged, and documented correctly, meaning the invoice, the packing slip, and the bill of lading all match. A few operations add a fifth or sixth, but these are the universal core. It sounds soft until you do the arithmetic. Because all four must hold at once, the score is their product, not their average, so four steps that each hit a respectable 97 percent do not give you 97. They give you something that will ruin your afternoon. Try it.

The tyranny of the multiplied nines
Four steps, each nearly perfect on its own. Upgrade them to best-in-class and watch what survives the multiplication.
88.5%perfect order rate
each link, tap to upgrade from typical (97%) to best-in-class (99.5%)
On time delivered by the date you promised
In full the complete order, no missing items or short counts
Undamaged arrives in sellable condition, nothing broken
Billed right the invoice and paperwork match the order

Four steps at 97 percent each. Feels like an A. But 0.97 times itself four times is only 88.5 percent, so more than one order in nine is imperfect somewhere. Tap the links to fix them.

The perfect order rate, the standard order-reliability metric from the SCOR supply-chain model, benchmarked by APQC.

That is the whole reason this software exists. A WMS does not win by being brilliant at one step. It wins by holding every link, the receiving and the slotting and the picking and the billing, up near the nines at the same time, because the customer's headline number is the product, not the average. For scale, the cross-industry median runs about 90 percent and a world-class operation 95 and up, so the 88.5 above is already below average. Lift the weakest link and the whole rate jumps.

The WMS pro's read

Your quarterly review with a customer should open on their perfect order rate, then walk back to which link is dragging it. That single chart turns a "are you happy with us" chat into a "here is the money we made you" one.

13

Every warehouse is a different planet

The path from dock to door is the same everywhere. What makes a deal succeed or fail is the one rule the customer's industry cannot bend.

The fastest way to sound like you belong is to know a customer's world from their industry alone. Each vertical bends the same software around one non-negotiable, and naming that rule before they do is what earns trust in the first meeting.

14

The labor math underneath it all

Strip away the software and the warehouse is a people problem. That is also where the money to buy the software comes from.

Labor is half to two-thirds of what it costs to run a warehouse, and the workforce churns ferociously: the work is hard, repetitive, and physically punishing, and roughly half the floor turns over in a year. So the business case for nearly every WMS and every robot is, at bottom, a labor case. A labor management system sets a fair time standard for each task and measures everyone against it, which raises productivity and, used badly, tips into the surveillance that makes headlines. This is the real tension in the industry: the same data that coaches a struggling worker can also grind one down.

It is also why automation keeps winning. When half your people quit every year and each replacement costs thousands, a robot that never quits starts to pencil out, especially rented by the month instead of bought, which is how a lot of warehouse robotics is sold now. The labor crisis is the engine under the whole industry.

~49%Annual turnover on a typical warehouse floor, with each departure costing thousands to replace. A 500-person building can spend over a million a year just replacing the people who left.US labor data and industry estimates

The WMS pro's read

The economic buyer is usually the CFO, and the CFO buys labor savings, not features. Frame value in fully-loaded hours saved and turnover absorbed, and you are arguing in the only currency that signs the renewal.

Act VI · The Playbook

The customer's success is the job

Everything so far was the floor. Here is the part that is actually your work: keeping the human beings who run that floor glad they chose you, year after year, in a product that is brutally hard to rip out once it is in.

People buy a WMS for a reason you can almost always name: they outgrew the warehouse module bolted onto their ERP, their inventory accuracy fell apart, a peak season melted down, or an audit went badly. Find that original wound on the first call and keep it in front of you, because two years later your renewal argument is "remember why you bought us," and you cannot make it if you never knew.

And you are never selling to one person. You are managing a small cast, each of whom wants something different and fears something different.

Below them sit the floor super-users, the lead pickers and supervisors who become your champions. Win them and adoption takes care of itself. Lose one to turnover and you have lost your eyes on the account.

The hardest stretch is the implementation, and it is famously rough. A WMS go-live can take many months, routinely runs over budget, and culminates in a cutover that is genuinely stressful, the day the old system goes dark and productivity dips before it climbs. The phases are predictable, and two of them are where reputations are made or lost.

Once they are live, your job becomes a number. The most important thing you do in the first ninety days is the least glamorous: write down the baseline. Capture their inventory accuracy, their picks per hour, their order accuracy before go-live, because without it you can never prove what you delivered, and memory always edits the past in the customer's favor. From then on every review is a before-and-after with dollar signs, and every dollar you can show is a wall against the competitor whispering in the next building over.

Churn in this world is rarely about price. It is a failed go-live that never healed, a champion who left, an acquisition that standardizes on someone else, or an ROI nobody ever bothered to measure. And the flip side is the happy truth of this category: a WMS with years of configuration, dozens of integrations, and hundreds of trained users is nearly impossible to tear out. The switching cost is your moat. Expansion, more sites, more modules, labor, slotting, automation, is the easiest motion you have, because the system is already the floor.

Which is the quiet point of this whole tour. The credibility that makes a great warehouse CSM is not a personality you are born with. It is this: knowing how the building thinks, well enough to stand on the floor and be useful. And that is learnable. You just did most of it.

A warehouse is just a promise,
kept at scale.

Somebody tapped "buy," and a building you will never see quietly made good on it, one scan at a time. The WMS is how a pile of stuff becomes a system that always knows where everything is and what to do next. Learn to read it, and the whole hidden machinery behind every package on your porch turns from a black box into a place you could walk into and understand.

The acronym zoo, decoded

This world runs on three-letter codes, and fluency in them is most of sounding like you belong. Here are the ones worth carrying in your head.

WMS Warehouse Management System. The brain of the floor.
ERP Enterprise Resource Planning. The financial system of record.
MES Manufacturing Execution System. Runs the production line.
WCS / WES Warehouse Control / Execution System. Drives and orchestrates the automation.
SKU Stock Keeping Unit. One distinct sellable item.
LPN License Plate Number. A barcode for a pallet, case, or tote.
ASN Advance Ship Notice. "Here is what is on the truck," sent before it arrives.
EDI Electronic Data Interchange. The old language systems trade documents in.
940 / 945 "Go ship this" / "It shipped." The WMS-to-ERP heartbeat.
FIFO / FEFO First In or First Expired, First Out. The order you sell stock in.
OS&D Over, Short & Damaged. Receiving discrepancies, logged at the dock.
ATP Available To Promise. On hand minus what is already spoken for.
BOM Bill of Materials. The recipe for a manufactured thing.
LTL / FTL Less-than / Full Truckload. How palletized freight ships.
3PL Third-Party Logistics. A company that runs warehouses for others.
OTIF On Time In Full. The retail compliance metric with the fines.
LMS Labor Management System. Sets and measures work standards.
DSCSA / FSMA The pharma and food traceability laws that shape those verticals.

Built from a wide read of the warehousing and supply-chain trade literature. Figures are industry benchmarks and are directional, not audited; where a number varies by source, the more conservative reading is used.
