# Marx post, source pack

Engine B (One-Reading Walk) post on **The Communist Manifesto** (Moore 1888), with a
little of **Capital**. Spine: history is class struggle; capitalism is dynamic but splits
society into owners and workers, alienates the worker, and breeds its own gravediggers;
the cure he predicts is the workers seizing the means of production and abolishing private
property. Special: let the critique land AND keep the 20th-century record visible.

All quotes transcribed verbatim by sub-agents from marxists.org (the authoritative
public-domain host). Locations and URLs below so every quote is re-checkable.

## The Communist Manifesto, Samuel Moore 1888 English (Engels-edited). British spelling.

Source: https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1848/communist-manifesto/ (ch01/02/04).

**Opening, Section I:**
> The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles.
> Freeman and slave, patrician and plebeian, lord and serf, guild-master and journeyman,
> in a word, oppressor and oppressed, stood in constant opposition to one another, carried
> on an uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight...

Engels's footnote on "all hitherto existing": "That is, all written history. In 1847, the
pre-history of society... all but unknown. Since then, August von Haxthausen (1792-1866)
discovered common ownership of land in Russia... the primitive form of society everywhere
from India to Ireland..." (i.e. the class-struggle claim is qualified to *written* history.)

**"Most revolutionary part" (the praise):**
> The bourgeoisie, historically, has played a most revolutionary part.
> ...has left remaining no other nexus between man and man than naked self-interest, than
> callous "cash payment."

> It has accomplished wonders far surpassing Egyptian pyramids, Roman aqueducts, and Gothic
> cathedrals...

> The bourgeoisie, during its rule of scarce one hundred years, has created more massive and
> more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations together.

**"All that is solid melts into air":**
> Constant revolutionising of production, uninterrupted disturbance of all social conditions,
> everlasting uncertainty and agitation distinguish the bourgeois epoch from all earlier ones.
> All fixed, fast-frozen relations, with their train of ancient and venerable prejudices and
> opinions, are swept away, all new-formed ones become antiquated before they can ossify. All
> that is solid melts into air, all that is holy is profaned, and man is at last compelled to
> face with sober senses his real conditions of life, and his relations with his kind.

**The two classes:**
> Society as a whole is more and more splitting up into two great hostile camps, into two
> great classes directly facing each other - Bourgeoisie and Proletariat.

Engels's defining footnote: "By bourgeoisie is meant the class of modern capitalists, owners
of the means of social production and employers of wage labour. By proletariat, the class of
modern wage labourers who, having no means of production of their own, are reduced to selling
their labour power in order to live."

**Appendage of the machine:**
> He becomes an appendage of the machine, and it is only the most simple, most monotonous,
> and most easily acquired knack, that is required of him.

**Gravediggers (close of Section I):**
> What the bourgeoisie therefore produces, above all, are its own grave-diggers. Its fall
> and the victory of the proletariat are equally inevitable.

**Abolition of private property (Section II):**
> In this sense, the theory of the Communists may be summed up in the single sentence:
> Abolition of private property.

Ten-point program follows (1. Abolition of property in land... 2. heavy progressive income
tax... 3. abolition of inheritance... 5. centralisation of credit in a national bank...
10. free education for all children in public schools). Several are now mainstream (progressive
tax, free public schooling, central bank), worth noting.

The classless goal: "an association, in which the free development of each is the condition
for the free development of all."

**The close (Section IV):**
> The Communists disdain to conceal their views and aims. They openly declare that their ends
> can be attained only by the forcible overthrow of all existing social conditions. Let the
> ruling classes tremble at a Communistic revolution. The proletarians have nothing to lose
> but their chains. They have a world to win.
> WORKING MEN OF ALL COUNTRIES, UNITE!

**WORDING FLAG (fact-checkers will catch this):** the actual 1888 Moore/Engels text ends
"Working Men of All Countries, Unite!" The famous "Workers of the world, unite! You have
nothing to lose but your chains" is a later *popularisation*, not in any official translation.
German original: "Proletarier aller Länder, vereinigt euch!" The post must use the real line
and note the popular one is a paraphrase.

## Capital, Vol. 1 (Moore/Aveling 1887). British spelling.

Source: https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1867-c1/ (ch01 §4, ch07, ch09).

**Commodity fetishism (Ch 1, §4):**
> A commodity is therefore a mysterious thing, simply because in it the social character of
> men's labour appears to them as an objective character stamped upon the product of that
> labour... There it is a definite social relation between men, that assumes, in their eyes,
> the fantastic form of a relation between things.

> In order, therefore, to find an analogy, we must have recourse to the mist-enveloped regions
> of the religious world... This I call the Fetishism which attaches itself to the products of
> labour, so soon as they are produced as commodities...

**Surplus value (Ch 9 / Ch 7):** the working day splits into *necessary* labour (reproduces
the worker's own wage) and *surplus* labour (unpaid, appropriated by the capitalist).
> He creates surplus-value which, for the capitalist, has all the charms of a creation out of
> nothing.

Worked example (Ch 7): labour-power costs half a day's labour but works a full day; "the value
which its use during one day creates, is double what he pays for that use." Surplus = the
second half of the day, unpaid.

## Estranged labour (Economic & Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844). American spelling.

Written 1844, **unpublished in Marx's lifetime, first published 1932.**
Source: https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1844/manuscripts/labour.htm

> The worker becomes all the poorer the more wealth he produces, the more his production
> increases in power and size. The worker becomes an ever cheaper commodity the more
> commodities he creates.

> ...the object which labor produces - labor's product - confronts it as something alien, as a
> power independent of the producer.

Four estrangements: from the product, from the act of producing, from species-being, from
other men.

## The 20th-century record (truth over narrative). Bounded, sourced, contested.

Honesty rule: never sum famine/policy deaths and executions as one act; flag when a figure
folds in *averted births*; give ranges, not false precision.

- **Black Book of Communism** (Courtois et al., 1997/99): headline "nearly 100 million"; his own
  per-regime numbers sum to ~94.36M (China 65M, USSR 20M, N Korea 2M, Cambodia 2M, etc.).
  **Contested from inside the book:** co-authors Werth and Margolin attacked Courtois in *Le Monde*
  (14 Nov 1997) for "the obsession with reaching one hundred million"; Werth's own USSR figure was
  ~15M. Mainstream critique (Getty, Suny, etc.): don't equate famine deaths with deliberate murder.
  What is NOT disputed: tens of millions died.
- **Great Leap famine, China 1959-61:** defensible range ~15-45M; specialist cluster ~23-36M.
  Yang Jisheng ~36M; Dikötter "at least 45M" (disputed as high); Ashton et al. / Banister ~30M.
  Largest single component and most disputed. Man-made policy famine.
- **Soviet famine / Holodomor 1932-33:** ~5.5-6.5M USSR-wide (Davies & Wheatcroft), of which
  ~3.5-3.9M in Ukraine (Ptoukha Institute 2015). Genocide-intent framing contested (Applebaum,
  Snyder yes; Davies & Wheatcroft, Tauger: man-made but not ethnically targeted). Kazakhstan lost
  the largest *share* (~38% of Kazakhs, Cameron).
- **Great Terror 1936-38:** 681,692 documented executions 1937-38 (archival, Getty/Rittersporn/
  Zemskov); up to ~1.2M terror deaths all-in. NKVD Order 00447 set shooting *quotas*.
- **Gulag:** ~1.05M recorded camp deaths 1934-53 (Getty/Rittersporn/Zemskov, AHR 1993); ~1.6M
  camps+colonies consensus; ~18M passed through (Applebaum). Higher (Solzhenitsyn 12-15M+) is
  literary/extrapolation.
- **Cambodia 1975-79:** median ~1.9M excess deaths (Heuveline 2015), ~a fifth to a quarter of the
  population; ~half executions, half famine/disease/overwork.

**Honest framing:** Marx died 1883, **34 years before** the 1917 Russian Revolution; never saw a
"Marxist" state, never commented on Lenin or the one-party state. He wrote three volumes
diagnosing capitalism and **almost nothing prescriptive** about running socialism ("no recipes for
the cook-shops of the future," Capital afterword 1873). "Dictatorship of the proletariat" appears
mainly in the Critique of the Gotha Programme (1875); his actual model of proletarian rule was the
elected, recallable Paris Commune (Civil War in France, 1871). Lenin hardened it into "a special
coercive force" in State and Revolution (1917).

**Continuity vs betrayal (don't resolve, surface it):**
- Continuity case: "Abolition of private property," "centralise all instruments of production in
  the hands of the State," ends attainable "only by the forcible overthrow," deterministic history
  that can license vanguard certainty. Kolakowski: Stalinism "a legitimate offshoot," not a mere
  distortion.
- Betrayal case: Marx expected revolution in *advanced* democracies (England, Germany), not
  agrarian Russia/peasant China; left no theory of permanent one-party rule, terror, or secret
  police; orthodox Marxists at the time objected (Plekhanov, Kautsky's *Dictatorship of the
  Proletariat* 1918). The Manifesto's stated end is "the free development of each."

**Analytic scorecard (grade which Marx, the 1848 firebrand or the 1867 economist):**
- WRONG: immiseration (real wages rose hugely; world GDP/capita ~10x 1820-2010, Maddison/OWID);
  revolution came in the wrong (backward) places; labour theory of value rejected by mainstream
  economics after the 1870s marginalist revolution; no general collapse; the middle class did not
  vanish into two camps.
- RIGHT / prescient: globalisation and the world market (the "All that is solid" passage reads as
  eerily current; *The Economist* 2018: "Rulers of the world: read Karl Marx!"); recurrent crises
  (2008 revived him); concentration of capital and monopoly; commodification of more of life; the
  "ideology" lens (a durable tool; SEP ranks him a "master of suspicion"); inequality and capital
  concentration back on the agenda (Piketty's r>g, though Piketty rejects Marx's apparatus and
  prescribes taxes, not abolition).
- Verdict: weak as a price theorist and forecaster, major and enduring as a diagnostician of what
  capitalism *does*. Don't let either column borrow credibility from the other.
