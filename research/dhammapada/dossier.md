# Dhammapada research dossier

For the "great books, made learn-from-able" post (Approach A, the Translation
Stack, the Tao Te Ching engine). Built from a deep-research fan-out (21 sources,
98 claims, 22 adversarially verified 3-0; the auto-synthesis step was cut off by

## 1. What the book is, in one paragraph

The Dhammapada ("words/path of dhamma") is a 423-verse anthology in the Khuddaka
Nikaya ("Minor Collection") of the Pali Canon's Sutta Pitaka. It is the most read
and most translated Buddhist text, the verses most Theravada Buddhists can recite
from memory. The verses are not original compositions so much as a curated
anthology: most appear elsewhere in the Canon, gathered here by theme into 26
chapters (vaggas). Traditionally the verses are the Buddha's own utterances;
modern scholars treat it as a compilation reaching something near its present form
by the 3rd century BCE (the council under Asoka) and fixed in Pali in Sri Lanka,
written down ~1st century BCE. Each verse comes wrapped in a back-story in
Buddhaghosa's 5th-century CE commentary, the *Dhammapada-atthakatha*, which is
where almost all the famous "the Buddha said this when..." framings come from.

## 2. The spine (diagnosis and prescription)

The whole series organizes each book on what is wrong with us and the way out.
For the Dhammapada that spine is unusually explicit:

- **Diagnosis.** Suffering (dukkha) is produced by the untrained mind and by the
  "three poisons": craving/greed (lobha/tanha), hatred/aversion (dosa), and
  delusion (moha). The mind is the source: v1-2 put it first. Heedlessness
  (pamada) and craving (tanha, the whole of ch. 24) keep the wheel turning.
- **Prescription.** Train the mind (ch. 3 Citta, the meditation verses), live
  ethically (the ch. 1/ch. 17/ch. 21 restraint-and-non-hatred verses), and cool
  the craving until it goes out (nibbana, literally "extinguishing"). The
  three-part program is stated by the tradition itself at v183.

The book's own self-summary verses (the "one foot of the Torah" move CANON.md
asks for):
- **v183** "Not to do evil, to cultivate good, to purify the mind: this is the
  teaching of the Buddhas." The tradition's explicit self-summary (the
  *Ovada-patimokkha*, the verse recited as the heart of the teaching). KEYSTONE.
- **v1-2** "Mind precedes all things." The psychology in two lines. KEYSTONE.
- **v273-279** name the Four Noble Truths and the three marks directly; v277-279
  is the three-marks capsule. KEYSTONE.

## 3. Keystone verses (verified Pali + the split)

Pali verified against GRETIL (PTS / von Hinuber & Norman 1995 critical edition)
and SuttaCentral root-pli-ms; English wordings against Access to Insight,
Wikisource (Muller), and the in-copyright editions where noted.

| v | Pali hook | Why it is load-bearing | The translator split to feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1-2** | *manopubbangama dhamma manosettha manomaya* | the whole psychology; the book's opening | *mano* = mind/heart/intention; *dhamma* = mental states/experiences/phenomena/"all that we are". Muller is the outlier ("All that we are is the result of what we have thought"); Thanissaro alone says "heart"; Sujato says "intention." |
| **5** | *na hi verena verani sammant'idha kudacana, averena ca sammanti* | the ethical keystone; non-retaliation | The cooling agent is literally *avera*, "non-hatred / absence of hate." Muller (1881) famously rendered it **"hatred ceases by love"**, and that interpretive "by love" is what went viral (and is now on the "fake Buddha quotes" lists). Buddharakkhita/Thanissaro keep the literal "by non-hatred / non-hostility." A clean split to expose: the most-quoted line in the book is a slight mistranslation. |
| **21** | *appamado amatapadam, pamado maccuno padam* | opens the Heedfulness chapter; the practitioner's keynote | *appamada* = heedfulness/diligence/vigilance/earnestness (Muller: "earnestness"); *amata* = the deathless/deathlessness/the undying, glossed by Muller as "immortality (Nirvana)". Said to be the verse that converted Asoka per the commentary. |
| **103** | *...ekanca jeyya-m-attanam, sa ve sangamajuttamo* | conquer-yourself; pairs ordinary vs real victory | "He who conquers himself is the greater victor." Clean across translators; the split is small (greatest/noblest/supreme conqueror), good as a "they finally agree" beat. |
| **153-154** | *anekajati-samsaram sandhavissam anibbisam, gahakaraka ditthosi...* | the house-builder; tradition says the Buddha's first words at awakening | Per the commentary (the *Udana* vatthu) these are the Buddha's *udana* (inspired utterance) at the moment of enlightenment under the Bodhi tree. House-builder = craving (tanha); the house = the body / repeated existence; rafters = defilements; ridgepole = ignorance. Note: many translators (Muller, Buddharakkhita) print them as one unit. ADVERSARIAL: the "first words at awakening" framing is commentarial, not in the verse itself; flag it as tradition, not text. |
| **183** | *sabbapapassa akaranam, kusalassa upasampada, sacittapariyodapanam, etam buddhana sasanam* | the tradition's own three-line summary | "Not to do evil, to cultivate the good, to purify one's own mind: this is the teaching of the Buddhas." Splits on *sasana* (teaching/instruction/dispensation) and *buddhana* (the Buddhas, plural). |
| **277-279** | *sabbe sankhara anicca / sabbe sankhara dukkha / sabbe dhamma anatta* | the three marks of existence, three verses in a row | THE asymmetry worth teaching: anicca (impermanent) and dukkha (suffering) are predicated of *sankhara* (conditioned/constructed things), but anatta (not-self) is predicated of *sabbe dhamma* (ALL things, including the unconditioned, nibbana). The wider net on anatta is deliberate and is the single most philosophically loaded line in the book. |

Other strong candidates (secondary breakdowns if room): **v50** ("not others'
faults but your own"), **v100-103** (the Thousands: one true word/one self-conquest
beats a thousand), **v160/165** ("you are your own refuge / by oneself is evil
done"), **v223** ("conquer anger by love, evil by good"), **v338** (craving like a
tree with the root uncut), **v348** (Tanha, "let go of front, back, middle"),
**v393-394** (a brahmin is not made by birth or matted hair). The Brahmana chapter
(383-423) redefines "brahmin" as the awakened person, the book's closing move.

## 4. The untranslatable terms (the Dhammapada's "dao" and "wu wei")

These are the words where the stack earns its keep. Pali, then the spread.

- **dukkha**, "suffering" (most), "unsatisfactoriness" (analytic Theravada),
  **"stress"** (Thanissaro's signature choice), "pain" (Muller), "sorrow"
  (Mascaro/Easwaran). Literally "hard to bear"; the cart-with-bad-axle image.
  Featured at v1 ("suffering follows"), v203 ("the greatest dukkha is the body /
  the khandhas"), v277-278.
- **anatta / anatta**, "not-self" vs "no-self." The split is not cosmetic:
  Thanissaro insists *anatta* is "not-self" (a value judgment: don't take this as
  self), NOT the metaphysical claim "there is no self," because the Buddha refused
  to answer the bald question (the Vacchagotta exchange, SN 44.10). "No-self" reads
  it as ontology; "not-self" reads it as a strategy. Feature at v279.
- **dhamma**, at v1 "mental states/phenomena/experiences/things"; as the book's
  title "the teaching/the law/the truth/the way"; at v279 "all things." One word
  doing at least three jobs across the book. (Capital-D Dhamma = the teaching;
  lowercase = a phenomenon/mental object.)
- **sankhara**, "conditioned things," "formations," "fabrications" (Thanissaro),
  "constructed/compounded things," "all created things" (Muller). The hardest word
  in the Canon; at v277-278 it means everything assembled from conditions.
- **nibbana**, "Nirvana" (Muller, as a loanword), "the Unconditioned"
  (Buddharakkhita at v154), "Unbinding" (Thanissaro's literal "un-binding /
  blowing out"), "extinction," "the deathless" (amata, v21). Literally the going-out
  of a flame.
- **appamada**, "heedfulness" (Buddharakkhita), "earnestness" (Muller),
  "vigilance," "diligence," "awareness" (Fronsdal). The whole of ch. 2. v21.
- **pamada / papanca**, pamada = heedlessness/negligence (v21's opposite term).
  papanca = "mental proliferation / conceptual elaboration" (the runaway labeling
  mind); less prominent in Dhp but worth a line if anatta/papanca comes up.

## 7. Theravada framing and the divergences to name

- **This is the Pali (Theravada) Dhammapada.** Anchor here, but name the contest.
- **Other recensions of the "same" book** (verified): the **Patna Dharmapada**
  (Buddhist Hybrid Sanskrit, 22 ch / 414 vv), the **Gandhari Dharmapada** (Kharosthi,
  26 ch / 344 extant vv, Brough estimating ~540 originally; one of the oldest Indic
  manuscripts known), and the **Udanavarga** (Sanskrit, Sarvastivada, 33 ch / ~1050
  vv, Bernhard ed.). Same genre, overlapping verses, different selection and order:
  concrete proof that "the Dhammapada" is a family of anthologies, not one fixed
  scripture. Great for the "watching a text get edited" beat the Tao post used.
- **Mahayana divergence.** Mahayana keeps the early verses but would not put THIS
  anthology at the center; its center of gravity is the Prajnaparamita/sutra
  literature (emptiness, bodhisattva vow, universal buddhahood), where the Dhp's
  arahant ideal (personal liberation, ch. 7) is reframed. The Dhp's "by oneself is
  one purified" (v165) sits differently next to the bodhisattva's vow to free all
  beings. Note it; don't relitigate it.

## 8. Adversarial fact-check (what to keep honest in the piece)

1. **"Hatred ceases by love" (v5)** is Müller's interpretive gloss; the Pali is
   *avera*, "non-hatred." Keep the correction visible: it is the book's most famous
   line and it is slightly mistranslated in the version everyone quotes. (Verified;
   it is on fakebuddhaquotes.com for exactly this reason.)
2. **v153-154 as "the Buddha's first words at awakening"** is the commentary's
   framing (the Udana vatthu in Buddhaghosa), not a claim the verses make about
   themselves. Present as tradition. (Verified the commentarial attribution; flag
   the certainty.)
3. **anatta is "not-self," not necessarily "no-self."** The Buddha set aside the
   bald "is there a self?" question (Vacchagotta, SN 44.10). Don't let the post
   assert the strong metaphysical "there is no self," which is a translator's
   reading, not the text's claim. (Verified via Thanissaro; note the counter-view
   exists, e.g. critiques that Thanissaro's "strategic" reading underplays the
   ontology.)
4. **Dating/authorship.** "The Buddha's own words" is the traditional claim. Safer:
   an anthology compiled over time, most of whose verses recur elsewhere in the
   Canon, reaching near-final form by ~3rd c. BCE, written down in Sri Lanka ~1st c.
   BCE. Don't assert single authorship or a precise date.
5. **The three-marks asymmetry (v277-279)** is real and load-bearing: anicca/dukkha
   are of *sankhara*; anatta is of *all dhamma*. Getting this right is a credibility
   signal to the Buddhist readers in the audience.
