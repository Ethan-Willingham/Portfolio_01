# Plato post, source dossier

Engine B (the one-reading walk). Spine: most people live chained in a cave,
mistaking shadows for reality; the way out is philosophy, the examined life,
turning toward what is truly real, even when the people still in the cave kill
you for coming back to free them.

Keystones: "the unexamined life is not worth living" (Apology); the trial and
death of Socrates (Apology, Crito, Phaedo, walked as the spine); the allegory of
the cave (Republic 7); the theory of the Forms; the Socratic method.

Special tension: Socrates wrote nothing, so Plato is our only real access, and
the historical man may be half-invented. That tension belongs in the post.

Featured spine translation: **G.M.A. Grube** (Hackett "Five Dialogues" for the
trial dialogues; Grube, revised by C.D.C. Reeve, for the Republic). One house
voice across both the trial and the cave. Stacks compare Jowett (PD), Tredennick
(Penguin "Last Days of Socrates"), Fowler/Shorey (PD, Loeb/Perseus), and Bloom
(Republic), naming every translator. All quotes verbatim, every quote located by
Stephanus number.

## CONFIRMED VERBATIM, public domain (Jowett unless noted)

### Apology (Jowett, MIT Internet Classics Archive)
- Oracle (20e-21a): "he asked the oracle to tell him whether there was anyone
  wiser than I was, and the Pythian prophetess answered that there was no man
  wiser." Reaction: "What can the god mean? and what is the interpretation of
  this riddle? for I know that I have no wisdom, small or great."
- Human wisdom / "I know that I know nothing" (21d): "I am better off than he is
  - for he knows nothing, and thinks that he knows. I neither know nor think
  that I know."
- Gadfly (30e-31a): "I am that gadfly which God has given the state and all day
  long and in all places am always fastening upon you, arousing and persuading
  and reproaching you."
- Unexamined life (38a), with preceding clause: "the greatest good of man is
  daily to converse about virtue, and all that concerning which you hear me
  examining myself and others, and that the life which is unexamined is not
  worth living"
- Counter-penalty / Prytaneum (36d-37a): "maintenance in the Prytaneum is the
  just return"
- No evil to a good man (41d): "no evil can happen to a good man, either in life
  or after death"
- Closing (42a): "I to die, and you to live. Which is better God only knows."

### Crito (Jowett, MIT)
- Never repay wrong with wrong (49c-d): "Then we ought not to retaliate or render
  evil for evil to anyone, whatever evil we may have suffered from him."
- The Laws on the agreement (51e-52a): "He who has experience of the manner in
  which we order justice and administer the State, and still remains, has entered
  into an implied contract that he will do as we command him."
- The Laws humming in his ears (54d): "This is the voice which I seem to hear
  murmuring in my ears, like the sound of the flute in the ears of the mystic;
  that voice, I say, is humming in my ears, and prevents me from hearing any
  other."

### Phaedo, the death scene (Jowett, MIT + BU Wildman page, 112e-118a)
- Sent the women away / be calm (117d-e): "I sent away the women mainly in order
  that they might not offend in this way, for I have heard that a man should die
  in peace. Be quiet, then, and have patience."
- Drinking calmly (117b-c): "took the cup and said: What do you say about making
  a libation out of this cup to any god?" / "Then holding the cup to his lips,
  quite readily and cheerfully he drank off the poison."
- Cold creeping up (117e-118a): "he walked about until, as he said, his legs
  began to fail, and then he lay on his back" / "after a while he pressed his
  foot hard and asked him if he could feel; and he said, no" / "showed us that he
  was cold and stiff" / "When the poison reaches the heart, that will be the
  end."
- LAST WORDS (118a): "Crito, I owe a cock to Asclepius; will you remember to pay
  the debt?"
- Eulogy (118a): "Such was the end, Echecrates, of our friend; concerning whom I
  may truly say, that of all the men of his time whom I have known, he was the
  wisest and justest and best."
- Philosophy as practice for death (64a, theme): the true philosopher is "ever
  pursuing death and dying" (verify exact Jowett clause if used).

### Allegory of the Cave, Republic VII (Jowett, MIT + maricopa Jowett page)
- Opening image (514a-515a), VERBATIM in full: "Human beings living in a
  underground den, which has a mouth open towards the light and reaching all
  along the den; here they have been from their childhood, and have their legs
  and necks chained so that they cannot move, and can only see before them, being
  prevented by the chains from turning round their heads. Above and behind them a
  fire is blazing at a distance, and between the fire and the prisoners there is
  a raised way; and you will see, if you look, a low wall built along the way,
  like the screen which marionette players have in front of them, over which they
  show the puppets."
- Release / painful ascent (515c-516a): "he will suffer sharp pains; the glare
  will distress him" / "reluctantly dragged up a steep and rugged ascent"
- They would kill the returner (517a): "Men would say of him that up he went and
  down he came without his eyes; and that it was better not even to think of
  ascending; and if any one tried to loose another and lead him up to the light,
  let them only catch the offender, and they would put him to death."
- Socrates' key (517b): "the prison-house is the world of sight, the light of the
  fire is the sun" / "in the world of knowledge the idea of good appears last of
  all, and is seen only with an effort"

## CONFIRMED FACTS (scholarly frame)

### The trial of 399 BCE (Wikipedia "Trial of Socrates"; cross-check SEP/IEP/Nails)
- Date: 399 BCE.
- Charges: asebeia (impiety): "failing to acknowledge the gods that the city
  acknowledges" and "introducing new deities"; plus "corrupting the youth."
- Accusers: Meletus (lead), Anytus (the political muscle), Lycon.
- Court: a dikastery (jury) of 501 citizens.
- Vote: convicted 280 to 221 (a ~30-vote margin).
- Counter-penalty: Socrates mock-proposed free meals at the Prytaneum (a civic
  honor), which alienated the jury; the death sentence then passed by a WIDER
  margin than the conviction.
- Execution: hemlock (Greek koneion, Conium maculatum).
- The ~month delay (lets Crito/Phaedo happen weeks later): the state ship was
  away on the annual sacred mission to Delos, during which no executions were
  permitted. VERIFY the Delos detail against SEP/Nails before asserting.

### TODO verify with SEP/IEP (do not assert until checked)
- The Socratic problem: Socrates wrote nothing; sources = Plato, Xenophon,
  Aristophanes (Clouds, 423 BCE), Aristotle (later). Vlastos developmentalist
  view: early/"Socratic" dialogues (Apology, Crito, Euthyphro) ~ the historical
  man and his method; middle dialogues (Phaedo, Republic, Symposium) = Plato's
  own doctrine, esp. the Forms, in Socrates' mouth. Minority skeptical view: the
  historical Socrates is unrecoverable.
- Theory of Forms is PLATO's, generally NOT attributed to the historical
  Socrates (SEP "Plato's Middle Period Metaphysics").
- "I know that I know nothing" is a later compression; the Apology says only that
  he does not think he knows what he does not know.
- The calm Phaedo death is Plato's literary/philosophical construction; the
  historical details are uncertain.
- Socratic method (elenchus): definition -> draw out consequences -> contradiction
  -> aporia. Socratic irony. The midwife image (Theaetetus 150).

## MODERN TRANSLATIONS (CONFIRMED VERBATIM)

### Grube (Hackett "Five Dialogues", rev. Cooper) -- FEATURED SPINE
- Human wisdom (21d): "I am wiser than this man; it is likely that neither of us
  knows anything worthwhile, but he thinks he knows something when he does not,
  whereas when I do not know, neither do I think I know; so I am likely to be
  wiser than he to this small extent, that I do not think I know what I do not
  know."
- Gadfly (30e): "I was attached to this city by the god ... as upon a great and
  noble horse which was somewhat sluggish because of its size and needed to be
  stirred up by a kind of gadfly."
- Unexamined life (38a): "...the unexamined life is not worth living for men..."
  (full: "if I say that it is the greatest good for a man to discuss virtue every
  day and those other things about which you hear me conversing and testing
  myself and others, for the unexamined life is not worth living for men, you
  will believe me even less.")
- Crito (49c-d): "One should never do wrong in return, nor mistreat any man, no
  matter how one has been mistreated by him."
- Last words (Phaedo 118a): "Crito, we owe a cock to Asclepius; make this
  offering to him and do not forget."
- Eulogy (Phaedo 118a): "Such was the end of our comrade, Echecrates, a man who,
  we would say, was of all those we have known the best, and also the wisest and
  the most upright."

### Grube/Reeve (Hackett Republic) -- FEATURED for Republic lines / cave stack
- 515c: prisoners "would in every way believe that the truth is nothing other
  than the shadows of those artifacts."
- 517a (kill him): "And, as for anyone who tried to free them and lead them
  upward, if they could somehow get their hands on him, wouldn't they kill him?"
- Fragments: "like the screen in front of puppeteers above which they show their
  puppets"; prisoners "fixed in the same place, with their necks and legs
  fettered, able to see only in front of them, because their bonds prevent them
  from turning their heads around."

### Tredennick (Penguin "Last Days of Socrates") -- stack
- Unexamined life (38a): "...life without this sort of examination is not worth
  living."
- Last words (118a): "Crito, we ought to offer a cock to Asclepius. See to it,
  and don't forget."

### Bloom ("The Republic of Plato", Basic Books) -- cave stack
- Cave opening (514a): human beings "in an underground cave-like dwelling with
  its entrance, a long one, open to the light across the whole width of the
  cave"; light "from a fire burning far above and behind them."
