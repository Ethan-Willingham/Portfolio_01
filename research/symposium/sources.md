# Plato's Symposium, source dossier

Built for `symposium.html` (Engine B, the One-Reading Walk). Featured translation:
**Benjamin Jowett** (1892, public domain), the verifiable backbone, quoted in every
passage box. Comparison stacks add **Nehamas & Woodruff** (Hackett, 1989) and, where
sourceable, **Christopher Gill** (Penguin, 1999). Every quote below was retrieved
verbatim from a real fetched page; nothing is reconstructed from memory.

## Featured: Jowett (PD)
All from Project Gutenberg EBook #1600: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/1600/1600-h/1600-h.htm

- **177d-e (setup):** "How can I oppose your motion, who profess to understand nothing but matters of love..."
- **189d-190a (the three sexes / round humans):** "The sexes were not two as they are now, but originally three... the primeval man was round, his back and sides forming a circle; and he had four hands and four feet, one head with two faces, looking opposite ways..."
- **190c-d (Zeus cuts them):** "He spoke and cut men in two, like a sorb-apple which is halved for pickling, or as you might divide an egg with a hair..."
- **191a-b (longing halves):** "...each desiring his other half, came together, and throwing their arms about one another, entwined in mutual embraces, longing to grow into one..."
- **191d (flat fish / indenture):** "Each of us when separated, having one side only, like a flat fish, is but the indenture of a man, and he is always looking for his other half."
- **192e-193a (wholeness, THE line):** "...human nature was originally one and we were a whole, and the desire and pursuit of the whole is called love."
- **203b-c (birth of Love):** Poros/Plenty, son of Metis, drunk with nectar; Penia/Poverty begging at the door, "plotted to have a child by him... and conceived Love."
- **203c-d (Love's nature):** "...always poor, and anything but tender and fair... rough and squalid, and has no shoes, nor a house to dwell in... but he is bold, enterprising, strong, a mighty hunter, always weaving some intrigue... a philosopher at all times..."
- **203e:** "He is by nature neither mortal nor immortal..."
- **210a-211c (the ladder):** "...using these as steps only, and from one going on to two, and from two to all fair forms, and from fair forms to fair practices, and from fair practices to fair notions, until from fair notions he arrives at the notion of absolute beauty..."
- **211a-b (Beauty itself):** "...a nature which in the first place is everlasting, not growing and decaying, or waxing and waning... but beauty absolute, separate, simple, and everlasting..."
- **211d-212a (payoff):** "...beholding beauty with the eye of the mind, he will be enabled to bring forth, not images of beauty, but realities... and bringing forth and nourishing true virtue to become the friend of God and be immortal, if mortal man may."
- **215a-b (Silenus / Marsyas):** "he is exactly like the busts of Silenus... made to open in the middle, and have images of gods inside them. I say also that he is like Marsyas the satyr."
- **216d-e (divine images within):** "...when he is opened, what temperance there is residing within!... I saw in him divine and golden images..."
- **219b-d (the refusal):** "...so contemptuous and derisive and disdainful of my beauty... I arose as from the couch of a father or an elder brother."

- **191d:** "Each of us, then, is a 'matching half' of a human whole, because each was sliced like a flatfish, two out of one, and each of us is always seeking the half that matches him."

- **203c-d:** "...he is tough and shriveled and shoeless and homeless, always lying on the dirt without a bed... a schemer after the beautiful and the good; he is brave, impetuous, and intense, an awesome hunter... a lover of wisdom through all his life..." / "He is by nature neither immortal nor mortal."
- **210e-211b:** "First, it always is and neither comes to be nor passes away, neither waxes nor wanes... but itself by itself with itself, it is always one in form..."

## Fact-check verdicts (SEP, IEP, Perseus, reference works)
- Dramatic date **416 BC**, Agathon's first tragic victory at the **Lenaia** (not the Dionysia).
- Frame: **Apollodorus** tells it to an unnamed companion, **c. 400 BC, ~15 years later**; he was not there, heard it from **Aristodemus** (eyewitness), checked points with Socrates. (172a-173b)
- Seven speakers in order: Phaedrus (178a), Pausanias (180c), Eryximachus (186a), Aristophanes (189c), Agathon (195a), Socrates/Diotima (201d), Alcibiades (212c/214e).
- The **hiccups** swap (185c-e): Aristophanes' turn, but hiccups, so the doctor Eryximachus speaks first.
- Socrates refutes Agathon (199c-201c): Love desires beauty, you desire what you lack, so Love lacks beauty, so Love is not beautiful (and not a god).
- Diotima, birth of Eros 203b-e: Poros (son of Metis) + Penia. daimon, between mortal/immortal, between wisdom/ignorance, hence a philosopher.
- Ladder 210a-211d; "great sea of beauty" = πέλαγος τοῦ καλοῦ (210d).
- Alcibiades 215a-222b; Silenus + Marsyas; father/elder-brother line 219c-d.
- **"Platonic love"**: term coined by **Marsilio Ficino** (15th c., *amor platonicus*), NOT Plato. Plato's eros is sublimated UP the ladder, not chaste friendship; the modern "chaste" sense is partly a later (17th-18th c. English) drift.
- **Diotima**: most scholars treat her as a **literary invention** by Plato (no independent source; Ficino first flagged her as fictional). Defenders of historicity: Wilamowitz, Kranz, Waithe.
- Pederastic frame: erastes (older lover) / eromenos (younger beloved); Socrates inverts the roles (222b).
- Ending 223c-d: same author should write comedy and tragedy; then Socrates leaves and spends the day as usual.
- Greek: τοῦ ὅλου οὖν τῇ ἐπιθυμίᾳ καὶ διώξει ἔρως ὄνομα (192e-193a); δαίμων μέγας (202d); Πόρος / Πενία / Μῆτις (203b).
