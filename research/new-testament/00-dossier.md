# New Testament post: research dossier

Research backing `new-testament.html` (CANON series, Approach A, the Translation
Stack). The post centers on the New Testament and how Christianity re-reads the
Hebrew scriptures; it builds on the Hebrew Bible post and does not re-treat the
shared Genesis/Exodus material. Because this is the most-scrutinized text in the
series, every quoted translation is verbatim and attributed, and the contested
doctrines are steelmanned, not smoothed.

## The spine (diagnosis -> prescription)

- **Diagnosis:** humanity is estranged from God by sin and cannot save itself.
- **Repair:** reconciliation by grace, through the life, death, and resurrection
  of Jesus, received by faith (trust), not earned.
- **Practice:** love God, and love your neighbor as yourself (the self-summary).

The explorer's four sections trace it: Jesus' teaching (Synoptics) -> who Jesus
is (John) -> the load-bearing event (Passion + Resurrection) -> Paul and the
letters (how the early church interpreted it).

## The Greek/Hebrew word forks the breakdowns lean on

- **Logos** (John 1:1): "Word" / "Reason"; Stoic + Hebrew-wisdom background. Hart keeps "the Logos."
- **theos en ho logos** (John 1:1c): anarthrous predicate before the verb. Consensus reads "the Word was God" (Colwell's rule: a definite predicate noun preceding the verb regularly drops the article). The Jehovah's Witnesses' New World Translation reads "a god"; near-universally rejected by Greek scholars. Hart's lowercase "the Logos was god" preserves a real shading without endorsing "a god."
- **agape** vs KJV "charity" (1 Cor 13): KJV via Latin *caritas*; moderns revert to "love" because English "charity" narrowed to almsgiving.
- **makarioi** (Beatitudes): "blessed/happy/fortunate/enviable." Hart "How blissful."
- **opheilemata** "debts" (Lord's Prayer): Matthew's Greek says debts; Luke uses "sins"; "trespasses" entered English via Tyndale's wording of the following verses and the Prayer Book.
- **epiousios** "daily bread": a near-unique word; DRB's Latin-driven "supersubstantial."
- **monogenes** (John 3:16): "only begotten" (KJV) vs "one and only/only" (modern).
- **aionios** (John 3:16): "eternal/everlasting" vs Hart's literal "of the Age."
- **almah** (Heb, Isaiah 7:14) = "young woman" (Heb "virgin" is *betulah*); the Septuagint rendered it **parthenos** = "virgin," which Matthew 1:23 quotes. The virgin-birth-as-Isaiah-fulfillment rides on that Septuagint choice.
- **hilasterion** (Rom 3:25): "propitiation" (appease wrath; KJV/ESV) vs "expiation" (wipe sin; RSV) vs "mercy seat" (the Ark's lid).
- **dikaioo** "justify" (Rom 3, James 2): declared righteous (forensic) vs made righteous; the Reformation hinge.
- **pistis** "faith/trust/faithfulness"; **metanoia** "change of mind" rendered "repentance."

## Textual variants surfaced

- **Lord's Prayer doxology** ("For thine is the kingdom..."): in the Textus Receptus and KJV (and Tyndale 1534), absent from the earliest manuscripts; RSV/NRSV/NIV/ESV drop or footnote it; DRB omits; Hart brackets it.
- **Ending of Mark:** the earliest manuscripts stop at 16:8 ("for they were afraid"); the longer ending (16:9-20) is later; all modern versions bracket/flag it.
- **Luke 23:34a** ("Father, forgive them"): absent from several of the oldest/best manuscripts; likely an early omission (a scribe uncomfortable with it) or addition; kept with a footnote by modern versions.

## Contested doctrines (steelmanned)

- **Faith vs works (Paul vs James).** Paul (Rom 3:28, Eph 2:8-9): justified by faith, not works. James 2:14-26: "faith without works is dead," "justified by works and not by faith alone," same proof-text (Abraham), opposite headline. Reformation = *sola fide*; Luther called James "an epistle of straw" (1522 preface) and relegated it. Council of Trent (1546) affirmed faith + works (and the deuterocanon). Reconciliation favored by most scholars: Paul answers "how you get in" (gift), James "what living faith does"; the New Perspective on Paul (Sanders, Dunn, Wright) argues "works of the Law" meant Jewish identity-markers (circumcision, food, sabbath), not good deeds in general. The seam is real; the churches still differ.
- **Canon differences.** Protestant 66; Catholic 73 (deuterocanon: Tobit, Judith, Wisdom, Sirach, Baruch, 1-2 Maccabees, additions to Daniel/Esther); Eastern Orthodox wider (3-4 Maccabees, Ps 151, etc.); Ethiopian Orthodox widest (1 Enoch, Jubilees). Root: the early church used the Greek Septuagint (which carried the extra books); the rabbinic Hebrew canon did not. Luther followed the Hebrew, labeled the extras "Apocrypha"; Trent canonized them in response. NT canon settled slowly: Athanasius's 39th Festal Letter (367) is the first list of exactly our 27; Marcion's truncated canon and the Muratorian fragment mark the earlier contest.
- **Deity of Christ / Trinity.** John 1:1 + the anarthrous theos; high Christology argued to be early (Bauckham, Hurtado) not a late Hellenistic overlay; the dogma is formalized at Nicaea (325, *homoousios*) and Constantinople (381).
- **Resurrection (historical claim).** Earliest source is the creed Paul cites in 1 Cor 15:3-8, dated to within a few years of the crucifixion (well before the Gospels). The "minimal facts" case (Habermas, Licona, Craig) argues the empty tomb + the disciples' experiences need explaining; skeptics (Ehrman; Allison's nuanced middle) grant the experiences but not the supernatural cause. The post states the claim is early (not a slow legend) and locates the believer/skeptic split honestly at the cause, without overclaiming.
- **Virgin birth / Isaiah 7:14.** Jewish reading: a near-term sign to Ahaz, almah = young woman. Christian reading: typological fulfillment via the Septuagint's parthenos quoted in Matt 1:23. The 1952 RSV "young woman" at Isaiah 7:14 triggered the burning incident (below). Both readings are linguistically honest about their own texts.

## Translation-lineage facts (verified)

- Tyndale: first English NT printed from the Greek, 1526; smuggled into England in bales of cloth; strangled then burned near Brussels (Vilvoorde), 1536. Wycliffe's English Bible (1380s) was from the Latin Vulgate, by hand, banned; his bones were exhumed and burned (1428).
- **KJV NT is ~83% Tyndale, word for word**: David Daniell, announced at the 1997 Library of Congress exhibition, citing the Nielson-Skousen computer study (published 1998, *Reformation* journal); Brian Moynahan cites 84% (NT) and 75.8% (the OT books Tyndale did). Cited as "an estimated 83 percent."
- Manuscript families: Textus Receptus (Erasmus, 1516, from a few late manuscripts) underlies Tyndale + KJV; the modern critical text (Nestle-Aland / UBS, from the oldest codices and papyri) underlies RSV/NRSV/NIV/ESV. ~5,800 Greek NT manuscripts survive; none is the autograph.

## The RSV "young woman" incident (verified)

When the RSV (1952) rendered Isaiah 7:14 itself as "a young woman shall conceive,"
a North Carolina pastor (Rocky Mount) publicly burned the page from his pulpit,
and a critic mailed the ashes in a tin can to Luther Weigle, dean of Yale
Divinity and head of the RSV committee. Documented by Bruce Metzger (*The Bible
in Translation*) and Peter Thuesen (*In Discordance with the Scriptures*); see
also the academic study "The Bible Under Fire." The RSV kept "virgin" at Matt
1:23 because there Matthew quotes the Greek Septuagint.
