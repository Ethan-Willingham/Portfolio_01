# The Art of War (孫子兵法): Research & Fact-Check Dossier

Working dossier for a scholarship-grade, side-by-side translation post on Sun Tzu's *The Art of War*. Audience includes sinologists and military historians who will fact-check, so contested points are flagged throughout and claims carry source URLs.

A one-line orientation before the detail: there is no contemporary evidence for Sun Wu the man, the received text is most likely a Warring-States composite, and the single most important modern event for the book is the 1972 Yinqueshan bamboo find. Get those three things right and the post stands up.

**Conventions in this file:** LEGEND = traditional story, EVIDENCE = documented/archaeological, CONTESTED = live scholarly dispute. Pinyin first, Wade-Giles in parentheses. The frozen knowledge cutoff means dates/quotes flagged "verify against print" should be confirmed against the physical edition before publication.

---

## 0. Cross-cutting cautions (read before drafting)

These are the traps most likely to embarrass the post in front of a fact-checking reader:

1. **Two different characters that look alike.** 勢 *shì* ("force/configuration/strategic advantage," the title of Chapter 5) is NOT 實 *shí* ("full/solid/substance," the second half of 虛實 in Chapter 6). Different word, different tone, different character. Keep them apart.
2. **Verbatim Sun Tzu vs. popular compressions.** "Every battle is won before it is fought" (Gordon Gekko, Bill Belichick locker room) is a *paraphrase* of Chapter 4, not a line in the text. "Know your enemy and you will win a hundred battles" is an *inflation* of 百戰不殆 ("you will not be in peril"). Attribute paraphrases as paraphrases.
3. **Mislabeled online texts.** A popular blog (sun-tzu-aow.blogspot.com) reproduces **Ralph Sawyer's** chapter titles as if they were Ames's. A widely circulated `griffith.txt` is actually **Giles**. The phrase "strategic configuration of power" for *shi* is **Sawyer's**, not Ames's. Do not trust uncredited full-text dumps.
4. **Ames works from a different base text.** Roger Ames (1993) translates the 1972 Yinqueshan bamboo manuscript, so his wording and sometimes sentence order legitimately differ from translators of the received text. His divergences are not errors.
5. **The book is mostly a field manual.** A curated "keystone passages" post is honest only if it says so: the famous aphorisms cluster in chapters 1 and 3 to 6, while more than half the book (chapter 2 and chapters 7 to 13) is concrete logistics, terrain taxonomy, sign-reading, fire tactics, and spycraft. See Section 5.

## 1. AUTHORSHIP & DATE (contested)

### 1.1 Was there a historical Sun Wu? (mostly LEGEND; the case against is strong)

**Traditional claim (LEGEND).** Sun Wu (孫武), a strategist from the state of Qi who fled to Wu and served King Helü (Helu / Ho Lu, r. 514 to 496 BCE); conventional lifetime c. 544 to 496 BCE; said to be a contemporary of Confucius. "Sun Tzu / Sunzi" (孫子) is a title, "Master Sun," not a personal name. ([Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War); [World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Sun-Tzu/))

**The decisive negative evidence (EVIDENCE): the *Zuo Zhuan* silence.** The strongest argument against historicity is that Sun Wu is entirely absent from the *Zuo Zhuan* (Zuozhuan), the major narrative history of the Spring and Autumn period, which records the era's events and military figures in detail. It never mentions him. ([Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))

This is sharpened by the **Battle of Boju (506 BCE)**, the Wu-versus-Chu campaign Sima Qian credits to Sun Wu. The *Zuo Zhuan*, the primary source for that battle, names the Wu leadership as King Helü, his brother Fugai, and Wu Zixu, and does not mention Sun Wu anywhere. The contemporary record of the victory he supposedly won omits him. ([Wikipedia: Battle of Boju](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Boju))

**The name itself is suspect.** "Sun Wu" appears in no surviving text earlier than Sima Qian's *Shiji* (c. 100 BCE). Some scholars propose it is not a personal name but a descriptive cognomen. ([Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War); [New World Encyclopedia](https://www.newworldencyclopedia.org/entry/The_Art_of_War))

**The doubt is old.** Chinese scholars questioned Sun Wu early. The Song-dynasty scholar **Ye Shi / Ye Shui-hsin** (12th to 13th c.) put the *Zuo Zhuan* problem sharply, preserved in Giles: "In Tso's Commentary no Sun Wu appears at all... In the case of Sun Wu, whose fame and achievements were so brilliant, the omission is much more glaring." ([Giles intro, Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War_(Sun)/Sun_Wu_and_his_Book))

**Net position (CONTESTED).** A real strategist behind a *core* of the text cannot be ruled out, but there is no positive contemporary evidence for the man, and the source that should mention him does not. Modern scholarship runs from cautious agnosticism (Ames, Griffith) to flat denial (Mair, who titles an appendix "The Pseudo-Biography of Sun Wu"). Mark his biography as tradition, not history.

### 1.2 The Sima Qian biography and the concubine story (LEGEND)

**Source and date.** The biography is in chapter 65 of Sima Qian's *Shiji* (*Records of the Grand Historian*), compiled c. 100 to 94 BCE, roughly **400 years** after Sun Wu's supposed lifetime. ([Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html))

**The story (LEGEND), per the *Shiji* via Giles.** King Helü, having read the thirteen chapters, asks Sun Wu to test his theories on women. Sun Wu is given 180 palace ladies, splits them into two companies, and puts the king's two favorite concubines at the heads. He confirms they know front, back, left, right, then drums a command; the women only laugh. Sun Wu says if orders are unclear "it is the fault of their officers," and repeats them. They laugh again. He rules the orders are now clear, so the fault lies with the officers, and orders the two favorite company commanders beheaded. The king begs him to spare them; Sun Wu refuses ("there are certain commands of His Majesty which... I am unable to accept") and executes them. The troops thereafter drill "with perfect accuracy and precision," and Helü makes him general. ([Giles intro, Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War_(Sun)/Sun_Wu_and_his_Book); [Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html))

**Reliability (assessment).** Single source, ~400 years later, uncorroborated and effectively contradicted by the earlier *Zuo Zhuan* on the very campaigns named. The anecdote reads as a didactic illustration of the text's own doctrine of clear command and discipline, the kind of exemplary tale a tradition builds around a founder. Chinese scholars have treated it as fiction since at least the 11th to 13th century. ([World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Sun-Tzu/))

**Defender's view (for balance).** Lionel Giles accepted Sun Wu as historical: "Sima Qian at least had no doubt about the reality of Sun Wu as an historical personage." This is an early-20th-century position; the scholarly center has since moved toward skepticism. ([Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html))

### 1.3 The dating debate: Spring and Autumn (~500 BCE) vs. Warring States (CONTESTED)

**Traditional dating (LEGEND/tradition).** Late Spring and Autumn, c. 500 BCE, contemporary with Confucius and Helü's reign. Giles narrowed it to c. 505 to 496 BCE. ([Giles intro, Wikisource](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War_(Sun)/Sun_Wu_and_his_Book))

**Modern-scholarly dating (EVIDENCE-based).** Prevailing view: the **Warring States period**, roughly the 5th to 4th centuries BCE, with many favoring the second half of the 4th century. Columbia's Asia for Educators: "There is no firm evidence that Sunzi existed... the book bearing his name was compiled a hundred years later, sometime in the second half of the fourth century B.C." A common compromise dates the earliest *core* to c. 500 to 430 BCE, reaching recognizable form later. ([Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html); [Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))

**Internal evidence pointing to Warring States.** Argued primarily from "style of composition and descriptions of warfare." The two anachronisms most consistently and explicitly named in reputable sources:
- **Crossbows**: the crossbow did not become a major weapon until the Warring States period, yet the text references it (and the crossbow-trigger image is central to Chapter 5). ([World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Sun-Tzu/))
- **Cavalry / scale of war**: mounted warfare and mass infantry armies fighting protracted campaigns belong to the later period, not the smaller chariot-led, ritual-bounded Spring and Autumn battlefield. ([World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Sun-Tzu/); [Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))

> **Verify-before-print flag.** The broader anachronism list often repeated (armies of tens of thousands, protracted sieges, professional generalship displacing the warrior-aristocrat, bureaucratic/administrative vocabulary) is the standard sinological rationale, but the encyclopedic sources name the crossbow and cavalry points explicitly while treating the rest as "style and descriptions of warfare." If the post asserts the others individually, pin each to a named translator's introduction (Griffith, Ames, or Mair) at page level first.

**Named scholars and positions:**
- **Samuel Griffith** (1963): argued for the Warring States period (453 to 221 BCE) because the military realities fit it better; held Sun Wu may not have written the text. ([Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html))
- **Victor Mair** (2007): strongest skeptic. Holds **Sun Wu never existed**; the text **coalesced over roughly 75 years, c. mid-4th to early-3rd century BCE** (in interview he dates individual chapters, e.g. Ch. 9 to ~345 BCE, Ch. 13 to ~272 BCE). ([Columbia UP](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/the-art-of-war/9780231133821/); [Sonshi: Mair interview](https://www.sonshi.com/victor-mair-interview.html))
- **Roger Ames** (1993): emphasizes the *Zuo Zhuan* silence; allows a possible early nucleus tied to a "Master Sun" later edited by other hands; dates the material to the Warring States milieu on the strength of Yinqueshan. ([eNotes: Ames 1993](https://www.enotes.com/topics/sun-tzu/criticism/criticism/roger-t-ames-essay-date-1993))
- **Liang Qichao** (early 20th c.): proposed the surviving text was written in the 4th century BCE by the descendant **Sun Bin**, not Sun Wu. ([Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))
- **Lionel Giles** (1910): traditionalist holdout for both Sun Wu's historicity and a late-Spring-and-Autumn date.

> **Verify-before-print flag.** The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy has **no standalone Sunzi entry**, and a dedicated Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy "Sunzi" article could not be confirmed. Do not cite SEP/IEP for Sunzi authorship unless checked directly. The philosophy-reference framing comes from the Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Sunzi entry) and Columbia's Asia for Educators. D.C. Lau and Christopher Rand have real composite/stratigraphic analyses but were not retrieved in primary form here; confirm wording before attributing.

### 1.4 One author vs. composite/accreted text (CONTESTED, composite is mainstream)

**Composite view (mainstream).** Modern scholarship overwhelmingly treats the received text as a **composite assembled over generations around an early "Sunzi" core**, edited, expanded, and reorganized before crystallizing into the received thirteen chapters. This sits inside Michael Nylan's broader point that essentially all early Chinese texts are "composite texts" compiled over time. On this view, "who was the author" is the wrong question. ([Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War); [The Conversation, Nylan](https://theconversation.com/guide-to-the-classics-the-art-of-war-the-ancient-chinese-war-manual-loved-by-edgelords-and-management-gurus-227040))

**Single-author defense (minority).** Giles and the older traditionalist line treat the thirteen chapters as a substantially unified composition. Ames's moderated version (genuine early nucleus, later editing) blends the two positions rather than defending sole authorship.

### 1.5 The relationship to Sun Bin's Art of War (孫臏兵法) (resolved by Yinqueshan, see Section 2)

**Two figures.** Tradition holds **Sun Bin** (孫臏, d. c. 316 BCE) to be a descendant of Sun Wu about a century later, a well-attested 4th-century strategist (associated with Qi's victory over Wei, conventionally 341 BCE), credited with a *separate* treatise also titled *Bingfa* (孫臏兵法, *Sun Bin Bingfa*). ([Wikipedia: Sun Bin's Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Bin%27s_Art_of_War))

**The centuries-long confusion.** Han bibliographies listed two "Master Sun" military texts (distinguished as 吳孫子 "the Master Sun of Wu" and 齊孫子 "the Master Sun of Qi"), but Sun Bin's was lost for more than a millennium (gone by the early Tang, 618 to 907). With one Sun text surviving, two attested, and Sun Wu's own historicity shadowed, scholars could not be sure whether Sun Wu and Sun Bin were one person or two, or whether there had ever been two distinct texts. That is exactly why Liang Qichao could argue the surviving text was really Sun Bin's. ([Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html); [Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))

**Resolution:** the 1972 Yinqueshan find recovered both texts separately and proved them distinct works by two different men, while leaving Sun Wu's personal historicity open. Detail in Section 2.

## 2. THE YINQUESHAN BAMBOO TEXTS (1972): the book's Mawangdui

This is the single most important modern event for the text. It is to the *Art of War* what the Mawangdui/Guodian finds are to the *Tao Te Ching*.

### 2.1 The discovery

On **April 10, 1972**, construction workers unearthed two Han-dynasty tombs at the foot of **Yinqueshan (銀雀山, "Silver Sparrow Mountain")**, just southeast of **Linyi (臨沂)** in southern **Shandong province**. ([Wikipedia: Yinqueshan Han Slips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yinqueshan_Han_Slips); [chinaculture.org](http://en.chinaculture.org/library/2008-01/15/content_39369.htm))

- **Two tombs.** Tomb 1 yielded the mass of texts; Tomb 2 a smaller cache.
- **Slip count.** ~**4,942 strips** (Tomb 1) plus 32 (Tomb 2). Some sources give ~4,974 total. Use "roughly 5,000 slips."
- **Condition.** Heavily damaged: more than 38% of the slips were badly degraded. The recovered texts are **fragmentary**, which matters for any claim about what the bamboo *Art of War* "says."
- **Occupant.** Tomb 1 held a military officer surnamed **Sima (司馬)**.
- **Dating.** Western Han, reign of **Emperor Wu (漢武帝)**. Tomb 2 contained a **calendar for 134 BCE**, anchoring the chronology; the two tombs date to roughly **140/134 BCE and 118 BCE**. Script is early clerical (隸書). Safe phrasing: "two Western Han tombs sealed around 140 to 118 BCE."

### 2.2 What was recovered

A small library of military, political, and divinatory texts. Headline recoveries:
- **Sun Tzu's *Art of War* (孫子兵法)**: fragments of the **thirteen-chapter core**, plus several associated/extra chapters (see 2.4).
- **Sun Bin's *Art of War* (孫臏兵法)**: a text **long thought lost**.

Also: the **Six Secret Teachings** (六韜, *Liutao*), the **Weiliaozi** (尉繚子), the **Yanzi** (晏子), the 134 BCE calendar, and assorted political/divination texts. ([Wikipedia: Yinqueshan Han Slips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yinqueshan_Han_Slips))

### 2.3 Why it matters (a): it settled the Sun Wu vs. Sun Bin debate

**Pre-1972 problem.** Both Sun Wu and Sun Bin were called "Sunzi" in classical sources, and Sun Bin's book had vanished, so some historians concluded the two men were identical or doubted Sun Bin's treatise ever existed. ([Facts and Details](https://factsanddetails.com/china/cat2/4sub7/entry-5359.html): "Since both Sun Wu and Sun Bin were referred to as Sun Tzu in classical texts, some historians believed them identical prior to the rediscovery of Sun Bin's treatise in 1972.")

**What the find proved.** Recovering **both texts in the same tomb library as two physically distinct works** demonstrated that Sun Wu and Sun Bin were two different men who wrote two different books, ending the conflation. Roughly **15 to 16 reconstructed chapters** of Sun Bin's long-lost treatise (lost ~1,400+ years) were recovered. ([Wikipedia: Yinqueshan Han Slips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yinqueshan_Han_Slips))

### 2.4 Why it matters (b): the oldest physical copy, vs. the received text

**The received ("transmitted") text.** The standard *Art of War* descends from the Song-dynasty **十一家注孫子 (*Shiyijia zhu Sunzi*, "Sunzi with the Collected Commentaries of Eleven Schools/Commentators")**, the layered commentary edition consolidated in the Song. The commentators run from **Cao Cao (曹操)** through Tang and Song scholars (Li Quan, Du Mu, Mei Yaochen, Wang Xi, He Yanxi, Zhang Yu), with Du You's *Tongdian* glosses counted as the eleventh. ([Library of Congress: *Shi yi jia zhu Sunzi*](https://www.loc.gov/item/2021666390))
> Naming note: sometimes given as "Ten Commentators" depending on how Du You is counted. Use "Eleven Commentators," noting the ambiguity if relevant.

**The age leap.** The Yinqueshan copy (sealed c. 140 to 118 BCE) **predates the Song received text by roughly a thousand years**, making it the oldest physical witness then known. ([historyofinformation.com, entry 2890](https://www.historyofinformation.com/detail.php?entryid=2890))

**What it confirms.** The bamboo version is **largely consistent with the received thirteen-chapter text**, strong evidence that the **core thirteen chapters are genuinely ancient** (stable and circulating by the early Han, not a Song-era reconstruction). About **2,700 characters** of the received text (roughly a third) survive on the slips.

**What it changes/adds (state carefully).** It is fragmentary and damaged; it shows **textual fluidity** (variant readings, omissions, additions vs. the later recension); and it came with **roughly five additional/supplemental chapters or fragments associated with Sun Tzu** that the received Song edition dropped. These are the materials Ames translates as supplementary.

> **Recommended framing (avoids overclaiming):** "The bamboo *Art of War* broadly matches the text we already had, which is the real headline: it shows the thirteen chapters were old and stable. But it is damaged and partial, it carries variant wordings, and it came with several extra Sun-Tzu-associated passages the received edition had dropped."

### 2.5 The Tao Te Ching analogy (accurate)

- **Mawangdui (馬王堆), 1973**: Han-tomb **silk** *Laozi* manuscripts near Changsha, Hunan (c. 200 and 175 BCE), which put the "De" section before the "Dao" section, reversing the received order. ([Wikipedia: Mawangdui Silk Texts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mawangdui_Silk_Texts))
- **Guodian (郭店), 1993**: **bamboo-slip** *Laozi* material from Hubei (c. 300 BCE), the earliest *Daodejing* manuscript yet found. ([religion.uga.edu Guodian PDF](https://www.religion.uga.edu/sites/default/files/inline-files/guodian.pdf))

In both the military and Daoist cases, a Han-or-earlier tomb manuscript pushed the physical record back by roughly a millennium and forced scholars to treat the "received" text as an edited synthesis. (Chronology for color: Yinqueshan 1972 came first, then Mawangdui 1973, then Guodian 1993.)

> **CONTESTED, do NOT assert flatly.** Some popular sources claim the Mawangdui (1973) cache *also* contained an *Art of War*. This is not securely established among specialists. Keep the manuscript analogy strictly *Daodejing*-side (Mawangdui/Guodian) vs. *Art of War* (Yinqueshan). If you mention a Mawangdui *Sunzi* at all, flag it as "reported but not firmly established."

### 2.6 Ames 1993 is built on Yinqueshan (CONFIRMED)

Roger T. Ames, **_Sun-Tzu: The Art of Warfare: The First English Translation Incorporating the Recently Discovered Yin-ch'üeh-shan Texts_** (New York: **Ballantine Books, 1993**). ("Yin-ch'üeh-shan" is Wade-Giles for Yinqueshan.) It is the first English translation to use the Yinqueshan thirteen-chapter version, preserves the ~2,700 surviving characters, **translates the five supplemental chapters**, and carries an introduction on the text, the excavations, and authorship. ([WorldCat](https://search.worldcat.org/title/680050665); [eNotes: Ames 1993](https://www.enotes.com/topics/sun-tzu/criticism/criticism/roger-t-ames-essay-date-1993))

> **Verify-before-print flag.** Most granular Yinqueshan claims (slip counts, the supplemental-chapter count, textual-comparison specifics) trace to Wikipedia, Baidu Baike, ChinaKnowledge, and tertiary encyclopedias. For a fact-checked post, anchor the textual-comparison and supplemental-chapter claims to a print authority (Ames 1993's own introduction, or Ralph Sawyer / Krzysztof Gawlikowski on the Yinqueshan finds). The freely downloadable [Sino-Platonic Papers no. 178](https://sino-platonic.org/complete/spp178_art_of_war.pdf) (Mair) is worth mining for citable specifics.

## 3. THE KEY UNTRANSLATABLE TERMS

A side-by-side post lives or dies on these. Verified translator wordings are in quotation marks; anything not confirmed against a primary source is flagged "verify against print."

### 3.1 勢 SHI (Wade-Giles *shih*): the single hardest word

**Character / readings.** 勢 (simplified 势). Pinyin **shì**, Wade-Giles **shih**. Ames's pronunciation aid: "pronounced like the affirmative, 'sure'." Title of **Chapter 5**.

**Gloss.** Dictionaries fan it across "power, force, momentum, influence, authority; outward appearance; posture, position; situation, circumstances; tendency, trend," plus the modern physics sense "potential." The strategic gloss to use: **the latent or configured force released by position and timing, the "potential energy" of a configured situation.** Mair: "one of the key concepts of the *Sun Zi*. It is also one of the most ineffable." ([Wiktionary 勢](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8B%A2); [Pinyin.info on Mair](https://pinyin.info/news/2008/the-art-of-war-a-new-translation/))

**The classic images** (Chinese from [ctext.org ch. 5](https://ctext.org/art-of-war/energy); Giles from [suntzusaid.com/book/5](https://suntzusaid.com/book/5)):
- **Water sweeping boulders:** 激水之疾，至於漂石者，勢也。 Giles: "The onset of troops is like the rush of a torrent which will even roll stones along in its course."
- **The round stone down the mountain (the chapter's closing line):** 如轉圓石於千仞之山者，勢也。 Giles: "the energy developed by good fighting men is as the momentum of a round stone rolled down a mountain thousands of feet in height."
- *Precision note:* the closing image is strictly the **round stone (圓石)** down a mountain of 千仞 ("thousands of feet"). The "logs and stones (木石)" phrasing is the *preceding* passage (positioning men on a slope creates the propensity; square ones stop, round ones roll). Keep them distinct.

**Why it resists one English word.** It is not the name of a thing. It names a **dynamic, relational propensity that emerges from how a situation is configured**, and it is never possessed or constant. Ames, the clearest scholarly witness: "Although I have translated *shih* consistently as 'strategic advantage,' it is a complex idea peculiar to the Chinese tradition, and resists easy formulaic translation," carrying its senses all at once, not one at a time. He also notes "an army does not have fixed strategic advantages (*shih*) or an invariable position (*hsing*)." ([Ames PDF, pp. 50 to 56](https://www.wrd.ch/triboni/store/Sun_Tsu_The_Art_of_Warfare.pdf)) The Legalist use confirms it is a relation to a *position*: for Shen Dao what matters is "the power of his position," not the ruler's personal qualities. ([SEP: Chinese Legalism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/chinese-legalism/))

**How each translator renders 勢:**

| Translator | Ch. 5 title | Renders 勢 as |
|---|---|---|
| Giles (1910) | Energy | **energy** |
| Griffith (1963) | Energy | **energy / potential** ("His potential is that of a fully drawn crossbow") |
| Cleary (1988) | Force | **force / momentum** |
| Ames (1993) | Strategic Advantage (shih) | **strategic advantage** (also "purchase") |
| Denma (2001) | **Shi** (left untranslated) | **shih / shi**, glossed not translated |
| Minford (2002) | **Potential Energy** | **potential energy** |
| Mair (2007) | **Configuration** | **configuration** |

Titles confirmed against primary TOCs ([Griffith archive.org](https://archive.org/details/artofwargriff00sunz); [Ames PDF](https://www.wrd.ch/triboni/store/Sun_Tsu_The_Art_of_Warfare.pdf); [Denma/Bowdoin textmenu](https://learn.bowdoin.edu/suntzu/textmenu.html); [Columbia UP](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/the-art-of-war/9780231133821/)). Denma's gloss: "Most simply said, *shih* is the ever-changing configuration of power in one's environment" ([Denma/Bowdoin ch.5](https://learn.bowdoin.edu/suntzu/content/chapter_05/index.html)).
> **Attribution caution:** "strategic configuration of power" is **Ralph Sawyer's** rendering, not Ames's. A fabricated "Giles footnote" defining shih via Li Quan/Du Mu circulates online and is **not** in Giles; do not use it.

#### François Jullien: shi as "propensity" (the heart of the post's intellectual payoff)

**The books.** *The Propensity of Things: Toward a History of Efficacy in China* (French 1992; English trans. Janet Lloyd, **Zone Books, 1995**). And *A Treatise on Efficacy* (French 1996; English trans. Janet Lloyd, **University of Hawai'i Press, 2004**). Jullien renders shi as **"propensity."** ([Princeton UP](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780942299953/the-propensity-of-things); [UH Press](https://uhpress.hawaii.edu/title/a-treatise-on-efficacy-between-western-and-chinese-thinking/))

**The thesis (two models of effective action), in his own words** (*Treatise*, [PDF](https://uberty.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/10/francois-jullien-treatise-on-efficacy-between-western-and-chinese-thinking.pdf)):
- The Greek/Western model, conceive an ideal form then impose it: "a tradition that conceives of efficacy on the basis of abstract, ideal forms, set up as models to be projected onto the world... the heroism of action." He names the Greek terms: "We set up an ideal form *(eidos)*, which we take to be a goal *(telos)*, and we then act in such a way as to make it become fact."
- The Chinese model, detect and ride the propensity already in the situation: "the key to Chinese strategy is to rely on the inherent potential of the situation and to be carried along by it as it evolves... A Chinese general... is careful not to impose upon the course of events any notion of his own of how things ought to be."

**Propensity comes from the configuration, not the actor** (best line for "not a thing one possesses"): wisdom "lies in strategically exploiting the propensity that emanates from this particular configuration of reality" (Princeton summary); "potential is circumstantial, it only exists thanks to the circumstances and vice-versa" (*Treatise*).

**Jullien reads Sun Tzu's rolling-stone image as the figure of propensity itself:** "The slope here serves as an image of the *propensity* that results from the relations of force that the general knows how to exploit... the effect happens *sponte sua* and is irresistible." The Latin *sponte sua* ("of its own accord") is his crisp encapsulation: you arrange the slope, the stone rolls itself, and "whoever knows how to exploit the potential of the situation can easily win the day."

**shi spans domains.** *The Propensity of Things* tours shi across military strategy, politics (the Legalist throne as a "position of strength"), calligraphy and painting, literary theory, history, and "first philosophy."

> **CONTESTED, flag in the post.** Jullien's neat China-vs-Greece binary is heavily criticized. The named attack is **Jean-François Billeter, *Contre François Jullien* (2006)**, charging a "myth of the alterity of China," an untenable dogma of radical East/West difference that treats Chinese thinkers ahistorically. Present Jullien as a powerful, influential interpretation, not settled fact. ([European Guanxi review](https://www.europeanguanxi.com/post/book-review-pourquoi-l-europe-r%C3%A9flexions-d-un-sinologue-by-jean-fran%C3%A7ois-billeter))

### 3.2 節 JIE (Wade-Giles *chieh*): the timed release, the "node"

**Character / readings.** 節 (simplified 节). Pinyin **jié**, Wade-Giles **chieh**. Bamboo radical 竹 + phonetic 即.

**Gloss.** Root is the **node/joint of bamboo**, extending to "measure, moderation, restraint; rhythm/beat/tempo; a section; integrity; a tally." It evokes both a *segmenting point* in a continuous thing and *measured regulation / the right interval*. In Chapter 5 it is the **precise instant** at which stored force is released, the paired counter-term to 勢. ([Wiktionary 節](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AF%80))

**The images** ([ctext.org ch. 5](https://ctext.org/art-of-war/energy)):
- **Crossbow and trigger:** 勢如張弩，節如機發。 shi = the drawn crossbow (stored potential); jie = the release of the trigger. (Variant printings: 勢如彍弩，節如發機; sense identical.)
- **The bird of prey:** 鷙鳥之疾，至於毀折者，節也。 The raptor's body-breaking strike works because of jie, the perfectly judged instant. (Water/stones illustrate **shi**; the falcon illustrates **jie**.)

**How translators render 節 (crossbow line):**

| Translator | 節 *jié* | Crossbow line |
|---|---|---|
| Giles (1910) | **"decision"** | "Energy may be likened to the bending of a crossbow; decision, to the releasing of a trigger." ([suntzusaid 5.15](https://suntzusaid.com/book/5/15/)) |
| Griffith (1963) | **"timing"** | "His potential is that of a fully drawn crossbow; his timing, the release of the trigger." |
| Cleary (1988) | **"timing"** | "the momentum of those skilled in war is like a drawn crossbow, the release of a trigger" |
| Ames (1993) | **"timing"** | "His strategic advantage (shih) is like a drawn crossbow and his timing is like releasing the trigger." |
| Denma (2001) | **"the node"** | "Shih is like drawing the crossbow. The node is like pulling the trigger." |

**Range:** "decision" (Giles, the outlier, widely felt weakest since it loses the temporal core), "timing" (the consensus: Griffith, Cleary, Ames), "the node" (Denma, most literal). Wider literature: "measure," "rhythm," "tempo," "control of timing," "the release."

**Why it is hard.** (1) The literal "node/joint" sense and the functional "timed release" sense both press, and no single English word holds both. (2) It is defined only *relationally*, as shi's counterpart (stored potential vs. discharge), so it must be rendered as a consistent pair. (3) shi itself is near-untranslatable, which destabilizes its partner. Giles concedes: "None of the commentators seem to grasp the real point of the simile of energy and the force stored up in the bent cross-bow until released by the finger on the trigger." ([suntzusaid 5.15](https://suntzusaid.com/book/5/15/))

### 3.3 詭道 GUI DAO: "the way of deception"

**Characters / readings.** 詭 *guǐ* (Wade-Giles *kuei*, 言 "speech" + phonetic 危) + 道 *dào* (*tao*).

**Gloss.** 詭 = "deceitful, deceptive, cunning, crafty, sly, tricky; to deceive," and a second branch "**strange, weird, bizarre, uncanny, eccentric**." 道 = "way, path, method, principle" (and, loaded, "the Tao"). So 詭道 is literally "the deceit-*dao*," "the way of the deceptive/uncanny." The strange/uncanny sense matters: 詭 is not only lying but the disorienting *unexpected*. ([Wiktionary 詭](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%A9%AD))

**The line and its place.** 兵者，詭道也 ("warfare is the *dao* of deception") opens the deception unit near the end of **Chapter 1** (計). It frames a catalogue of paired ruses ("when able, seem unable; when near, seem far"), capped by 攻其無備，出其不意 ("attack where unprepared, emerge where unexpected"). The recurring verb 示之 ("show him") signals the subject is the *management of appearances*. ([ctext.org ch. 1](https://ctext.org/art-of-war/laying-plans))

**How translators render 兵者，詭道也:**

| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| Giles (1910) | "**All warfare is based on deception.**" |
| Griffith (1963) | "**All warfare is based on deception.**" |
| Cleary (1988) | "A military operation involves deception." |
| Ames (1993) | "Warfare is the art (tao) of deceit." |
| Minford (2002) | "The Way of War is / A Way of Deception." |
| Mair (2007) | "Warfare is a way of deception." |
| Denma (2001) | "The military is a Tao of deception." (best-attested form; **verify against print**) |

**The fault line:** render 道 flatly ("based on," Giles/Griffith) or keep the **Way/Tao** resonance (Ames, Minford, Mair, Denma). Keeping "Way/Tao" is more defensible: 道 is freighted, and the sentence makes deception a *method/principle*.

**Why "deception" carries weight.** The line is often read as the book's thesis of an amoral, anti-heroic ethos (win by manipulation, not virtue or valor). Modern translators push back on "lying": **Mair** writes, "Too many people think that this gives a license to lie. I would suggest, rather, that what Sun Zi was getting at here is not sheer mendacity, but the need for the general to conceal from the enemy his true intentions." On this reading 詭道 is the controlled management of appearances (misdirection, the uncanny), closer to the second sense of 詭 than to mere falsehood. ([Sonshi: Mair interview](https://www.sonshi.com/victor-mair-interview.html))

### 3.4 無形 WU XING: "formlessness"

**Characters / readings.** 無形 (無 *wú* "without" + 形 *xíng* "form/shape"). Pinyin **wú xíng**, Wade-Giles **wu hsing**. 形 spans "form, shape, body, appearance, disposition, configuration."

**Gloss.** Having no perceptible form, no fixed disposition the enemy can read, so even the best spies learn nothing. **Central to Chapter 6**, the climax of the empty/full argument.

**The formlessness-and-spies passage.** Cleary: "Therefore the consummation of forming an army is to arrive at formlessness. When you have no form, undercover espionage cannot find out anything, intelligence cannot form a strategy." Giles renders the idea as concealment: "the highest pitch you can attain is to conceal them [your dispositions]... you will be safe from the prying of the subtlest spies." Griffith's key verse: "The ultimate in disposing one's troops is to be without ascertainable shape." (Chinese: 故形兵之極，至於無形.) ([Cleary, MIT etext](https://web.mit.edu/~dcltdw/AOW/6.html); [Giles ch.6](https://suntzusaid.com/book/6))

**Range of 無形:** "formless / formlessness" (Cleary; Ames "the formless"), "without ascertainable shape" (Griffith), or paraphrased "conceal your dispositions" (Giles). Literal options: "without form," "the shapeless."

**Why it is hard, and the link to 形 (Chapter 4 title).** 形 is *also* the title of Chapter 4, where it means the army's *visible* disposition. So the same character that names "having a disposition" in Ch. 4 is what you negate (無形) in Ch. 6. Giles's footnote states the core problem: 形 "is a very comprehensive and somewhat vague term. Literally, 'form,' 'body,' it comes to mean 'appearance,' 'attitude' or 'disposition'." ([Wikisource, Section IV](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War_(Sun)/Section_IV)) The negation reads simultaneously as a tactical instruction (show no exploitable formation) and a quasi-Daoist ideal (be like formless water).

### 3.5 虛實 XU SHI: "empty and full"

**Characters / readings.** 虛 *xū* ("empty, void, vacuity, weakness") + 實 *shí* ("full, solid, substance, strength"). Pinyin **xū shí**, Wade-Giles **hsü shih**. (Again: 實 *shí* is NOT 勢 *shì*.) **Title of Chapter 6** (虛實第六).

**Gloss.** Attack where the enemy is empty (undefended), avoid where he is full (strong); concentrate your substance against his void. Works concretely (gaps vs. defended positions) and as a yin-yang complementarity.

**Chapter 6 title renderings (all confirmed against primary sources):**

| Translator | Ch. 6 title |
|---|---|
| Giles (1910) | "**Weak Points and Strong**" ([Wikisource VI](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War_(Sun)/Section_VI)) |
| Griffith (1963) | "**Weaknesses and Strengths**" ([archive.org TOC](https://archive.org/details/artofwargriff00sunz)) |
| Cleary (1988) | "**Emptiness and Fullness**" ([MIT/Cleary](https://web.mit.edu/~dcltdw/AOW/6.html)) |
| Ames (1993) | "**Weak Points and Strong Points**" ([Ames PDF TOC](https://www.wrd.ch/triboni/store/Sun_Tsu_The_Art_of_Warfare.pdf)) |
| Minford (2002) | "**Empty and Full**" |
| Mair (2007) | "**Emptiness and Solidity**" ([Columbia UP](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/the-art-of-war/9780231133821/)) |
| Denma (2001) | "**The Solid and Empty**" ([Denma textmenu](https://learn.bowdoin.edu/suntzu/textmenu.html)) |

> **Three corrections caught during verification (do not revert):** Ames Ch. 6 is "**Weak Points and Strong Points**," NOT "Vacuity and Substance" (that is **Sawyer's**). Denma Ch. 6 is "**The Solid and Empty**." Griffith Ch. 6 is "**Weaknesses and Strengths**" (do not let it drift to Giles's "Weak Points and Strong").

**Why it is hard.** The polarity is doubly loaded and no English pair carries both loads. (1) *Concrete-military*: 虛 = an undefended gap, 實 = a defended strong point; "weak points and strong" (Giles) / "weaknesses and strengths" (Griffith) capture the tactics but lose the void/substance metaphysics. (2) *Abstract-philosophical*: 虛/實 are a yin-yang complementarity where each reverts into the other; "emptiness and fullness" (Cleary), "emptiness and solidity" (Mair) preserve the metaphysics but lose the concreteness. The split maps onto the translators' stances: the military-minded go concrete, the philosophically inclined go abstract, Minford's "Empty and Full" is neutral. **The chapter title alone tells you a translator's interpretive stance**, a strong citable thesis point for a side-by-side post.

### 3.6 廟算 MIAO SUAN: "temple calculations"

**Characters / readings.** 廟 *miào* (*miao*) = ancestral temple/shrine; 算 *suàn* (*suan*) = to calculate, reckon, count (cognate with the **counting rods** of early Chinese arithmetic). "Temple calculation/reckoning."

**The passage and its place.** It closes **Chapter 1** (計), tying the five factors and seven comparisons to a pre-war reckoning. Giles: "Now the general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought. The general who loses a battle makes but few calculations beforehand... It is by attention to this point that I can foresee who is likely to win or lose." Note the wordplay: 算 is both the *act* of reckoning (廟算) and the near-countable *score* accumulated (得算多, "gets many counts"), literally tallying counting-rods. ([ctext.org ch. 1](https://ctext.org/art-of-war/laying-plans); [suntzusaid 1.26](https://suntzusaid.com/book/1/26/))

**The five factors (五事) weighed:** 道 *dào* (the Way / moral influence), 天 *tiān* (Heaven / weather, season), 地 *dì* (Earth / terrain), 將 *jiàng* (the general), 法 *fǎ* (method-and-discipline / organization).

**The ritual.** The "temple" is the **ancestral temple (宗廟)**, the lineage shrine where the ruling house took ritual and major state decisions. Giles cites the Song commentator Zhang Yu (Chang Yu): "in ancient times it was customary for a temple to be set apart for the use of a general who was about to take the field, in order that he might there elaborate his plan of campaign." The picture is a formal, quasi-ritual pre-war council of estimation, tallying rods to see which side scores more.

**How translators render 廟算:**

| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| Giles (1910) | "calculations **in his temple**" |
| Griffith (1963) | "estimates... made **in the temple**" |
| Ames (1993) | "the **temple rehearsal** of the battle" |
| Cleary (1988) | "**at headquarters**" / having more "**strategic factors**" (deliberately de-ritualized, the outlier) |

**Range:** keep 廟 as the concrete ancestral temple (most translators, and the scholarly reading) or abstract it to "headquarters" (Cleary), which sacrifices ritual texture for accessibility.

**Why it is hard.** (1) 廟 anchors the line in a specific early-Chinese ritual-political institution with no English equivalent: "temple" risks a churchy connotation, "headquarters" erases the ritual. (2) The 算 pun (activity vs. counted score, rooted in physical rods) is nearly impossible in English. (3) The passage grounds the "assess-then-win-first" logic of the whole book, so a translator's choice here signals the whole reading of Sunzi as a treatise of *calculation* over *combat*.

### 3.7 The Taoist resonance, and the debate (CONTESTED)

**The resonance is real and shared by serious translators.** Five clusters:
- **Wu wei (無為) / winning without exertion.** Ch. 3: 不戰而屈人之兵 ("breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting"). The IEP stresses wu wei is "effortless action," not "doing nothing." ([IEP: Daoist Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/daoismdaoist-philosophy/))
- **Water (soft overcomes hard).** Ch. 6's water passage maps onto *Daodejing* ch. 8 ("The highest good is like water... not contending") and ch. 78 ("Nothing in the world is as soft and yielding as water. Yet for dissolving the hard... nothing can surpass it"). The Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy: "Sunzi employs the Daoist metaphor of being like water."
- **Formlessness (wu xing) and the void (xu).**
- **The adept who rides circumstance (shi).** Ames's translation is the standard scholarly statement.
- **Reversal and complementarity of opposites.**

Translators who foreground it: **Cleary** presents the text "from the point of view of its background in the great spiritual tradition of Taoism"; **Mair** says he has "shown how the Sun Zi reflects a Taoistic approach to war"; **Ames** anchors the early-Chinese correlative-cosmology reading. ([Shambhala: Cleary](https://www.shambhala.com/thomas-cleary-translations-art-of-war-martial-arts/); [Sonshi: Mair](https://www.sonshi.com/victor-mair-interview.html))

**The caution (get this nuance right).**
- **The chronology blocks simple "borrowing."** Yinqueshan confirmed the *Sunzi* as a genuine pre-Han text with a core around the 5th century BCE. The received *Daodejing* is generally a composite Warring States anthology stable only by the mid-3rd century BCE (SEP: the traditional 6th-century authorship "seems unlikely"; Chad Hansen: "there was no single author, no Laozi"). So the *Sunzi*'s core is contemporary with or earlier than the finished *Laozi* and cannot straightforwardly have "borrowed" from it. (CONTESTED: Mair pushes the *Sunzi*'s compilation later, to ~mid-4th century BCE, so the dating itself is disputed.) ([SEP: Laozi](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/laozi/); [World History Encyclopedia](https://www.worldhistory.org/Sun-Tzu/))
- **Careful framing = shared milieu and mutual dialogue, not one-way derivation.** Thomas Michael (*Dao* 19, 2020) argues the two texts' authors "honed their respective systems of thought by way of at least occasional encounter, dialogue, and debate" while their texts were still fluid (intertextuality, not borrowing). Robin Yates showed the *other* Yinqueshan texts (yin-yang military texts buried with the *Sunzi*) tie to Huang-Lao Daoism and yin-yang/Five-Phases cosmology. Christopher Rand (*Military Thought in Early China*, 2017) frames "Daoist-looking" military thought as a Han-era Huang-Lao **syncretism**, warning against retrojecting it onto the 5th-to-4th-century *Sunzi*. ([Michael, Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11712-019-09701-3); [Yates, Cambridge *Early China* 19](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/early-china/article/abs/yinyang-texts-from-yinqueshan-an-introduction-and-partial-reconstruction-with-notes-on-their-significance-in-relation-to-huanglao-daoism/3445D7ABDA8D2437C70344444A883378); [Rand, SUNY Press](https://sunypress.edu/p-6357-military-thought-in-early-china.aspx))
- **The "schools" are a retrospective Han invention.** The category *daojia* ("Daoist school") was "a creation of the historian Sima Tan (d. 110 B.C.E.)." The *Art of War* was traditionally classed as **military writings (兵家 bingjia), not Daoist**, and headed the *Seven Military Classics* canonized in 1080 CE. ([IEP: Daoist Philosophy](https://iep.utm.edu/daoismdaoist-philosophy/); [Wikipedia: School of the Military Strategists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School_of_the_Military_Strategists))

**Defensible bottom line for the post.** The affinity is genuine (even Mair, a hard skeptic of the Sun Wu legend, affirms a "Taoistic approach to war"). The sentence to avoid is "Sun Tzu borrowed from the *Tao Te Ching*." The honest framing is a **shared late-Zhou cosmological milieu and mutual conversation**, with the "school" labels being retrospective Han classifications.

## 4. ADVERSARIAL FACT-CHECK

Verdicts use the original Chinese plus Giles (1910) and Griffith (1963). Verbatim quotes are given so the writer can rely on them.

### a) "To subdue the enemy without fighting is the acme of skill" is the book's central, top-ranked ideal

**VERDICT: TRUE, with one nuance.** The passage is real, prominent, and explicitly framed as the highest tier. Chapter 3 ("Attack by Stratagem" / 謀攻), Giles:
- §2: "to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting." (Chinese 不戰而屈人之兵; the popular "to subdue the enemy without fighting is the acme of skill" is the same verse in standard editions.)
- §3, the explicit ranking: "the highest form of generalship is to balk the enemy's plans; the next best is to prevent the junction of the enemy's forces; the next in order is to attack the enemy's army in the field; and the worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities." (伐謀 > 伐交 > 伐兵 > 攻城.)

So the post's framing is accurate: it is the stated #1 ideal, with the descending ranking (plans > alliances > army > walled cities) exactly as described. **Nuance to keep honest:** this is the *ideal ceiling*, not the bulk of the treatise. The other chapters are concrete operational doctrine. Fair to call it the book's stated supreme principle; a stretch to imply the book is mostly about avoiding combat. Sun Tzu's broader thesis is to win *cheaply and decisively* (prolonged war ruins the state, Ch. 2), with bloodless subjugation as the perfect-case extreme. ([suntzusaid ch.3](https://suntzusaid.com/book/3))

### b) "All warfare is based on deception" is genuinely in Chapter 1 and central

**VERDICT: TRUE.** Chapter 1 ("Laying Plans" / 始計), Giles §18: "All warfare is based on deception." (Chinese 兵者，詭道也.) The deceptions follow immediately (§§19 to 24): "when able to attack, we must seem unable... when we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away... Hold out baits to entice the enemy. Feign disorder, and crush him... Attack him where he is unprepared, appear where you are not expected." No overstatement. ([suntzusaid ch.1](https://suntzusaid.com/book/1)) (See Section 3.3 for the translation nuance on 詭道.)

### c) "Know the enemy and know yourself" and the common misquote

**VERDICT on the genuine passage and three-part structure: TRUE. VERDICT on the popular "you will WIN a hundred battles" paraphrase: FALSE (a real, meaningful inflation the post is right to flag).**

The authentic passage is the last verse of Chapter 3, Giles: "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle." The three-part structure (both = no peril; self-only = one win one loss; neither = certain defeat) is exactly right.

**The misquote is the load-bearing finding.** The Chinese is 知彼知己，百戰不殆 (zhī bǐ zhī jǐ, bǎi zhàn bù dài). 不殆 (bù dài) means "**not endangered / not imperiled / will not be in peril**," literally "hundred battles, no danger." It does NOT mean "win." Griffith confirms with even sharper wording: "if you know your enemies and know yourself, you will **not be imperiled** in a hundred battles; if you do not know your enemies but do know yourself, you will win one and lose one; if you do not know your enemies nor yourself, you will be imperiled in every single battle." Even the full-knowledge reward is only "not imperiled," never "you will win." The popular "know your enemy and know yourself and you will win a hundred battles" swaps *non-defeat* for *victory*. Frame the post around "you will not be in danger / will never be defeated," and explicitly call out the "you will win" version as an inflation (不殆 = no peril, not 百勝 = a hundred victories). ([suntzusaid ch.3](https://suntzusaid.com/book/3); [Wikiquote: Sun Tzu](https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Sun_Tzu); [yellowbridge ch.3](https://www.yellowbridge.com/onlinelit/artofwar03.php))

### d) The book teaches winning BEFORE the battle (win first, then fight; temple calculations)

**VERDICT: TRUE.** Two independent supports. Chapter 4 ("Tactical Dispositions" / 軍形), Giles §15: "In war the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory." (Chinese 勝兵先勝而後求戰，敗兵先戰而後求勝; the popular "victorious warriors win first and then go to war" is Cleary's phrasing of this verse.) Supported by §1: "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy." And Chapter 1's temple-calculation passage (§26, see Section 3.6) makes the decisive work happen before contact. "Win first, then fight" is a fair, well-supported reading. ([suntzusaid ch.4](https://suntzusaid.com/book/4); [ctext 軍形](https://ctext.org/art-of-war/tactical-dispositions/zh))

### e) "Be like water / formless" is really in Chapter 6

**VERDICT: TRUE.** Chapter 6 ("Weak Points and Strong" / 虛實), Giles: §29 "Military tactics are like unto water; for water in its natural course runs away from high places and hastens downwards. So in war, the way is to avoid what is strong and to strike at what is weak." §31 "Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing." §32 "just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions." The formlessness (無形) ideal is also genuinely in this chapter (Griffith: "to be without ascertainable shape"; Chinese 至於無形). Both elements correctly located in Chapter 6. ([suntzusaid ch.6](https://suntzusaid.com/book/6))
> Precision note: the cleanest formlessness citation is the 至於無形 verse (Griffith "without ascertainable shape"), not Giles's §9 "divine art of subtlety and secrecy... invisible... inaudible," which is also Ch. 6 but looser.

### f) "The bible of business / sport strategy" and the Western application boom

**VERDICT: TRUE as a real modern phenomenon; OVERSTATED as a claim of fidelity to the text.** The timeline is accurate.

- **1963, Griffith / Oxford / UNESCO.** Griffith's translation (Clarendon Press / Oxford UP, 1963), foreword by **B.H. Liddell Hart**, published in the **UNESCO Collection of Representative Works**. This put Sun Tzu in serious Western military and academic circles; effectively required reading for US officers since the Cold War. ([JRAS review, Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-the-royal-asiatic-society/article/abs/sun-tzu-the-art-of-war-by-samuel-b-griffith-with-a-foreword-by-b-h-liddell-hart-pp-vii-195-clarendon-press-oxford-university-press-1963-35s/6450D1E71CBB08C7863F7DA4AD8BDEDF))
- **1980s business boom.** *Wall Street* (1987): Gordon Gekko's verbatim line is "I don't throw darts at a board. I bet on sure things. Read Sun-tzu, *The Art of War*. Every battle is won *before* it is ever fought." (That last clause is Gekko's compression of the Chapter 4 idea, NOT a literal Sun Tzu sentence; flag it if quoted.) The book became the "corporate raider's bible"; venture capitalist Asher Edelman taught a late-1980s Columbia course informally dubbed "Corporate Raiding: The Art of War"; Japanese-management literature of the 1980s invoked it and "some Japanese companies make the book required reading for their key executives." ([IMDb: Wall Street quotes](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0094291/quotes/); [Wikipedia: The Art of War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))
- **1988, Cleary / Shambhala.** Cleary's translation became the popular mass-market bestseller that rode and fed the wave. ([Shambhala](https://www.shambhala.com/the-art-of-war-158.html))
- **Sports.** NFL coach **Bill Belichick** has repeatedly credited the book (the Patriots' "Every battle is won before it is fought"). Brazil's 2002 World Cup coach **Luiz Felipe Scolari** used it, sliding passages under players' doors. ([CNBC on Belichick](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/01/31/bill-belichick-uses-this-sun-tzu-quote-to-inspire-the-patriots-to-win.html); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Art_of_War))

**Fair verdict on fidelity.** The text genuinely contains transferable principles (know yourself and your competitor, win cheaply, avoid attrition, exploit weakness, prepare before committing, prize position over brute force), so the application is not pure hype. But a large fraction of the book is concrete ancient battlefield logistics that does not transfer (chariot costs, feeding an army, fire attacks on granaries, fording rivers, running spies). The "bible of business" label is itself a **modern, largely 1980s-marketing construction** amplified by *Wall Street* and a flood of "Sun Tzu for [X]" derivative books, presented descriptively (e.g. by Wikipedia) without scholarly endorsement of its rigor. Honest framing: root the book in its real Warring States context first, then note the modern application as a real phenomenon that is faithful in spirit and a stretch in the literal details.

## 5. THE 13 CHAPTERS

Title characters verified against the UCSD scholarly edition (D.K. Jordan), Chinese Wikipedia, and the Giles text. Chapters 1, 4, 5 have both long and short traditional names; the longer form with 篇 (*piān*, "chapter") is the conventional heading.

| # | Chinese | Pinyin | Common English title(s) | One-line content |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 始計 / 計 | Shǐ jì / Jì | Laying Plans (Giles) / Estimates (Griffith) / Calculations (Ames) | The five factors (Way, Heaven, Earth, Command, Method) and seven considerations; "all warfare is deception"; the temple calculations. |
| 2 | 作戰 | Zuòzhàn | Waging War / Doing Battle (Ames) | The economics and ruinous **cost** of prolonged war; provisioning; forage off the enemy. |
| 3 | 謀攻 | Móugōng | Attack by Stratagem (Giles) / Offensive Strategy (Griffith) | Subdue the enemy without fighting; the hierarchy of attack (plans > alliances > army > cities); "know the enemy and know yourself." |
| 4 | 軍形 / 形 | Jūnxíng / Xíng | Tactical Dispositions (Giles) / Dispositions (Griffith) / Form | Defense vs. offense; secure yourself first; the victorious army wins first, then seeks battle. |
| 5 | 兵勢 / 勢 | Bīngshì / Shì | Energy (Giles/Griffith) / Force (Cleary) / Configuration (Mair) | **Shi/momentum**; direct (正 zhèng) and indirect (奇 qí) forces; torrent, drawn crossbow, round stone down a mountain. |
| 6 | 虛實 | Xūshí | Weak Points and Strong (Giles) / Emptiness and Fullness (Cleary) | Attack weakness (虛), avoid strength (實); make the enemy come to you; the **water/formlessness** passage. |
| 7 | 軍爭 | Jūnzhēng | Maneuvering (Giles) / Maneuver (Griffith) / Armed Struggle (Ames) | The difficulty of maneuver; deception in movement; the wind/forest/fire/mountain lines (later 風林火山 *Fūrinkazan*). |
| 8 | 九變 | Jiǔbiàn | Variation of Tactics (Giles) / The Nine Variables (Griffith) | Flexibility and adaptation (roads not to follow, orders not to obey); the five dangerous faults of a general. (Textually disordered in transmission.) |
| 9 | 行軍 | Xíngjūn | The Army on the March (Giles) / Marches (Griffith) | A long **catalogue of observable signs** (dust columns, bird flight, soldiers' posture) for reading terrain and the enemy; camping advice. |
| 10 | 地形 | Dìxíng | Terrain (Giles/Griffith) / Configurations of Terrain (Ames) | **Six types of ground**; six ways an army is ruined through the general's fault; the general may disobey the ruler; "regard your soldiers as your children." |
| 11 | 九地 | Jiǔdì | The Nine Situations (Giles) / The Nine Varieties of Ground (Griffith) | **Nine kinds of ground** (dispersive to death ground); put troops where they must fight or die; the longest, most discursive chapter. |
| 12 | 火攻 | Huǒgōng | The Attack by Fire (Giles) / Attack by Fire (Griffith) | The **five ways of fire attack** and their conditions; the warning against acting in **anger** ("no ruler should put troops into the field merely to gratify his own spleen"). |
| 13 | 用間 | Yòngjiān | The Use of Spies (Giles) / Employment of Secret Agents (Griffith) | The supreme value of **foreknowledge** from spies; the **five classes of spy** (local, internal, converted/double, doomed, surviving); the converted spy is most prized. |

(Chapter texts: [suntzusaid.com](https://suntzusaid.com/artofwar.php); titles cross-checked at [UCSD/D.K. Jordan](https://pages.ucsd.edu/~dkjordan/chin/Suentzyy/Suentzyy00.html).)

### The book is mostly a field manual, not aphorisms (VERIFIED, and the post should say so)

The quotable epigrams cluster in **chapters 1 and 3 to 6** (subdue without fighting, know yourself, all warfare is deception, water/formlessness, shi). Everything from **Chapter 7 onward, plus Chapter 2, is overwhelmingly concrete operational doctrine**: war economics and provisioning (2), the mechanics of maneuver and forced marches (7), contingency rules and a general's faults (8), a literal list of dozens of battlefield signs (9), taxonomies of terrain (10, 11), the five techniques and weather/timing of fire attack (12), and a typology of five spy types and how to run them (13). So **roughly seven of thirteen chapters (2 and 7 to 13), more than half the book, are field-manual material.** The "fortune-cookie" reputation reflects heavy anthologizing of a small slice. A curated "keystone passages" post is honest only if it tells the reader it is selecting, and that the bulk is ancient logistics about killing people efficiently in 5th-to-4th-century-BCE China.

> **Contested/naming flags for the chapter list:**
> 1. Chapters 1, 4, 5 have two-character and one-character names (始計/計, 軍形/形, 兵勢/勢); both legitimate, longer forms safest.
> 2. 九 = "nine" vs. "many": translators split on 九變 (Ch. 8). Ch. 11's nine grounds *are* enumerated, so "nine" is literal there; Ch. 8's "nine" is the contested one.
> 3. Ch. 11 is standardly called the longest; one Giles-edition note frames it as "double any other except Chapter 9." Say "the longest chapter," not a specific multiple.
> 4. *Fūrinkazan* (風林火山) is a later Japanese condensation of Ch. 7's lines (Takeda Shingen's banner, popularized further by a 1953 Yasushi Inoue novel), not four characters in Sun Tzu's own text. Say "the lines later condensed into *Fūrinkazan*." ([Wikipedia: Fūrinkazan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C5%ABrinkazan))

## 6. BEST EDITIONS / TRANSLATIONS

Scholar-respected tier: **Griffith, Ames, Denma, Mair** (plus Giles as the venerable public-domain standard). Cleary is the commercial best-seller, sometimes faulted by sinologists. Minford is the literary one.

- **Lionel Giles (1910), *Sun Tzŭ on the Art of War* (London: Luzac & Co.).** First fully annotated scholarly English translation and the de-facto **standard reference for a century**; still the most-quoted because it is **public domain** (hence everywhere online: Project Gutenberg, suntzusaid.com). Giles explicitly corrected the error-ridden earlier translation of **Everard Calthrop** (1905/1908), whose "omissions were frequent; hard passages were wilfully distorted." Based on the **received text** (the bamboo strips were undiscovered until 1972). Language dated but reliable. ([Project Gutenberg](https://www.gutenberg.org/files/132/132-h/132-h.htm); [Wikipedia: Lionel Giles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lionel_Giles))
- **Samuel B. Griffith (1963), *Sun Tzu: The Art of War* (Oxford: Clarendon Press / OUP).** The "**soldier's translation**," by a US Marine **brigadier general** and Chinese-studies PhD; foreword by **B.H. Liddell Hart**; in the **UNESCO Collection of Representative Works**. For decades the standard modern scholarly-military translation, with strong supplementary essays (Sun Tzu in Japan, on Mao). Scholar-respected. ([Oxford UP](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-art-of-war-9780195014761))
- **Thomas Cleary (1988), *The Art of War* (Boston: Shambhala).** The **best-selling popular translation**, prized for accessibility and for presenting the classical **commentarial tradition** (the eleven commentators) within a **Taoist/Buddhist framing**. Commercially enormous. **Criticized by some sinologists** for the spiritualizing/interpretive framing and occasional loose renderings (the "headquarters" choice for 廟算 is an example of de-ritualizing). ([Shambhala](https://www.shambhala.com/the-art-of-war-158.html)) [Note: the named-scholar takedown is a general reservation, not pinned to one critic here; characterize accordingly.]
- **Roger T. Ames (1993), *Sun-Tzu: The Art of Warfare* (New York: Ballantine).** The **scholarly translation built on the 1972 Yinqueshan bamboo strips**, including the **recovered supplemental material** and a substantial **philosophical introduction** (shi, classical Chinese thought). Ames, a leading comparative philosopher, is **highly respected academically**. ([Penguin Random House](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/3172/sun-tzu-the-art-of-warfare-by-roger-t-ames/))
- **Denma Translation Group (2001), *The Art of War: The Denma Translation* (Boston: Shambhala).** A **collaborative translation prizing fidelity to the original's terseness/compression**, with extensive essays, line-by-line commentary, and notes on key terms (it keeps *shi* untranslated). **Respected for fidelity**; even Mair praised its translation principles. ([Shambhala](https://www.shambhala.com/the-art-of-war-156.html); [Sonshi interview](https://www.sonshi.com/denma-translation-group-interview.html))
- **John Minford (2002), *The Art of War* (New York: Viking / Penguin Classics).** A **literary translation by a major sinologist** (co-translator of *The Story of the Stone*, translator of the *I Ching* and *Tao Te Ching*). Praised for **readability and literary quality** while staying close to the terse original format, with layered commentary. ([Wikipedia: John Minford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Minford)) (First US hardcover Viking 2002; Penguin Classics in paperback.)
- **Victor H. Mair (2007), *The Art of War: Sun Zi's Military Methods* (New York: Columbia UP).** A **philological, deliberately iconoclastic** translation by a leading sinologist, with an introduction **skeptical of traditional claims**: argues Sun Wu "never existed" (appendix "The Pseudo-Biography of Sun Wu"), dates the text as a composite c. mid-4th to early-3rd century BCE, and renders the title "**Military Methods**" (兵法) rather than "Art of War." **Respected academically** (its revisionism debated). ([Columbia UP](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/the-art-of-war/9780231133821/); [H-Net review](https://networks.h-net.org/node/12840/reviews/13403/goldschmidt-mair-art-war-sun-zis-military-methods-translations-asian))
