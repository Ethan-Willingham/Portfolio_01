# Meditation Dossier 02: Mantra, Insight, and Zen

Research for the "Meditation" great-books post. Three techniques, each mapped on five
axes, with the **HOW-TO** as the deepest layer. Evidence is flagged only at the headline
level (a separate agent owns the rigorous evidence synthesis). URLs cited throughout.

Techniques covered:
- A. Transcendental Meditation (TM) / mantra meditation
- B. Vipassana / insight meditation (Satipatthana Sutta + Goenka)
- C. Zazen (Zen sitting)

---

## A. TRANSCENDENTAL MEDITATION (TM) / mantra meditation

### Load-bearing line
> "Water the root to enjoy the fruit." — Maharishi Mahesh Yogi

The whole TM pitch in one image: don't fuss over the leaves (behavior, stress, performance);
nourish the source (consciousness) and the rest follows.
(https://quotefancy.com/quote/1478309/Maharishi-Mahesh-Yogi-Water-the-root-to-enjoy-the-fruit)

### 1. What it is (one plain mechanism sentence)
You sit with eyes closed and silently, effortlessly repeat a meaningless sound (a mantra)
for about 20 minutes; the sound is a vehicle that lets the mind settle inward on its own
until thought quiets, rather than something you concentrate on.
(https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22292-transcendental-meditation)

### 2. Where it came from
- Developed by **Maharishi Mahesh Yogi** (an Indian teacher), who began teaching it in the
  **1950s** and brought it West through the 1960s-70s, presenting it as a secular,
  systematized distillation of older Vedic/yogic mantra practice.
  (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_Meditation;
  https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22292-transcendental-meditation)
- His foundational book, **"Science of Being and Art of Living"** (first published **1963**),
  remains the standard introduction; it has sold well over a million copies.
  (https://meditationlifestyle.com/science-of-being-art-living-maharishi/)
- **The Beatles connection** is what made TM a household word. George, Paul, and John
  attended a Maharishi lecture in London in summer **1967**; in February **1968** all four
  Beatles traveled to his ashram in **Rishikesh, India** for a TM teacher-training course
  (the same retreat where much of the White Album was written). The Beach Boys and others
  followed. This pop-culture moment turned TM from a niche import into a global brand.
  (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Beatles_in_India;
  https://www.beatlesbible.com/people/maharishi-mahesh-yogi/)

### 3. What it claims (the tradition's own claims)
- The practice produces **"transcending"**: the mind settles "inward into a quiet, deeply
  restful state, while you're still fully awake," reaching what the organization calls
  **"pure consciousness, a unique state of deep rest."** (official TM org, per fetch of
  https://www.tm.org/en-us)
- This state is described as **"restful alertness"**: body deeply rested, mind awake and
  clear at the same time.
- It is marketed as **effortless and non-religious**: "No concentration or effort is
  required," the settling "happens automatically through the TM technique." Claimed downstream
  benefits include stress and anxiety reduction, better sleep, focus, and "self-development /
  higher states of consciousness."
  (https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22292-transcendental-meditation)

### 4. What the evidence shows (headline only)
Headline: TM has a genuine, fairly large research literature (much of it produced by
TM-affiliated institutions, which is a known limitation) showing modest reductions in stress,
anxiety, and blood pressure. Independent reviews (e.g., Cochrane-style and AHRQ) tend to find
TM is **about as effective as other relaxation/meditation methods, with no strong evidence it
is uniquely superior** once study quality and conflicts of interest are accounted for. (Flag
for the evidence agent; do not over-claim the "scientifically proven unique state.")
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_Meditation)

### 5. HOW TO DO THE CORE OF IT  (most important section)

**Important honesty note for the post.** TM proper is **trademarked and taught only through a
paid, multi-session course with a certified teacher**, who assigns you a "personal" mantra in
a brief ceremony (puja). The org's standard format is roughly **four sessions: one private
one-on-one instruction, then three guided follow-up sessions**, plus lifetime check-ins.
(official TM org, per fetch of https://www.tm.org/en-us)

**Cost.** In the US the one-time course fee is **tiered by household income, roughly $420 to
$980** as of 2024-2025 (commonly cited: ~$960 single adult, ~$720 second person in a couple,
~$480 full-time student; grants/payment plans for hardship).
(https://meditationlifestyle.com/transcendental-meditation-cost/;
https://www.thumbtack.com/p/transcendental-meditation-cost)

That said, the **actual mechanics are simple and openly documented.** Here is the real core
instruction, and a free generic version a reader can use today.

**The TM core practice (what actually happens in the 20 minutes):**
1. **Posture.** Sit comfortably in a chair (or anywhere you can sit upright but relaxed),
   back supported, hands in your lap, feet flat. No special posture is required, no lotus.
2. **Eyes.** Close your eyes. Sit quietly for a few seconds first.
3. **The mantra.** Begin to **silently repeat your mantra** (TM uses short, meaningless
   Sanskrit-derived sounds). The repetition is mental, not spoken or mouthed.
4. **Effortlessness is the whole game.** Do **not** concentrate on the mantra, force it, or
   hold a rhythm. Let it be faint, vague, even change in speed or clarity. It should feel
   "easy, like a casual thought." The mantra is a thread the attention follows inward; if it
   fades or you forget it, that is fine, that is the mind settling.
5. **When the mind wanders** (and it will, constantly): the moment you **notice** you've
   drifted into ordinary thoughts (planning, memories) or that the mantra has slipped away,
   **gently, without judgment, come back to the mantra.** No effort, no annoyance. Drift,
   notice, return: that cycle *is* the practice.
6. **Duration.** **About 20 minutes, twice a day** (classically morning before breakfast and
   late afternoon/early evening before dinner). Beginners often start with ~10 minutes.
   (https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/22292-transcendental-meditation)
7. **Coming out.** Stop repeating the mantra and **sit with eyes closed for 2-3 minutes**
   before opening them and standing up. Rushing out is the classic comfort-breaking mistake.

**A free generic mantra meditation a reader can actually do:**
You do not need a secret mantra to get the core effect. Pick a short, neutral, meaningless
sound. Herbert Benson (Harvard) showed in *The Relaxation Response* that a plain word like
**"one"** works; many traditions use a soft hum like **"so-hum"** or simply a meaningless
syllable like **"aim"** or **"shi-rim."** Then follow the exact same steps above: sit
comfortably, eyes closed, repeat the sound silently and effortlessly, and whenever you notice
you've wandered, gently return to it, ~20 minutes. The point is a soft, repeating, meaning-free
anchor, not which syllable you chose.

**The two most common mistakes:**
- **Trying too hard / concentrating.** TM is not focused attention. If you're "gripping" the
  mantra or straining to keep it clear, you're doing a different (harder) practice. Loosen.
- **Treating wandering as failure.** Noticing you drifted and returning is not an interruption
  of meditation; it is the rep. People quit because they think a busy mind means they "can't
  meditate."

## B. VIPASSANA / INSIGHT MEDITATION

Two threads: the **source text** (Satipatthana Sutta), and the dominant **modern lay method**
(S.N. Goenka, in the U Ba Khin lineage). They share DNA but differ in emphasis.

### Load-bearing line
> "This is the only way [*ekayano maggo*], monks, for the purification of beings... for the
> attainment of Nibbana, namely, the four foundations of mindfulness."
> — Satipatthana Sutta, opening declaration (trans. Nyanasatta Thera)
> (https://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/mn/mn.010.nysa.html)

And the principle that drives the Goenka method:
> **Anicca** (impermanence): everything you observe is "by nature arising and passing away."

---

### B1. THE SOURCE TEXT: the Satipatthana Sutta (MN 10 / DN 22)

#### 1. What it is
The **Satipatthana Sutta** ("Discourse on the Establishing of Mindfulness") is the Buddha's
foundational teaching on **mindfulness practice**, laying out **four domains to contemplate**:
the body, feelings (feeling-tones), the mind/consciousness, and *dhammas* (mental objects /
categories of experience). It exists in two near-identical versions, **MN 10** (Majjhima
Nikaya) and the longer **DN 22** (Digha Nikaya, the *Maha*satipatthana Sutta).
(https://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/mn/mn.010.nysa.html;
https://www.suttas.com/mn-10-satipatthana-sutta-the-foundations-of-mindfulness.html)

#### 2. Where it came from
Attributed directly to the **Buddha** (~5th c. BCE), preserved in the **Pali Canon**. It is one
of the most commented-on texts in Buddhism and the root source nearly every modern "insight"
and secular-mindfulness program traces back to. Translations cited here: **Nyanasatta Thera**
and **Thanissaro Bhikkhu**. (https://www.dhammatalks.org/suttas/MN/MN10.html)

#### 3. What it claims
That establishing mindfulness across these four foundations is **the direct path** to
purification and to *Nibbana* (liberation). The sutta's striking guarantee: anyone who
develops the four foundations diligently can expect, even within seven years (and in some
passages, seven days), either final knowledge here-and-now or the state of non-return.
(https://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/mn/mn.010.nysa.html)

#### 4. What the evidence shows (headline only)
Headline: this is the textual taproot of the modern mindfulness movement (MBSR, MBCT). The
*secularized* descendants have the strongest evidence (moderate benefit for stress, anxiety,
recurrent depression). The sutta itself is a religious/contemplative text, not an empirical
claim. (Flag for evidence agent.)

#### 5. HOW-TO (what the sutta actually instructs)
The sutta is unusually concrete. The **four foundations**, in order:

> "a monk lives **contemplating the body in the body**... he lives **contemplating feelings in
> feelings**... he lives **contemplating consciousness in consciousness**... he lives
> **contemplating mental objects in mental objects**." (trans. Nyanasatta Thera)

The **first and most practical exercise is mindfulness of breathing** (the root of both modern
breath-focus and body-scan methods). The instruction verbatim:

> "Ever mindful he breathes in, mindful he breathes out. **Breathing in a long breath, he
> knows, 'I am breathing in a long breath'**; ... breathing in a short breath, he knows, 'I am
> breathing in a short breath'..."

> "**'Experiencing the whole (breath-) body, I shall breathe in,'** thus he trains himself...
> **'Calming the activity of the (breath-) body, I shall breathe in,'** thus he trains himself."
> (trans. Nyanasatta Thera, https://www.accesstoinsight.org/tipitaka/mn/mn.010.nysa.html)

A beginner can practice this directly: **sit, breathe naturally, and simply *know* each breath
as it happens** (long when long, short when short), without controlling it; then broaden to feel
the whole sweep of the breath; then notice it growing calm. The body section continues into the
famous **postures exercise** (knowing "I am walking / standing / sitting / lying down"), clear
comprehension of activities, **the parts-of-the-body contemplation** (the 31/32 parts, the
seed of body-scanning), the four elements, and the charnel-ground contemplations. Feelings =
noticing each feeling-tone as **pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral**. Mind = noting the mind's
state (e.g., "mind with lust / without lust, contracted / distracted"). Dhammas = the five
hindrances, five aggregates, six sense-bases, seven factors of awakening, four noble truths.

**Common mistake (for the source practice):** turning "knowing the breath" into *controlling*
the breath. The instruction is to *observe and know*, not to deepen or pace it.

### B2. THE MODERN METHOD: S.N. Goenka's Vipassana (10-day course)

#### 1. What it is
A two-stage technique: **first sharpen attention by watching the natural breath at the
nostrils (Anapana), then systematically scan bodily sensations from head to feet and back
(body-sweeping), observing every sensation with perfect equanimity** so you experience
directly, in your own body, that all sensations arise and pass (anicca).
(https://www.vridhamma.org/10-Day-Courses)

#### 2. Where it came from (lineage)
A specific Burmese lay lineage, presented by Goenka as exactly what the Buddha taught:
- **Ledi Sayadaw** (1846-1923), Burmese monk-scholar who helped revive Vipassana for lay people.
- **Saya Thetgyi** (Saya Thet), a layman who studied 14 years under Ledi Sayadaw and taught
  thousands.
- **Sayagyi U Ba Khin** (1899-1971), Saya Thetgyi's student and the **first Accountant General
  of independent Burma**; founded the International Meditation Centre, Rangoon (1952).
- **S.N. Goenka** (1924-2013), Burmese-Indian businessman who took his first course under U Ba
  Khin in **1955**, studied 14 years, and in **1969** moved to India to begin teaching, founding
  the global network of centers (dhamma.org / Vipassana Research Institute).
  (https://www.vridhamma.org/Sayagyi-U-Ba-Khin;
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S._N._Goenka;
  https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sayagyi_U_Ba_Khin)
When U Ba Khin died, Goenka cabled the Pali verse: **"Aniccā vata saṅkhārā..."** ("Impermanent
truly are compounded things, by nature arising and passing away; if they arise and are
extinguished, their eradication brings happiness.") That verse is the lineage's heartbeat.

#### 3. What it claims
That by observing sensation with equanimity you **dissolve deep-seated reactive habits
(sankharas) of craving and aversion at their physical root**, gaining direct experiential
insight into **anicca (impermanence), dukkha (unsatisfactoriness), and anatta (non-self)**, and
moving toward liberation. The method is taught as **non-sectarian and universal** ("the art of
living"), not as conversion to Buddhism. (https://www.vridhamma.org/10-Day-Courses)

#### 4. What the evidence shows (headline only)
Headline: the 10-day course produces large reported well-being gains; there is real but
methodologically mixed research (including studies in prisons). The intensity (10 days, ~10h/day
silent) also carries documented risk of adverse experiences for a minority. (Flag for evidence
agent.)

#### 5. HOW-TO (the real core, both stages)

**Course frame.** A **10-day residential course**, taught free: per the official code,
**"There are no charges for the courses, not even to cover the cost of food and
accommodation,"** funded entirely by **voluntary donations from past students.** Students keep
**Noble Silence** (no talking, eye contact, gestures, phones, reading, or writing) for the first
~9 days, and keep five precepts (no killing, stealing, sexual activity, lying, or intoxicants).
Roughly **10 hours of meditation a day**; technique is delivered via **Goenka's recorded audio
instructions**, with an evening recorded **discourse** each day. New students must complete a
full course to learn the technique (you can't half-do it remotely).
(https://www.vridhamma.org/10-Day-Courses)

**STAGE 1: ANAPANA (Days 1-3) - sharpening attention.**
1. **Posture.** Sit comfortably, legs crossed on a cushion or in a chair, back and neck erect
   but not stiff, **eyes closed**, hands resting.
2. **Object.** Fix attention on the **natural breath as it enters and leaves the nostrils**,
   "the natural reality of the ever changing flow of breath." Just **observe** it; do **not**
   regulate, deepen, or count it (this is bare observation, not pranayama).
3. **Narrowing.** Over the days the zone of attention is narrowed to the **small triangle
   below the nostrils and above the upper lip**, and you begin to feel **subtle natural
   sensations** there (warmth, tingling, touch of air). This trains a sharp, stable, sensitive
   attention.
4. **When the mind wanders:** the instant you realize the mind has run off, **calmly, without
   frustration, bring it back** to the breath/sensation at the nostrils. Again and again.

**STAGE 2: VIPASSANA / body-sweeping (Days 4-9) - the heart of the method.**
1. With attention now sharp, you **move it systematically through the entire body**, typically
   **from the top of the head, part by part, down to the tips of the toes, then back up**, in an
   orderly sweep (and later free-flowing).
2. In each small area you **observe whatever sensation is present**, gross or subtle: heat,
   cold, tingling, pulsing, pressure, itching, pain, numbness, vibration, or even a blank/no
   clear sensation (which you simply note and move past).
3. **The cardinal instruction is EQUANIMITY** (*upekkha*): **do not react.** Do not crave or
   chase pleasant sensations; do not resist or recoil from unpleasant ones (including pain from
   long sitting). You **just observe each sensation and let it be**, understanding it as
   impermanent. (https://www.vridhamma.org/10-Day-Courses)
4. **The insight (anicca).** By watching sensations arise and dissolve while staying neutral,
   you experience for yourself that **everything is in flux** ("arising and passing away"). The
   equanimity, not the sensations, is the point: you are training the mind to stop reacting.
5. **Day 10:** Noble Silence ends, plus **Metta Bhavana** (loving-kindness) to share the
   practice's goodwill before returning to the world.

**The two most common mistakes (Goenka method):**
- **Reacting to the sensations** instead of observing them: getting excited by pleasant
  "free-flow" vibration (craving) or fighting/dreading pain and numbness (aversion). Either way
  you've turned the practice into the very reactive habit it's meant to dissolve.
- **Forcing or "looking for" a particular sensation.** You observe whatever is actually there,
  including subtle or absent sensation; straining to manufacture tingling defeats the bare
  observation.

## C. ZAZEN (Zen sitting)

### Load-bearing line
> "Think of not-thinking. How do you think of not-thinking? **Non-thinking.** This in itself is
> the essential art of zazen." — Dogen, *Fukanzazengi*
> (https://thedewdrop.org/2020/03/09/fukanzazengi-dogen/)

Dogen also calls zazen **"the dharma-gate of repose and bliss, the practice-realization of
totally culminated enlightenment."** (same source)

### 1. What it is (one plain mechanism sentence)
**Zazen ("seated Zen") is upright, alert sitting in a precise posture in which you don't
manipulate the mind at all; in the Soto style you "just sit" (shikantaza), letting thoughts
come and go without following or fighting them, and in the Rinzai style you sit while
relentlessly investigating a koan, while beginners in both schools often start by counting
breaths.**
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shikantaza; https://www.lionsroar.com/zazen-just-sitting-going-nowhere/)

### 2. Where it came from
- **Chan Buddhism** (China) crossing into **Zen** (Japan). Two main streams:
  - **Soto Zen**, brought from China and codified in Japan by **Eihei Dogen** (1200-1253), who
    centered everything on **shikantaza, "just sitting,"** which he learned under his Chinese
    teacher **Rujing** ("Just sit; only then will you attain it"). Dogen's brief manual
    **Fukanzazengi** ("Universally Recommended Instructions for Zazen") is the canonical how-to.
    (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shikantaza)
  - **Rinzai Zen**, the **koan** stream, revitalized in Japan by **Hakuin Ekaku** (1686-1769),
    who systematized koan curriculum and devised **"the sound of one hand."**
    (https://www.newworldencyclopedia.org/entry/Hakuin)

### 3. What it claims
- **Soto / Dogen:** zazen is **not a means to a later enlightenment but enlightenment enacted
  now** ("practice-realization"); sitting in the posture *is* awakening, not a technique for
  producing it. (https://thedewdrop.org/2020/03/09/fukanzazengi-dogen/)
- **Rinzai / koan:** sustained "Great Doubt" (*daigi*) on a koan builds to a sudden
  breakthrough, **kensho** (a direct seeing into one's true nature), which is then deepened
  through successive koans. (https://www.newworldencyclopedia.org/entry/Hakuin)

### 4. What the evidence shows (headline only)
Headline: Zen meditators show the expected attention/EEG signatures of long-term practice;
high-quality clinical trials specifically on zazen (as opposed to MBSR) are sparse, partly
because Zen frames sitting as awakening itself, not as a health intervention. (Flag for
evidence agent.)

### 5. HOW-TO (the actual posture and three methods)

This is where zazen is most exacting: the **form is the practice**, so posture gets unusual
attention. Dogen's instructions below are verbatim from *Fukanzazengi*.

**POSTURE (shared by all zazen):**
1. **Seat / legs.** Sit on a firm cushion (a *zafu*) on a mat. Dogen: spread "thick matting and
   place a cushion above it," then **"Sit either in the full-lotus or half-lotus position. In
   the full-lotus position, you first place your right foot on your left thigh and your left
   foot on your right thigh."** Half-lotus: **"you simply press your left foot against your
   right thigh."** Modern centers also accept **Burmese** (both feet flat on the mat, neither
   stacked), **seiza** (kneeling, often astride a cushion or bench), or **a chair** (feet flat,
   sit forward off the backrest). The aim of every variant is a **stable tripod** (two knees +
   seat) and an erect, self-supporting spine.
2. **Spine and head.** Sit **"upright in correct bodily posture, neither inclining to the left
   nor to the right, neither leaning forward nor backward."** Lift the crown as if pushing the
   sky; tuck the chin slightly; ears over shoulders, nose over navel.
3. **Hands (the cosmic mudra).** **"Place your right hand on your left leg [palm up] and your
   left palm (facing upward) on your right palm, thumb-tips touching."** The thumbs barely
   touch, forming a flat oval; hands rest against the lower belly. (A collapsing or pressing
   thumb-line is a built-in feedback gauge for drowsiness/tension.)
4. **Mouth and tongue.** **"Place your tongue against the front roof of your mouth, with teeth
   and lips both shut."** Breathe through the nose.
5. **Eyes.** **"Your eyes should always remain open"** (Dogen) but **lowered to about a
   45-degree angle, gaze resting softly on the floor roughly two to three feet ahead**,
   unfocused. Half-open eyes (Japanese *hangan*) keep you out of both dreamy drowsiness (closed)
   and distraction (wide open): "you must not shut your eyes."
   (https://www.themathesontrust.org/library/susokukan-zen-counting)
6. **Settling in.** Dogen: take a breath, then **"rock your body right and left and settle into
   a steady, immovable sitting position"** (a few decreasing side-to-side sways to find your
   center).
7. **Breath.** Let breathing be **natural, quiet, and through the nose**, settling low into the
   belly (*hara*). You do not force it.

**METHOD 1 - BREATH COUNTING (susokukan): the standard beginner entry, both schools.**
- Breathe naturally and **count each breath silently, usually on the exhale: "one" on the first
  out-breath, "two" on the next, up to "ten," then start again at "one."** (Some teachers count
  both in- and out-breaths.) Let exhalations be long and unforced.
  (https://www.themathesontrust.org/papers/buddhism/tatsuta-susokukan.pdf;
  http://nondualcafe.blogspot.com/2015/07/zen-zazen-1-breath-counting-1.html)
- **When the mind wanders** (or you "wake up" at "seventeen," or lose the count): note it
  without judgment and **go back to "one."** Returning to one is not punishment; it is the rep.
- Counting trains continuous attention; many practitioners later drop the numbers and just
  follow the breath, then move to shikantaza.

**METHOD 2 - SHIKANTAZA ("just sitting"): the heart of Soto practice.**
- **No object at all.** No mantra, no counting, no breath-focus, no koan, no goal. You **simply
  sit in the posture in open, alert awareness.**
- **Thoughts come and go; you neither chase them nor push them away.** As one teacher puts it,
  "Thoughts come, thoughts go, and you simply watch them"; the mind is "aware yet unattached."
  This is Dogen's **"think of not-thinking... non-thinking"** (*hishiryo*): not blanking the
  mind, and not following its stories, but a third thing, bare awareness that lets thoughts be.
  (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shikantaza; https://thedewdrop.org/2020/03/09/fukanzazengi-dogen/)
- **Alertness is essential:** "an alert condition, performed erect, with no trace of
  sluggishness." Shikantaza is wakeful, not relaxation or trance.

**METHOD 3 - KOAN INTROSPECTION: the Rinzai path.**
- A teacher assigns a **koan**, classically **"Mu"** (Zhaozhou's answer to "does a dog have
  Buddha-nature?") or Hakuin's **"What is the sound of one hand [clapping]?"**
- In sitting you **hold the koan continuously**, not as a riddle to be solved intellectually but
  as a question to **become**, generating intense, single-pointed "Great Doubt" until
  conceptual mind exhausts itself and breaks open (kensho). Progress is checked in private
  interview (*dokusan/sanzen*) with the teacher.
  (https://www.newworldencyclopedia.org/entry/Hakuin; https://www.learnreligions.com/introduction-to-koans-449928)

**Duration.** A typical sit (*zazen* period) is **~25-40 minutes**, often two periods with a
slow walking meditation (*kinhin*) between; beginners do well starting at **10-15 minutes**.

**The two most common mistakes:**
- **Slumping or stiffening the posture / closing the eyes.** Because in zazen the posture *is*
  the practice, a collapsed spine breeds drowsiness and a rigid one breeds tension; closed eyes
  invite daydreaming. Erect, settled, eyes lowered and open.
- **Fighting thoughts or "trying to empty the mind."** Especially in shikantaza: the goal is not
  a blank mind but a mind that lets thoughts arise and pass without grabbing them. Straining for
  emptiness is itself a thought you're chasing.

## Quick comparison (for the post's mental model)

| | Object of attention | What you do with the mind | What it's "for" (own claim) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TM / mantra** | a meaningless sound, held effortlessly | let it fade, drift, return; no concentration | transcend thought → "pure consciousness," restful alertness |
| **Vipassana (Goenka)** | the breath (anapana), then body sensations | observe with total equanimity; don't react | direct insight into impermanence; dissolve reactive habits |
| **Zazen (Soto)** | nothing (shikantaza), or breath-count to start | let thoughts come and go; "non-thinking" | sitting *is* awakening (practice-realization) |
| **Zazen (Rinzai)** | a koan (Mu, one hand) | hold it as Great Doubt until it breaks open | sudden kensho, then deepened |
