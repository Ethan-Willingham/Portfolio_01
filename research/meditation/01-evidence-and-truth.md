# Meditation: Evidence vs. Hype (truth-over-narrative dossier)

Adversarial fact-check for the "Meditation" post. Goal: separate what the science
actually supports from the marketing. Every claim below carries a primary source and
a URL. Where a number is contested, soft, or unverifiable, that is stated explicitly.

Bottom line up front: meditation has real, **small-to-moderate** benefits for anxiety,
depression, and pain that hold up in decent trials, but those benefits **shrink toward
zero when compared against active controls** (exercise, drugs, other therapies, or any
credible placebo). The famous larger effects mostly come from comparing meditation to
*doing nothing* (waitlists). The field is also riddled with small, low-quality, unblinded
trials, weak adverse-event reporting, and an app/TM industry that oversells. Harm is real
and under-counted.

---

## 1. THE ANCHOR: Goyal et al. 2014 (JAMA Internal Medicine)

**Citation:** Goyal M, Singh S, Sibinga EMS, et al. "Meditation Programs for Psychological
Stress and Well-being: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis." *JAMA Internal Medicine*.
2014;174(3):357-368. doi:10.1001/jamainternmed.2013.13018.
- Full text (PMC): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4142584/
- Journal: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/1809754

**Scope.** Screened 18,753 unique citations and 1,651 full-text articles. Included **47 RCTs
with 3,320 participants.** This is the AHRQ-commissioned review, the most-cited rigorous
meta-analysis in the field, and it deliberately restricted to *randomized* trials with
control groups.

**Outcomes with MODERATE strength of evidence (mindfulness programs):**
- **Anxiety:** ES (standardized mean difference) **0.38 (95% CI 0.12 to 0.64) at 8 weeks**;
  **0.22 (95% CI 0.02 to 0.43) at 3-6 months.**
- **Depression:** ES **0.30 (95% CI 0.00 to 0.59) at 8 weeks**; **0.23 (95% CI 0.05 to 0.42)
  at 3-6 months.**
- **Pain:** ES **0.33 (95% CI 0.03 to 0.62).**

These are "small to moderate" effects. For calibration, an SMD of ~0.3 is roughly what
antidepressants show over placebo, and is at the low end of "clinically noticeable."

**Outcomes with LOW or INSUFFICIENT evidence:** stress/distress, mental-health-related
quality of life (low); and positive mood, attention, substance use, eating habits, sleep,
and weight (low or insufficient). Mantra-based programs (including TM) had **low or
insufficient evidence for ALL outcomes** in this review, largely because the trials were
poorer quality.

**The honest finding (the load-bearing quote).** From the conclusions:
> "We found low evidence of no effect or insufficient evidence that any of the meditation
> programs affected positive mood, attention, substance use, eating habits, sleep, and
> weight. We found no evidence that meditation programs were better than any active
> treatment (ie, drugs, exercise, and other behavioral therapies)."

And the abstract conclusion:
> "Clinicians should be aware that meditation programs can result in small to moderate
> reductions of multiple negative dimensions of psychological stress."

Read that twice: **no evidence meditation beat any active treatment.** The benefit over a
waitlist is real but modest; the benefit over a credible alternative is unproven.

## 2. THE BROADER EVIDENCE, OUTCOME BY OUTCOME

### Umbrella review (the single best "where does it shrink" source)
**Citation:** Goldberg SB, Riordan KM, Sun S, Davidson RJ. "The Empirical Status of
Mindfulness-Based Interventions: A Systematic Review of 44 Meta-Analyses of Randomized
Controlled Trials." *Perspectives on Psychological Science*. 2022;17(1):108-130.
doi:10.1177/1745691620968771.
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8364929/

Synthesized **44 meta-analyses, 336 unique RCTs, 30,483 participants.** Key pattern:
- **vs. passive/no-treatment controls:** moderate effects (adults ~**d = 0.55**; range
  d = 0.10 in kids to ~0.89 for anxiety disorders).
- **vs. ACTIVE controls:** effects collapse. Adults ~**d = 0.13**; "often non-significant"
  across pain, anxiety, cancer, and physical-health conditions.
- **vs. evidence-based treatments (CBT, drugs, etc.):** MBIs "did not differ ... for any
  [outcome] subcategory at post-treatment, with one exception" - **smoking (d = 0.42)**.
  Also a small edge for depression *relapse* prevention (MBCT, ~d = 0.17 at follow-up).
- **Quality:** **43.6%** of trials rated high risk for blinding of participants/personnel;
  **70.5%** of the meta-analyses did **not** test for publication bias; only **34.1%**
  even discussed adverse effects. Bottom line: MBIs hold "transdiagnostic potential ...
  albeit with stronger evidence for some [outcomes] than others," and are "at least
  partially supported by scientific evidence." A measured endorsement, not a rave.

**Takeaway for the post:** the headline-grabbing effect sizes are almost always vs. doing
nothing. Against a real comparison, mindfulness is roughly *as good as* other treatments,
not better - except a modest, real edge for smoking and depression relapse.

### Anxiety & depression
- Goyal 2014: moderate evidence, SMD ~0.3 (above).
- Goldberg 2022: vs. active controls, anxiety/depression effects are small and frequently
  not significant; no advantage over evidence-based treatments.
- A representative recent review (acceptance/mindfulness for DSM-5 anxiety disorders):
  large effects vs. inactive controls but effects "did not reach statistical significance"
  for several outcomes vs. active controls. (Nature *Scientific Reports* 2021,
  https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-99882-w)
- Honest verdict: real for mild-to-moderate anxiety/depression, comparable to (not better
  than) CBT or medication; the depression-*relapse*-prevention case (MBCT) is the
  strongest, endorsed in some clinical guidelines.

### Chronic pain
**Citation:** Hilton L, Hempel S, Ewing BA, et al. "Mindfulness Meditation for Chronic Pain:
Systematic Review and Meta-analysis." *Annals of Behavioral Medicine*. 2017;51(2):199-213.
doi:10.1007/s12160-016-9844-9.
- https://academic.oup.com/abm/article/51/2/199/4564147
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27658913/

38 RCTs. **Low-quality evidence** of a **small** decrease in pain vs. all controls:
**SMD 0.32 (95% CI 0.09 to 0.54)** across 30 RCTs, with **high heterogeneity (I-squared
= 77.6%)**. Only 7 of 38 trials reported on safety. Verdict: small, real-ish, but
low-confidence and noisy. Matches Goyal's pain estimate (~0.33).

### Stress / distress
Goyal 2014 rated this **low** strength of evidence despite stress reduction being the entire
selling point of MBSR ("Mindfulness-Based Stress *Reduction*"). The effect exists vs.
waitlist but is not robustly better than active comparators.

### Blood pressure / cardiovascular
- A 2017 meta-analysis (non-TM meditation, 19 studies): pooled **systolic BP -5.09 mmHg
  (95% CI -6.34 to -3.85)** and **diastolic -2.57 mmHg (95% CI -3.36 to -1.79)**.
  (PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28033127/)
- A 2020 meta-analysis: MBSR cut systolic BP by ~**-6.9 mmHg**, generic MBIs by ~**-2.7
  mmHg.** (ScienceDirect: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2405844020306794)
- Verdict: a genuine but **modest** drop, on the order of a few mmHg, comparable to other
  lifestyle changes; trials are small and often unblinded. See AHA caveat in section 5.

### Attention / cognition
- RCTs in healthy adults (27 studies, N=1,632): overall **g = 0.2**; attention **g = 0.18**,
  executive control **g = 0.18**, **no** significant working-memory effect.
  (Cognitive Therapy and Research 2021, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10608-020-10177-2)
- Brief mindfulness-induction meta-analysis (34 studies): **g = 0.18** overall; effect only
  in higher-order cognition (g = 0.35). (ScienceDirect:
  https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1053810019304283)
- Verdict: small effects on attention; the "rewires your brain / boosts IQ" framing is not
  supported. Most neuroimaging claims rest on tiny samples (see Van Dam 2018).

### Sleep
- Black DS, O'Reilly GA, Olmstead R, Breen EC, Irwin MR. "Mindfulness Meditation and
  Improvement in Sleep Quality and Daytime Impairment Among Older Adults With Sleep
  Disturbances: A Randomized Clinical Trial." *JAMA Internal Medicine*. 2015;175(4):494-501.
  doi:10.1001/jamainternmed.2014.8081.
  https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2110998
- This single trial (N=49) showed mindfulness beat sleep-hygiene education in older adults
  with moderate sleep complaints. BUT Goyal 2014 rated sleep overall as **low/insufficient**
  evidence. Verdict: promising for some populations, not established.

### Addiction / substance use
**Citation:** Grant S, Colaiaco B, Motala A, et al. "Mindfulness-based Relapse Prevention
for Substance Use Disorders: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis." *Journal of Addiction
Medicine*. 2017;11(5):386-396. doi:10.1097/ADM.0000000000000338.
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28727663/ (RAND: https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RR1031.html)

9 RCTs, 901 participants. **No statistically significant difference** between
Mindfulness-Based Relapse Prevention and comparators on **relapse (OR 0.72, 95% CI
0.46-1.13), frequency of use, or dropout** - all **low quality of evidence.** Small
favorable effects only on craving (SMD -0.13) and negative consequences (SMD -0.23).
Verdict: helps craving a little, has NOT been shown to prevent relapse better than standard
care. (Note: Goldberg's smoking-cessation edge, d=0.42, is the brighter spot here.)

## 3. ADVERSE EFFECTS (the part the apps don't mention)

The harm side is real, was ignored for decades, and the prevalence numbers come from
different denominators that are easy to mix up. Be precise.

### Lindahl, Britton et al. 2017 (the qualitative taxonomy)
**Citation:** Lindahl JR, Fisher NE, Cooper DJ, Rosen RK, Britton WB. "The varieties of
contemplative experience: A mixed-methods study of meditation-related challenges in Western
Buddhists." *PLOS ONE*. 2017;12(5):e0176239. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0176239.
- https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0176239
- PMC: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5443484/

Interviewed ~60 meditators plus teachers. Catalogued **59 experiences across 7 domains**:
cognitive, perceptual, affective, somatic, conative, sense of self, and social. This is a
*taxonomy*, NOT a prevalence study - it proves these harms exist and can be severe and
long-lasting, including in experienced practitioners with no prior psychiatric history. It
does **not** tell you how common they are. Harms documented include fear/anxiety/panic,
**depersonalization and derealization** (feeling unreal or detached from self/world),
re-experiencing of trauma, involuntary movements, hypersensitivity, insomnia, and, in
vulnerable people, **mania or psychosis.**

### Schlosser et al. 2019 (the "~25%" figure)
**Citation:** Schlosser M, Sparby T, Vago DR, Kreplin U, Lyytinen J. "Unpleasant
meditation-related experiences in regular meditators: Prevalence, predictors, and conceptual
considerations." *PLOS ONE*. 2019;14(5):e0216643. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0216643.
- https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0216643
- PMC: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6508707/

Online survey of **1,232 regular meditators** (>=2 months' practice). **25.6% (n=315, 95%
CI 23.1 to 28.0)** answered yes to a **single question**:
> "Have you ever had any particularly unpleasant experiences (e.g., anxiety, fear, distorted
> emotions or thoughts, altered sense of self or the world), which you think may have been
> caused by your meditation practice?"

**Critical caveats (state these in the post):** it is ONE retrospective question; the
authors themselves write the data gave "no indication of the exact type of experiences or
their severity and impact," did not assess pre-existing mental-health problems, used a
self-selected sample, and is subject to memory bias. So "1 in 4 meditators is harmed" is an
**overstatement** - it is "1 in 4 has *ever* had an unpleasant experience they attribute to
practice," most of which were probably transient. More likely with retreats, with
deconstructive (vipassana/insight) practice, and with higher repetitive negative thinking.

### Britton, Lindahl et al. 2021 (the controlled study, source of the "58%" stat)
**Citation:** Britton WB, Lindahl JR, Cooper DJ, Canby NK, Palitsky R. "Defining and
Measuring Meditation-Related Adverse Effects in Mindfulness-Based Programs." *Clinical
Psychological Science*. 2021;9(6):1185-1204. doi:10.1177/2167702621996340.
- PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8845498/
- Brown summary: https://www.brown.edu/news/2021-05-18/adverse-effects

A **3-arm RCT** dismantling MBCT, **N=96**, using a structured 44-item interview (MedEx-I)
that asked about each effect's occurrence, causal link, duration, valence, and impact.
This is the most rigorous prevalence estimate available because it is prospective and
controlled. Exact figures from the paper:
- **83%** reported **at least one** meditation-related effect (most reported several).
- **58%** had effects with **negative valence** (unpleasant while occurring).
- **37%** had effects with **negative impact on functioning.**
- **6%** had **"lasting bad effects"** - impairment in functioning lasting **more than a
  month.** (Britton's talks cite a 6-14% range across her datasets for lasting impairment.)

**Important framing note / trap to avoid:** Brown's own press release garbled this, saying
"58% reported at least one adverse effect." The *paper* says 83% reported at least one and
58% were negatively valenced. Use the paper's numbers, not the press release's.

### Farias et al. 2020 (the systematic review across all trials)
**Citation:** Farias M, Maraldi E, Wallenkampf KC, Lucchetti G. "Adverse events in
meditation practices and meditation-based therapies: a systematic review." *Acta
Psychiatrica Scandinavica*. 2020;142(5):374-393. doi:10.1111/acps.13225.
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32820538/

**83 studies, 6,703 participants.** **55 of 83 studies (65%)** reported at least one type
of adverse event. Pooled prevalence **8.3% (95% CI 5% to 12%).** Most common: **anxiety
(33% of the reporting studies), depression (27%), cognitive anomalies (25%)**, then GI
problems and suicidal behaviors (~11% each). Conclusion:
> "The occurrence of adverse events during or after meditation practices is not uncommon,
> and may occur in individuals with no previous history of mental health problems."

### Reconciling the numbers (so the post doesn't contradict itself)
- **8.3%** (Farias) = pooled rate of adverse *events* reported across heterogeneous trials,
  most of which never even looked for harm.
- **25.6%** (Schlosser) = self-selected meditators who *ever* had *any* unpleasant
  experience (one question, severity unknown).
- **58% / 83%** (Britton) = prospective, structured interview in one MBCT trial, counting
  any negatively-valenced or any effect at all - high because they actually asked carefully.
- **6%** (Britton) = the meaningful, durable-harm figure: lasting functional impairment.
The honest one-liner: *meditation is not risk-free; transient unpleasant experiences are
common, and a small but non-trivial minority (single digits percent) suffer lasting harm.*
The harms were under-studied for decades: as of 2015, **fewer than 25% of meditation trials
even monitored for adverse effects** (Van Dam 2018, below).

## 4. THE OVERCLAIMING

### The industry
- Global meditation-app market: ~**$1.4-1.6 billion in 2023-2024**, projected to ~$7+
  billion by 2033 (CAGRs ~18%). Sources: GlobeNewswire/Polaris
  (https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2024/04/08/2858965/0/en/Global-Meditation-Management-Apps-Market-Size-To-Worth-USD-7-25-Billion-By-2033-CAGR-of-17-9.html);
  Straits Research (https://straitsresearch.com/report/meditation-management-apps-market).
- **Calm:** ~$300M revenue / ~$150M consumer spend in 2023; highest-grossing health app in
  Jan 2024 (~$7.7M in-app that month). **Headspace:** ~$110M consumer spend in 2023.
  (Statista: https://www.statista.com/statistics/1239670/top-health-and-meditation-apps-by-revenue/)
- These are valuations/sales figures, not health outcomes. Treat the dollar size as
  evidence of *market hype*, not efficacy. (Market-size projections come from commercial
  research firms and should be cited as "industry estimates," not peer-reviewed.)

### The hype critique (the keystone quote)
**Citation:** Van Dam NT, van Vugt MK, Vago DR, et al. "Mind the Hype: A Critical Evaluation
and Prescriptive Agenda for Research on Mindfulness and Meditation." *Perspectives on
Psychological Science*. 2018;13(1):36-61. doi:10.1177/1745691617709589.
- https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691617709589
- PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29016274/
- APS press: https://www.psychologicalscience.org/news/releases/mindfulness-and-meditation-need-more-rigorous-study.html

Written by 15 authors, several of them *pro-meditation* researchers (incl. Willoughby
Britton and Sara Lazar), which makes it a from-inside-the-field warning, not a hit piece.
Core caution (abstract):
> "Misinformation and poor methodology associated with past studies of mindfulness may lead
> public consumers to be harmed, misled, and disappointed."

The damning specifics (from authors/press release):
- **"Only 30 percent of mindfulness-based interventions have moved past the first stage [of
  clinical testing], and only 9 percent have tested efficacy in a research clinic against an
  active control."** (i.e., ~9% have a real comparison group.)
- "As of 2015, fewer than 25 percent of meditation trials actively monitored for negative
  or challenging experiences."
- Willoughby Britton: *"We are sometimes overselling the benefits of mindfulness to pretty
  much any person who has any condition, without much caution, nuance or condition-specific
  modifications."*
- Nicholas Van Dam: *"The rigor that should go along with developing and applying them just
  isn't there yet."*
- There is **no single agreed definition of "mindfulness"**, which makes pooling studies
  scientifically shaky.

## 5. TRANSCENDENTAL MEDITATION: where real research meets the cosmic claims

TM is the clean case study of legitimate findings entangled with unfalsifiable marketing.

### The grand claims
- **Yogic Flying / TM-Sidhi program.** Marketed with imagery of levitation. What it
  actually is: practitioners sitting cross-legged who **hop** off a foam pad. No one has
  ever demonstrated levitation or hovering under any controlled condition. (Wikipedia,
  "Yogic flying": https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yogic_flying)
- **The "Maharishi Effect."** Claim that if the square root of 1% of a population practices
  TM/TM-Sidhi, group meditation lowers crime and conflict via a "field of consciousness."
  The flagship study: Hagelin J, et al. "Effects of Group Practice of the Transcendental
  Meditation Program on Preventing Violent Crime in Washington, D.C." *Social Indicators
  Research*. 1999;47(2):153-201. (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/A:1006978911496).
  It claimed a **23.3% drop in violent crime** during a summer 1993 gathering of ~4,000
  meditators, with an implausibly tiny p-value. **Scientific consensus: pseudoscience.**
  Problems: no plausible causal mechanism, the model was fit with after-the-fact weather/
  temperature covariates, crime actually *rose* during parts of the period, the analysis was
  authored by TM-movement members, and it has never independently replicated. Carl Sagan and
  James Randi both labeled TM's broader claims pseudoscience. (Wikipedia, "Transcendental
  Meditation": https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_Meditation)
- **TM as religion (court finding).** In *Malnak v. Yogi* (1977, upheld by the Third Circuit
  in 1979), U.S. courts ruled TM/SCI **religious in nature**, barring it from public schools
  under the Establishment Clause - despite the movement marketing TM as secular and
  scientific.

### The cost
Learning TM in the U.S. costs about **$980** for the standard adult course (4 sessions plus
lifetime follow-up), with sliding-scale and payment-plan options (~$420 reduced).
(Official: https://www.tm.org/en-us/course-fee) The TM-Sidhi/Yogic-Flying course costs
substantially more on top of that. So the entry price to the "scientifically proven"
technique is roughly $1,000 - itself a point worth making about the incentive structure.

### What the rigorous evidence on TM actually shows
- **Blood pressure:** the legitimate core. The **American Heart Association**'s 2013
  scientific statement ("Beyond Medications and Diet: Alternative Approaches to Lowering
  Blood Pressure," *Hypertension* 61(6):1360-1383,
  https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/HYP.0b013e318293645f) concluded TM "may be
  considered in clinical practice" for BP but gave it only a cautious **Class IIB, Level of
  Evidence B** rating ("modest, mixed data"), and stated **other meditation/relaxation
  techniques had insufficient evidence to recommend.** Crucially the AHA also noted that
  **exercise and device-guided breathing have stronger BP evidence than TM.** So even TM's
  best result is "could be considered," not "use this instead of proven treatments."
- Independent meta-analyses find TM lowers BP by a few mmHg for up to ~3 months, after which
  the effect wanes, and find effects **comparable to other meditation techniques** (i.e.,
  nothing magic about the mantra). Much of the older TM literature comes from
  movement-affiliated researchers, a recognized conflict-of-interest concern.

**The entanglement to name in the post:** the TM organization cites the genuine AHA/BP
finding as a halo to validate yogic flying and the Maharishi Effect, which are not science.
A reader should keep the modest, real BP benefit and discard the cosmic claims.

## WHAT WE CAN HONESTLY SAY (the summary the post can lean on)

1. **Meditation works, modestly, for anxiety, depression, and pain.** The best rigorous
   meta-analysis (Goyal 2014, 47 RCTs, 3,320 people) found moderate-strength evidence with
   small effect sizes around **0.3** - about the same as antidepressants over placebo.
2. **The big benefits are mostly "vs. doing nothing."** Against active controls (exercise,
   drugs, therapy, real placebos) the advantage largely disappears: Goyal found **"no
   evidence that meditation programs were better than any active treatment,"** and the
   44-meta-analysis umbrella review (Goldberg 2022, 336 RCTs, 30,483 people) found effects
   drop from ~**d=0.55 vs. passive controls to ~d=0.13 vs. active controls.**
3. **It is roughly as good as, not better than, standard care** - with two modest real
   edges: smoking cessation (d=0.42) and depression-relapse prevention (MBCT).
4. **The field is methodologically weak.** Per Van Dam 2018's "Mind the Hype," **only ~9%
   of mindfulness interventions have been tested against an active control**, there's no
   agreed definition of mindfulness, and publication bias is rarely checked.
5. **Meditation is not risk-free.** A pooled review (Farias 2020) found adverse events in
   **65% of the 83 studies** examined; **25.6%** of regular meditators report having had an
   unpleasant experience (Schlosser 2019); a controlled MBCT trial found **58%** had a
   negatively-valenced experience and **6%** had lasting functional impairment (>1 month)
   (Britton 2021). Harms include anxiety, depersonalization, re-traumatization, and, rarely,
   psychosis - and were almost entirely unmonitored until recently.
6. **The hype is a billion-dollar business.** Meditation apps are a ~**$1.5B/year** market;
   Calm alone made ~**$300M** in 2023. Commercial scale is not clinical evidence.
7. **TM is the cautionary tale.** Its one legitimate result (a few-mmHg BP drop, AHA Class
   IIB) is used to sell yogic "flying" (cross-legged hopping) and the Maharishi Effect (the
   pseudoscientific claim that group meditation cut Washington D.C. crime 23%). The course
   costs ~**$980**. Keep the BP finding; discard the cosmos.

**Net thesis for the post:** the science supports a humble, useful claim - meditation is a
low-cost practice with small, real benefits for stress, anxiety, depression, and pain,
about on par with other good options - and refutes the grand one - that it is a uniquely
powerful, side-effect-free cure-all or a force that reshapes reality.
