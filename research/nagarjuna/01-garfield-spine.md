## Garfield's rendering of the three technical terms

This is the headline finding for the translator-comparison angle.

- **svabhava** (Tib. *rang bzhin*) → Garfield's **primary, declared choice is "essence."** From his own translator's note (the book's footnote 4): *"I have generally translated the Tibetan 'rang bzhin' (Skt: svabhava) with the English philosophical term 'essence,' as opposed to the more traditional 'self-nature' or 'own-being' used by many Buddhologists. ... I think that this best captures Nagarjuna's usage, and this choice makes good etymological sense as well."* In his **commentary prose** he uses **"essence," "inherent existence," "intrinsic identity," and "nature"** more or less interchangeably (e.g. "Empty of what? And the answer is, Empty of inherent existence"; "they have no essence"). So: **verse text leans on "essence"; commentary leans on "inherent existence."** He explicitly does **not** use "self-nature" or "own-being."

- **sunyata** → **"emptiness"** (and the adjective *sunya* → **"empty"**). Unambiguous and consistent throughout translation and commentary. Note he insists emptiness is itself empty ("the emptiness of emptiness").

- **prapanca** (Tib. *spros pa*) → Garfield has **no single fixed English word**; he renders it by context with a family of terms:
  - **"conceptual construction"** — in the dedicatory verse ("And free from conceptual construction").
  - **"objectification"** — in the climactic final verse 24/25.24 ("The pacification of all objectification") and at 18.5's neighborhood ("Do not see the pacification of objectification").
  - **"fabrication" / "mental fabrication"** — at 18.5 ("This comes from mental fabrication. / Fabrication ceases through emptiness") and in the surrounding commentary ("The realization of emptiness eliminates that fabrication of essence").
  - **"conceptual fabrications" / "cognitive fabrications"** — in the Tathagata chapters.
  - So for the post's comparison table: Garfield's MMK does **not** standardize prapanca to one English word; his signature renderings are **"objectification" (final verse) and "fabrication"/"mental fabrication,"** with **"conceptual construction"** in the opening salutation.

## Dedicatory Verses (the salutation / eight negations, before Chapter I)

> I prostrate to the Perfect Buddha,
> The best of teachers, who taught that
> Whatever is dependently arisen is
> Unceasing, unborn,
>
> Unannihilated, not permanent,
> Not coming, not going,
> Without distinction, without identity,
> And free from conceptual construction.

**Source:** archive.org full-text (clean block) and Garfield's PDF reprint both confirm this wording verbatim; also confirmed by the aaari.info selection. The eight negations are: unceasing, unborn / unannihilated, not permanent / not coming, not going / without distinction, without identity — plus the salute to the Buddha as teacher of dependent arising, and the ninth qualifier "free from conceptual construction" (his rendering of *prapanca*).

**Commentary gist:** Garfield treats the eight as four pairs that each make a point. To say "whatever is dependently arisen is unceasing and unborn" is, he writes, *"to emphasize that dependent arising amounts to emptiness, and emptiness amounts to nonexistence in the ultimate sense."* The "unannihilated, not permanent" pair establishes impermanence without nihilism ("The empirical reality of things ... is not denied by asserting their emptiness"). The "not coming, not going" pair denies intrinsically identifiable entities persisting over time. The final phrase ("free from conceptual construction") he says *"raises a tension that is central to Madhyamika philosophy ... between the desire to characterize the ultimate nature of things and the recognition that all characterization is conventional."* The verses contain "the seeds of the eventual equation of ... samsara with nirvana."

## 1.1 — Examination of Conditions

> Neither from itself nor from another,
> Nor from both,
> Nor without a cause,
> Does anything whatever, anywhere arise.

**Source:** archive.org full-text (both the clean block and the commentary copy print it identically); chapter titled "Chapter I / Examination of Conditions."

**Commentary gist:** Chapter 1 turns on Garfield's reading of the *cause* (Skt. *hetu*) vs *condition* (*pratyaya*) distinction: a "cause" for Nagarjuna is an event or state "that has in it a power ... to bring about its effect and has that power as part of its essence or nature (svabhava, rang bzhin)," whereas a "condition" is merely something appealed to in explaining another event. Nagarjuna rejects causation-by-inherent-power; this fourfold negation of arising (from self / other / both / no cause) is, on Garfield's reading, where the doctrine of the emptiness of emptiness is "already found in the opening chapter."

## 2.1, 2.8, 2.25 — Examination of Motion

**Chapter title (Garfield):** "Chapter II / Examination of Motion."

### 2.1
> What has been moved is not moving.
> What has not been moved is not moving.
> Apart from what has been moved and what has not been moved,
> Movement cannot be conceived.

(The clean block wraps the third line as "Apart from what has been moved and what has not / been moved," purely a page-width line wrap; it is one sentence-line.)

### 2.8
> Inasmuch as a real mover does not move,
> And a non-mover does not move,
> Apart from a mover and a non-mover,
> What third thing could move?

### 2.25
> Neither an entity nor a nonentity
> Moves in any of the three ways.
> So motion, mover,
> And route are nonexistent.

**Source:** archive.org full-text, both copies; 2.25 wording confirmed from the commentary copy (which resolved two OCR artifacts in the clean copy — see corrections note at top).

**Commentary gist:** Garfield reads Chapter 2 as denying that motion is an entity, or a property existing independently of moving things, or part of the nature (essence) of moving things. "Nobody thinks Nagarjuna is denying that anything ever moves"; rather, *"a reification of motion is incoherent. Motion is therefore empty."* The "three ways" (in the moved, the not-yet-moved, the present-moving) are each shown to be untenable as loci of inherently existent motion. He immediately asks after 2.25 whether this means "motion is completely nonexistent" and the universe is static — and answers no; the target is inherent existence, not conventional motion.

## 13.8 — Examination of Compounded Phenomena (the emptiness of emptiness)

> The victorious ones have said
> That emptiness is the relinquishing of all views.
> For whomever emptiness is a view,
> That one will accomplish nothing.

**Source:** archive.org full-text; appears identically in the clean block, the commentary copy, and again where Garfield cross-references it (as "XIII: 8") in the Chapter 24 commentary.

**Commentary gist:** Garfield calls the sense of "view" here "crucial." A view, in the sense Nagarjuna warns against, is *"a theory on the same level of discourse at which reificationist-nihilist debates proceed"* — a claim about what does or does not exist *when existence is taken to mean inherent existence.* Both "things have natures" and "things therefore don't exist at all" are views in this bad sense. Emptiness analysis is *not* such a view: *"the claim is not that things exist in virtue of having the property of emptiness as an essence. Rather it is the claim that they are empty because they have no essence."* He adds (footnote) that emptiness "is not an entity," yet "remains a characteristic of all phenomena (including itself) and, hence, like them, is conventionally real" — so taking emptiness *itself* as a really-existing view is the incurable error.

## 18.5 — Examination of Self and Entities

> Action and misery having ceased, there is nirvana.
> Action and misery come from conceptual thought.
> This comes from mental fabrication.
> Fabrication ceases through emptiness.

**Source:** archive.org full-text, both copies, identical. ("Action and misery" = action and affliction; "mental fabrication" / "fabrication" = *prapanca*.)

**Commentary gist:** This verse states the soteriological chain in reverse: liberation comes from the ceasing of action and affliction; these come from conceptual thought; that from fabrication (*prapanca*); and fabrication ceases in emptiness. Garfield: *"Grasping, contaminated action, and suffering are rooted in delusion, and this delusion comes from cognitive error. The root delusion ... is the confusion of merely conventional existence with inherent existence. The realization of emptiness eliminates that fabrication of essence, which eliminates grasping, contaminated action, and its pernicious consequences."* So for Garfield the engine of bondage is the false projection of *svabhava*, and seeing emptiness shuts it off.

## 24.8, 24.9, 24.10 — Examination of the Four Noble Truths (the two truths)

### 24.8
> The Buddha's teaching of the Dharma
> Is based on two truths:
> A truth of worldly convention
> And an ultimate truth.

### 24.9
> Those who do not understand
> The distinction drawn between these two truths
> Do not understand
> The Buddha's profound truth.

### 24.10
> Without a foundation in the conventional truth,
> The significance of the ultimate cannot be taught.
> Without understanding the significance of the ultimate,
> Liberation is not achieved.

**Source:** Garfield's own scanned PDF (image quality, page 30 of the reprint) — authoritative — and confirmed by the archive.org full-text. Numbering and wording match exactly.

**Commentary gist:** Garfield stresses that Nagarjuna *"is not disparaging the conventional by contrast to the ultimate, but is arguing that understanding the ultimate nature of things is completely dependent upon understanding conventional truth."* He notes the key ambiguity of *samvrti* (conventional): it "can also mean concealing, or occluding," and Madhyamikas exploit this — "the conventional, in occluding its conventional character, covers up its own emptiness." Liberation requires insight into emptiness, but that insight can only be reached and communicated through language and thought, which are themselves conventional — hence the conventional truth is the indispensable ladder to the ultimate.

## 24.18, 24.19 — the keystone

### 24.18
> Whatever is dependently co-arisen
> That is explained to be emptiness.
> That, being a dependent designation,
> Is itself the middle way.

### 24.19
> Something that is not dependently arisen,
> Such a thing does not exist.
> Therefore a nonempty thing
> Does not exist.

**Source:** Garfield's own scanned PDF (page 31 of the reprint) — authoritative — and confirmed twice in the archive.org full-text. The comma after "designation" in 24.18 line 3 is from Garfield's own scan (see corrections note).

**Commentary gist:** This is the chapter's fulcrum. Garfield: *"Here Nagarjuna asserts the fundamental identity of (1) emptiness, or the ultimate truth; (2) the dependently originated, that is, all phenomena; and (3) verbal convention. Moreover, he asserts that understanding this relation is itself the middle-way philosophical view he articulates in Mulamadhyamakakarika."* He reads 24.18 as the explicit statement of a doctrine (the emptiness of emptiness) "already found in the opening chapter," and the verse that "show[s] us exactly how XXIV:18 is to be understood." 24.19 supplies the converse: since whatever exists is dependently arisen, and the dependently arisen is empty, "there does not exist anything that is not empty."

## 24.40 — whoever sees dependent arising

> Whoever sees dependent arising
> Also sees suffering
> And its arising
> And its cessation as well as the path.

**Source:** Garfield's own scanned PDF (page 33 of the reprint) — authoritative — and confirmed in the archive.org full-text.

**Commentary gist (chapter-level, from Garfield's essay):** Garfield frames 24.40 as the verse that "reconnects the entire analysis to the four noble truths": verses 36–39 demonstrate that emptiness is the only coherent ontology, and 40 shows that to see dependent arising (hence emptiness) is *ipso facto* to see the Four Noble Truths. He summarizes the chapter's heart (16–19) as the argument that "emptiness and dependent arising (ultimate and conventional truth) are identical, and that all conventional existents are empty," singling out 24.18 as the verse where Nagarjuna "equates emptiness and dependent arising and asserts that each is conventionally existent, as is the relation between them."

## 25.19, 25.20 — no difference between samsara and nirvana

### 25.19
> There is not the slightest difference
> Between cyclic existence and nirvana.
> There is not the slightest difference
> Between nirvana and cyclic existence.

### 25.20
> Whatever is the limit of nirvana,
> That is the limit of cyclic existence.
> There is not even the slightest difference between them,
> Or even the subtlest thing.

**Source:** archive.org full-text, both copies (identical; "cyclic existence" = samsara).

**Commentary gist:** Garfield reads these as the consummation of the two-truths identity: since samsara (the conventional, dependently arisen world) and nirvana (its emptiness, the ultimate) are not two separate realms but the same reality seen correctly vs. incorrectly, there is literally no gap between them — "emptiness is just the conventional seen as it is." Nirvana is not another place reached by leaving the world; it is a transformed relation to this empty world. (He notes nirvana, like everything else, must itself be empty — "a way of engagement with nonentities by nonentities.")

## 25.24 — the final verse

> The pacification of all objectification
> And the pacification of illusion:
> No Dharma was taught by the Buddha
> At any time, in any place, to any person.

**Source:** archive.org full-text, both copies (identical). ("Objectification" = *prapanca*.) Note: this is the last verse of Chapter 25 in Garfield's edition; the very last verses of the whole MMK are in Chapter 27, but 25.24 is the verse the prompt identifies and it is the celebrated "no dharma taught" stanza.

**Commentary gist:** Garfield reads the closing as a warning against reifying the teaching itself. The whole apparatus — the Buddha, the path, nirvana — is a conventional, soteriological device aimed at "dispel[ling] illusion and ... end[ing] deluded ontological fabrication." Therefore *"it is important, Nagarjuna concludes, not to reify that doctrine, or any of the entities that appear as prima facie referents of the words used to expound it (the Buddha, the spiritual community, etc.)."* Nirvana "does not, on this account, amount to an entity ... Rather it is a way of engagement with nonentities by nonentities." Hence, ultimately, no dharma was ever taught: at the ultimate level there is nothing to assert and no one to assert it to.

## Notes on numbering and edition for the post

- Garfield numbers chapters with Roman numerals and verses with Arabic (e.g. "XXIV: 18"). The post's "24.18" maps to his "XXIV:18."
- The dedicatory verses are unnumbered in Garfield ("Dedicatory Verses," before Chapter I). They are two four-line stanzas.
- The aaari.info / Yin Shun PDF is a **70-stanza thematic selection** that re-numbers everything 1–70 using Garfield's wording. It is fine for confirming Garfield's *English wording* but its numbers are NOT Garfield's chapter.verse numbers — do not cite "verse 14" etc. from it.
- Chapter title variants in the literature: Garfield's Ch 2 = "Examination of Motion" (others say "of Coming and Going" / "the Examined, the Examiner..."); Ch 13 = "Examination of Compounded Phenomena" (others "of Dispositions/Samskaras"); Ch 18 = "Examination of Self and Entities"; Ch 24 = "Examination of the Four Noble Truths"; Ch 25 = "Examination of Nirvana." Use Garfield's titles when quoting Garfield.
