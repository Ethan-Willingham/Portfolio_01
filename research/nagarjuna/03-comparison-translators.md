# MMK comparison translators (for the sidebar stacks)

The post's spine is Garfield. On a few pivotal verses it stacks rivals to show where
they split. This file holds the verbatim rivals, attributed, with sources.

**Confidence:** HIGH on everything quoted below. Where a translator could not be sourced
verbatim from the open web (Streng's verses), that is stated and NOT faked.

---

## The keystone: 24.18, six translators (all verbatim-sourced)

Sourced from the Indian Philosophy Blog's verse-24.18 comparison post
(https://indianphilosophyblog.org/2015/09/16/another-attempt-at-nagarjunas-mmk/),
which quotes each translation directly. This is the single best stack in the post.

- **Richard Robinson (1967):** "It is dependent arising that we term emptiness; this is a designation overlaid (on emptiness); it alone is the middle path."
- **Kenneth Inada (1970):** "We declare that whatever is relational origination is sunyata. It is a provisional name (i.e. thought construction for the mutuality of being), and indeed, it is the middle path."
- **Mervyn Sprung (1979):** "We interpret the dependent arising of all things as the absence of being in them. Absence of being is a guiding, not a cognitive notion, presupposing the everyday. It is itself the middle way."
- **David Kalupahana (1986):** "We state that whatever is dependent arising, that is emptiness. That is dependent upon convention. That itself is the middle path."
- **Jay Garfield (1995):** "Whatever is dependently co-arisen, that is explained to be emptiness. That, being a dependent designation, is itself the middle way."
- **Mark Siderits & Shoryu Katsura (2013):** "Dependent origination we declare to be emptiness. It (emptiness) is a dependent concept; just that is the middle path."

The split is on `prajnaptir upadaya` (the third quarter): "designation overlaid" (Robinson) /
"provisional name" (Inada) / "a guiding, not a cognitive notion" (Sprung) / "dependent upon
convention" (Kalupahana) / "dependent designation" (Garfield) / "dependent concept"
(Siderits-Katsura). Same two words, six readings of what kind of thing emptiness IS.

## The dedication / eight negations

- **Garfield (1995):** "I prostrate to the Perfect Buddha, / The best of teachers, who taught that / Whatever is dependently arisen is / Unceasing, unborn, // Unannihilated, not permanent, / Not coming, not going, / Without distinction, without identity, / And free from conceptual construction." (his rendering of prapanca here = "conceptual construction")
- **Stephen Batchelor (literal, 2000):** "I bow down to the most sublime of speakers, the completely awakened one who taught contingency (no cessation, no birth, no annihilation, no permanence, no coming, no going, no difference, no identity) to ease fixations." (Source: Batchelor's romanized-Tibetan + literal English document, the draft for *Verses from the Center*, archive.org MMK_texts_202210. "contingency" = dependent origination; "to ease fixations" = prapancopasama, the pacification of prapanca.)

Batchelor's secular voice is the sharpest contrast in the whole post: he dissolves the
technical vocabulary entirely. Dependent origination becomes "contingency," the pacification
of conceptual proliferation becomes "to ease fixations."

## 18.5 (the soteriological chain; prapanca)

- **Garfield (1995):** "Action and misery having ceased, there is nirvana. / Action and misery come from conceptual thought. / This comes from mental fabrication. / Fabrication ceases through emptiness."
- **Batchelor (literal, 2000):** "Through the ceasing of action and affliction, there is freedom. Action and affliction [come] from thoughts and they from fixations. Fixations are stopped by emptiness."

Same verse: Garfield's "mental fabrication" is Batchelor's "fixations" (both = prapanca).

## The three words: how each translator renders the technical terms

This is its own comparison (a "three words" section / sidebar), not tied to one verse.

### svabhava (the thing Nagarjuna denies of everything)
| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| Garfield (1995) | **"essence"** (verse), **"inherent existence"** (commentary). His footnote: "as opposed to the more traditional 'self-nature' or 'own-being'." |
| Streng (1967) | **"self-existence"** / **"own-being"** (confirmed from his book text) |
| Inada (1970) | **"self-nature"** / **"own-being"** |
| Siderits & Katsura (2013) | **"intrinsic nature"** |
| Batchelor (2000) | **"essence"** |

Literal Sanskrit: sva ("own") + bhava ("being") = "own-being." Garfield's "essence" is the
boldest choice (it imports an Aristotelian word); he defends it in a footnote. The split
"essence / inherent existence / intrinsic nature / self-existence / own-being" is real and
worth showing: the post turns on what exactly is being denied.

### sunyata (the cure)
Near-universal: **"emptiness."** Streng (1967) helped standardize "emptiness" and explicitly
flags that it had earlier been mistranslated as "nothingness" or "relativity" (and, by
Stcherbatsky and others, as "the Void" / "voidness," capital-V, which reads as a cosmic
substance). The post's point: "voidness"/"the void" misleads toward nihilism or a thing;
"emptiness" (empty OF something, i.e. of svabhava) is the accurate, deflationary word.
sunya is also the Sanskrit for the number zero (see 02-sanskrit.md).

### prapanca (the engine of delusion)
The widest spread of all; almost nobody agrees:
| Translator | Rendering |
|---|---|
| Garfield | **"conceptual construction"** (dedication), **"fabrication" / "mental fabrication"** (18.5), **"objectification"** (25.24). He does NOT standardize it. |
| Streng (1967) | **"phenomenal extension"** (his signature, distinctive choice) |
| Siderits & Katsura | **"conceptual proliferation" / "hypostatization"** |
| Batchelor | **"fixations"** |
| literal | "spreading-out, diversification, the mind's proliferation into concepts/names" |

This is the best single illustration of the translation problem: one Sanskrit word, and the
English candidates run from "phenomenal extension" to "fabrication" to "fixations" to
"objectification." Good sidebar material.

## Notes / honesty flags

- **Streng's verbatim verses are NOT openly available** (his 1967 book is a borrow-only scan
  on archive.org; the open mirrors expose only term choices and ch. XV). His term renderings
  above ARE confirmed. So in the post: use Streng for the term comparison (svabhava =
  "self-existence"; the "emptiness over voidness" history), but do NOT put a verbatim Streng
  verse in a stack. The 24.18 stack already has six sourced translators without him.
- Inada's and Streng's 24.18 are famously near-identical ("relational origination ...
  provisional name"); the blog attributes that wording to **Inada**, so attribute it to
  Inada, not Streng.
- Batchelor's document is his LITERAL translation of the Tibetan (the working draft for the
  poetic *Verses from the Center*, Riverhead 2000). Attribute it as "Stephen Batchelor's
  literal rendering" to be precise; the published poem is even freer.
- Kalupahana, Sprung, Robinson are bonus translators that strengthen the 24.18 stack; all
  three are real, sourced, named.
