## DELIVERABLE 1 — VERBATIM ROSS QUOTES (exact wording, every word + punctuation)

All quotes below were extracted character-for-character from the Ross text and

Ross's text; they are shown only to pin the Bekker reference and must be removed

### a. The regress of ends / happiness chosen for itself

**1094a18-22** (the regress argument):
> "If, then, there is some end of the things we do, which we desire for its own sake (everything else being desired for the sake of this), and if we do not choose everything for the sake of something else (for at that rate the process would go on to infinity, so that our desire would be empty and vain), clearly this must be the good and the chief good."

**1097a34-b6** (we choose happiness always for itself):
> "Now such a thing happiness, above all else, is held to be; for this we choose always for itself and never for the sake of something else, but honour, pleasure, reason, and every virtue we choose indeed for themselves (for if nothing resulted from them we should still choose each of them), but we choose them also for the sake of happiness, judging that by means of them we shall be happy. Happiness, on the other hand, no one chooses for the sake of these, nor, in general, for anything other than itself."

(Immediately preceding, the definition of "final without qualification," 1097a30-34:)
> "and therefore we call final without qualification that which is always desirable in itself and never for the sake of something else."

### b. The three wrong lives

**1095b14-19** (pleasure, the life of enjoyment):
> "To judge from the lives that men lead, most men, and men of the most vulgar type, seem (not without some ground) to identify the good, or happiness, with pleasure; which is the reason why they love the life of enjoyment."

**1095b19-22** ("suitable to beasts"):
> "Now the mass of mankind are evidently quite slavish in their tastes, preferring a life suitable to beasts, but they get some ground for their view from the fact that many of those in high places share the tastes of Sardanapallus."

**1095b22-26** (honour / the political life — superficial, depends on others):
> "A consideration of the prominent types of life shows that people of superior refinement and of active disposition identify happiness with honour; for this is, roughly speaking, the end of the political life. But it seems too superficial to be what we are looking for, since it is thought to depend on those who bestow honour rather than on him who receives it, but the good we divine to be something proper to a man and not easily taken from him."

**1096a5-7** (money-making — merely useful):
> "The life of money-making is one undertaken under compulsion, and wealth is evidently not the good we are seeking; for it is merely useful and for the sake of something else."

### c. The function (ergon) argument and its conclusion

**1097b24-1098a7** (the setup — man's function):
> "This might perhaps be given, if we could first ascertain the function of man. For just as for a flute-player, a sculptor, or any artist, and, in general, for all things that have a function or activity, the good and the 'well' is thought to reside in the function, so would it seem to be for man, if he has a function. ... Now if the function of man is an activity of soul which follows or implies a rational principle ... life in the sense of activity is what we mean; for this seems to be the more proper sense of the term."

**1098a7-18** (the conclusion):
> "if [we state the function of man to be a certain kind of life, and this to be an activity or actions of the soul implying a rational principle, and the function of a good man to be the good and noble performance of these, and if any action is well performed when it is performed in accordance with the appropriate excellence:] if this is the case, human good turns out to be activity of soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete."

  The single load-bearing sentence (1098a16-18):
> "human good turns out to be activity of soul in accordance with virtue, and if there are more than one virtue, in accordance with the best and most complete."

### d. One swallow does not make a summer

**1098a18-20**:
> "But we must add 'in a complete life'. For one swallow does not make a summer, nor does one day; and so too one day, or a short time, does not make a man blessed and happy."

### e. Virtue comes by habit

**1103a31-b2**:
> "so too we become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts."

(Fuller context, 1103a31-b2:)
> "For the things we have to learn before we can do them, we learn by doing them, e.g. men become builders by building and lyre-players by playing the lyre; so too we become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts."

**1103b23-25** (habituated from youth — "all the difference"):
> "It makes no small difference, then, whether we form habits of one kind or of another from our very youth; it makes a very great difference, or rather all the difference."

(Also useful — the ethos pun, 1103a17-18, which grounds "ethics = habit"; note Wikisource preserves the actual Greek where MIT transliterates as ēthikē / ethos:)
> "moral virtue comes about as a result of habit, whence also its name (ἠθική) is one that is formed by a slight variation from the word ἔθος (habit)."

### f. The definition of moral virtue as a mean

**1106b36-1107a2**:
> "Virtue, then, is a state of character concerned with choice, lying in a mean, i.e. the mean relative to us, this being determined by a rational principle, and by that principle by which the man of practical wisdom would determine it."

(Continues, 1107a2-6:)
> "Now it is a mean between two vices, that which depends on excess and that which depends on defect; and again it is a mean because the vices respectively fall short of or exceed what is right in both passions and actions, while virtue both finds and chooses that which is intermediate."

### g. Courage as the mean between cowardice and rashness (Book 3)

**1115a6-9** (courage is a mean re fear and confidence):
> "That it is a mean with regard to feelings of fear and confidence has already been made evident; and plainly the things we fear are terrible things, and these are, to speak without qualification, evils".

**1115b17-20** (the brave man — the mean):
> "The man, then, who faces and who fears the right things and from the right motive, in the right way and at the right time, and who feels confidence under the corresponding conditions, is brave; for the brave man feels and acts according to the merits of the case and in whatever way the rule directs."

**1115b24-28** (the two extremes named):
> "Of those who go to excess he who exceeds in fearlessness has no name (we have said previously that many states of character have no names), but he would be a sort of madman or insensible person if he feared nothing ... while the man who exceeds in confidence about what really is terrible is rash."

**1115b34-1116a1** (the coward — the deficiency):
> "The man who exceeds in fear is a coward; for he fears both what he ought not and as he ought not, and all the similar characterizations attach to him."

  Cleanest single-pair line for the post: brave = the mean; **rash** exceeds in
  confidence (1115b28); **coward** exceeds in fear / is deficient in confidence
  (1115b34). [Aristotle notes the excess-of-fearlessness extreme "has no name."]

### h. Practical wisdom / phronesis

**1140b4-6**:
> "The remaining alternative, then, is that it is a true and reasoned state of capacity to act with regard to the things that are good or bad for man."

  i.e. phronesis = "a true and reasoned state of capacity to act with regard to
  the things that are good or bad for man."

### i. Friendship (Books 8-9)

**1155a5-6** (without friends no one would choose to live):
> "For without friends no one would choose to live, though he had all other goods".

**1155b18-21** (the lovable = good, pleasant, useful — the three grounds):
> "For not everything seems to be loved but only the lovable, and this is good, pleasant, or useful".

**1156a6-10** (three kinds of friendship):
> "Now these reasons differ from each other in kind; so, therefore, do the corresponding forms of love and friendship. There are therefore three kinds of friendship, equal in number to the things that are lovable".

**1156b7-12** (the highest — friendship of the good / virtue):
> "Perfect friendship is the friendship of men who are good, and alike in virtue; for these wish well alike to each other qua good, and they are good in themselves. Now those who wish well to their friends for their sake are most truly friends".

**1166a31-32** (a friend is another self):
> "and he is related to his friend as to himself (for his friend is another self)".

**1170b6-7** (a friend is another self — second occurrence):
> "and if as the virtuous man is to himself, he is to his friend also (for his friend is another self)".

(Also 1169b6-7, the "another self furnishes what a man cannot provide" line:)
> "while a friend, being another self, furnishes what a man cannot provide by his own effort".

### j. Contemplation as the highest happiness (Book 10)

**1177a12-17** (happiness = activity of the best thing in us):
> "If happiness is activity in accordance with virtue, it is reasonable that it should be in accordance with the highest virtue; and this will be that of the best thing in us."

**1177a17-18** (this activity is contemplative — perfect happiness):
> "the activity of this in accordance with its proper virtue will be perfect happiness. That this activity is contemplative we have already said."

**1177b31-1178a2** (make ourselves immortal):
> "But we must not follow those who advise us, being men, to think of human things, and, being mortal, of mortal things, but must, so far as we can, make ourselves immortal, and strain every nerve to live in accordance with the best thing in us".

**1178b7-8** (perfect happiness is a contemplative activity):
> "But that perfect happiness is a contemplative activity will appear from the following consideration as well."

**1178b32** (happiness must be some form of contemplation):
> "Happiness, therefore, must be some form of contemplation."

### k. External goods + luck (the caveat)

**1099a31-b2** (needs external goods):
> "Yet evidently, as we said, it needs the external goods as well; for it is impossible, or not easy, to do noble acts without the proper equipment."

(Continues, 1099b2-6:)
> "In many actions we use friends and riches and political power as instruments; and there are some things the lack of which takes the lustre from happiness, as good birth, goodly children, beauty; for the man who is very ugly in appearance or ill-born or solitary and childless is not very likely to be happy".

**1100a5-9** (Priam — great misfortune can wreck a life):
> "the most prosperous may fall into great misfortunes in old age, as is told of Priam in the Trojan Cycle; and one who has experienced such chances and has ended wretchedly no one calls happy."

**1101a6-8** (Priam again — em dash in original):
> "the happy man can never become miserable—though he will not reach blessedness, if he meet with fortunes like those of Priam."

## DELIVERABLE 3 — ADVERSARIAL FACT-CHECK

### 3.1 "We are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act, but a habit." — DURANT, NOT ARISTOTLE

CONFIRMED: this is Will Durant's paraphrase, not Aristotle. From *The Story of
Philosophy* (1926), Ch. II "Aristotle and Greek Science," §VII "Ethics and the
Nature of Happiness," **p. 76** in the standard Simon & Schuster / Pocket Books
reprint (page varies by printing — 87 and 103 appear in other editions). Durant's
actual sentence, in context:

> "Excellence is an art won by training and habituation: we do not act rightly
> because we have virtue or excellence, but we rather have these because we have
> acted rightly; 'these virtues are formed in man by his doing the actions'; we
> are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an act but a habit: 'the
> good of man is a working of the soul in the way of excellence in a complete
> life ... for as it is not one swallow or one fine day that makes a spring, so
> it is not one day or a short time that makes a man blessed and happy.'"

Key points for honest use:
- The famous sentence ("we are what we repeatedly do. Excellence, then, is not an
  act but a habit") sits OUTSIDE Durant's quotation marks — it is his own
  connective prose, NOT a translation of any single Aristotle sentence.
- The phrases Durant *did* quote (in single quotes) are loose renderings of
  Aristotle: "these virtues are formed in man by his doing the actions" maps to
  NE 2.1-2.4 (the habituation argument, ~1103a-b); "the good of man is a working
  of the soul ... one swallow ..." maps to NE 1.7 (~1098a, the function argument
  and the swallow line).
- The nearest ACTUAL Aristotle: the habit material at 1103a31-b2 ("we become just
  by doing just acts...") and 1103b6-25 (states of character arise from like
  activities; "all the difference"); and the hexis doctrine — virtue is a settled
  *state*, not a mere act — at NE 2.4-2.6 (~1105a-1106a). Durant's line distills
  that hexis doctrine but he did not cite 1105b.
- Common meme variant inserts a comma ("not an act, but a habit") and swaps "then"
  for "therefore" — both are later smoothings.
- Named-expert confirmation: Richard Kraut (Northwestern) — "Those aren't
  Aristotle's words translated into English. But it is an accurate and succinct
  way of conveying what Aristotle says in a more complicated way." Jonathan
  Buttaci (Catholic Univ. of America) — "This is a well-known misattribution.
  It's Aristotelian in spirit but not [Aristotle's actual words]."
- Sources: Wikiquote *Aristotle* (Misattributed) https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Aristotle ;
  Check Your Fact (verdict False, 2019) https://checkyourfact.com/2019/06/26/fact-check-aristotle-excellence-habit-repeatedly-do/ ;
  WIST https://wist.info/aristotle/1334/ ; Snopes (paywalled)
  https://www.snopes.com/fact-check/quote-habit-excellence-aristotle/

### 3.2 Is "eudaimonia = happiness" misleading?

Yes — and the post should say so. The scholarly consensus:
- Modern English "happiness" connotes a subjective mood you cannot be wrong about
  ("if I think I am happy, then I am"). Aristotle's eudaimonia is an OBJECTIVE
  condition of a whole life going well — assessable from outside, and something
  you can be mistaken about (SEP, *Virtue Ethics*: "I might think I was
  flourishing but be wrong").
- Etymology: eu ("well") + daimon ("spirit/divinity") = literally "good-spirited"
  / watched over by a good guardian spirit (SEP, *Aristotle's Ethics*). Worth
  flagging: Aristotle himself "never calls attention to this etymology" and treats
  eudaimon as a substitute for *eu zen* ("living well") — so the etymology is
  background colour, not load-bearing in his argument.
- Aristotle's OWN gloss (NE 1.4, 1095a18-20): both the many and the cultivated
  identify the good with eudaimonia and treat "living well" (**eu zen**, εὖ ζῆν)
  and "doing well / faring well" (**eu prattein**, εὖ πράττειν) as the same as
  being eudaimon. So eudaimonia is closer to "living-and-doing well" than to a
  feeling.
- It is an ACTIVITY (energeia) of soul in accordance with virtue, over a COMPLETE
  life ("one swallow," 1098a18). Not a transient feeling, and not a dormant state:
  a mere disposition can sit idle in someone asleep (NE 1.8, ~1098b-1099a), which
  is exactly why Aristotle chooses *energeia* (active exercise) over *hexis* (mere
  possessed state) for happiness. The Solon "count no man happy until he is dead"
  motif (1.10) only makes sense because eudaimonia is a life-shaped achievement.
- Best honest framing (per SEP): "each translation has its disadvantages" — gloss
  the Greek directly rather than pick one English word.
- Sources: SEP *Aristotle's Ethics* https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle-ethics/ ;
  SEP *Virtue Ethics* https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/ethics-virtue/

### 3.3 Inclusive-end vs dominant-end (intellectualist) tension — NAME IT, don't smooth it

The genuine fault line in the text:
- Book 1 (function argument ~1098a; finality + self-sufficiency ~1097b) reads as
  an INCLUSIVE end: happiness is the whole active life of virtue, the most "final"
  and "self-sufficient" good, "lacking in nothing" — seemingly containing all
  intrinsic goods (moral virtue, friendship, the civic life).
- Book 10.7-8 (~1177a-1178a) elevates *theoria* (contemplation, the activity of
  *nous*) as *teleia eudaimonia* — the most complete/perfect happiness — and
  demotes the moral-virtue life to happiness "in a secondary degree" (1178a). That
  reads as a single DOMINANT end.
- The vocabulary "inclusive end" vs "dominant end" is W. F. R. Hardie's ("The
  Final Good in Aristotle's Ethics," *Philosophy*, 1965; *Aristotle's Ethical
  Theory*, 1968). Hardie thought Aristotle conflated the two.
- INCLUSIVIST camp: **J. L. Ackrill**, "Aristotle on Eudaimonia" (1974 British
  Academy lecture; repr. in Rorty, *Essays on Aristotle's Ethics*, 1980) — the
  classic statement. Reads "most final" + self-sufficient as the all-embracing end
  comprising every intrinsic good; a whole life "worthwhile all the way through."
- DOMINANT / INTELLECTUALIST camp: **Richard Kraut**, *Aristotle on the Human
  Good* (1989) — sustained defense that happiness strictly IS excellent rational
  (contemplative) activity, grounded in Aristotle's "imitate the divine" theme
  (the gods' only activity is thinking). **Thomas Nagel**, "Aristotle on
  Eudaimonia" (in Rorty 1980) — frames it as "comprehensive vs intellectualist"
  and leans intellectualist (identify with the highest part, *nous*). **Anthony
  Kenny** is standardly grouped here.
- THE TELL FOR HONESTY: **John M. Cooper** changed his mind. *Reason and Human
  Good in Aristotle* (1975) resisted the strict Book-10 reading; "Contemplation
  and Happiness: A Reconsideration" (*Synthese* 72, 1987) reversed and accepted
  contemplation as the primary/dominant good. A leading Aristotelian switching
  sides on the same evidence is the cleanest proof this is unsettled.
- Harmonizing third way: **Gabriel Richardson Lear**, *Happy Lives and the Highest
  Good* (2004) — moral virtue is "for the sake of" / an approximation of the
  contemplative ideal, not a mere tool and not an independent co-component. SEP
  adopts a similar line (you need the ethical virtues to *live* the philosopher's
  life), while noting "some scholars have held that X.7-8 are deeply at odds with
  the rest of the Ethics."
- Why unresolved: the text genuinely says both; the function argument's "best and
  most complete virtue" is ambiguous (full set vs single highest); it turns on
  hard calls about developmental chronology and how literally to read the "divine"
  passages.
- Sources: SEP *Aristotle's Ethics* (§§2,10) https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle-ethics/ ;
  Ackrill via PhilPapers https://philpapers.org/rec/ACKAOE-2 ; Kraut
  https://philpapers.org/rec/KRAAOT-7 ; Cooper 1987
  https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF00413638

### 3.4 The bootstrapping / circularity worry

(a) "How do you do just acts before you're just?" Aristotle raises it himself
(NE 2.4, ~1105a17-b18) and answers by distinguishing the ACT from the AGENT'S
STATE. An action is "just" merely by being the sort of thing a just person does
(right deed, right object). But a person does it JUSTLY — "as the just person does
it" — only when (1) he KNOWS what he's doing, (2) CHOOSES it and chooses it for
its own sake, and (3) acts from a FIRM and unchangeable character (1105a31-33). The
learner's deeds meet the first description but not the rest; guided HABITUATION
turns repeated right actions into the settled state. Causation runs acts -> state,
so no vicious loop. Craft analogy: you learn to build by building. (Live nuance:
Burnyeat, "Aristotle on Learning to Be Good," in Rorty 1980 — the learner grasps
"the that" (that such acts are noble) before "the why," and comes to find virtuous
action pleasant; so habituation is a cognitive-affective apprenticeship, not blind
drill.)

(b) The doctrine-of-the-mean circle: virtue is the mean "as determined by ... the
man of practical wisdom" (1106b36-1107a2), yet phronesis seems defined by hitting
the mean / reasoning well about the good. Standard response: it is a SUBSTANTIVE
INTERDEPENDENCE (a hermeneutic circle), not a vicious one, because virtue of
character and phronesis co-develop and mutually require each other (NE 6.13,
~1144b: you cannot be fully good without phronesis nor have phronesis without
virtue of character; virtue "makes the goal right," phronesis "makes right the
things toward the goal"). And the doctrine is explicitly NOT a decision procedure
— Aristotle insists no formula can compute the mean; the doctrine does explanatory
work, so "no algorithm" is a feature, not a failure. (Hursthouse: a naive "aim
between extremes" reading is "extremely silly"; defenders reply that's a faulty
interpretation — the mean is about feeling passions appropriately, which is
substantive.)
- Sources: SEP *Aristotle's Ethics* (§§4, 5.2) ; Moss, "Virtue Makes the Goal
  Right," *Phronesis* 56 (2011)
  https://as.nyu.edu/content/dam/nyu-as/philosophy/documents/faculty-documents/moss/Moss_Virtue-Goal.pdf

### 3.5 Aristotle's elitism / the dark side (one honest paragraph's worth)

All in the POLITICS, not the Ethics (state this so readers don't think it's
misattributed):
- Natural slavery: *Politics* I.4-8, 13 — some are "slaves by nature," who
  "participate in reason only to the extent of perceiving it, but do not have it"
  (1254b16-23), lacking the deliberative faculty, allegedly better off ruled. SEP
  stresses Aristotle "asserts without evidence" that any actual people are natural
  slaves, and even concedes despotic rule mainly benefits the master.
- Women: *Politics* I.13, 1260a13-14 — women have the deliberative faculty but it
  is *akuron*, "without authority." PRECISION: render this as the deliberative
  part "lacks authority," NOT "women can't reason" — there is a live debate
  (Deslauriers, Karbowski) over whether *akuron* means deficient reason or merely
  lacks standing/legal force (from *kurios*, the male guardian).
- Leisure: the best life presupposes *schole* (leisure) freed from manual labour
  and necessity; *Politics* 1278a20 — "it is impossible to pursue the things of
  virtue when one lives the life of a vulgar person [banausos] or a laborer."
  Manual workers are barred from citizenship in the best regime. In his society
  that leisure rested on slaves and the subordination of women.
- DEFENSIBLE FRAMING: it's real, it's in the text, don't hide it; but the core
  apparatus (habituation, the mean, phronesis, friendship, the function argument)
  is WIDELY HELD to be separable from the prejudices, which live in the Politics'
  empirical claims about who has which capacities, not in the structure of the
  virtue theory. Note honestly that some critical/feminist scholars argue the
  elitism runs deeper (that ranking by "function" itself invites it) — so write
  "widely held to be separable," not "obviously separable." And don't claim a
  unanimous ancient consensus excuses it: Aristotle himself argued AGAINST
  contemporaries who held all slavery to be merely conventional and unjust
  (Politics I.3, I.6) — the question was contested in his own day.
- Sources: SEP *Aristotle's Political Theory* https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle-politics/ ;
  IEP *Aristotle: Politics* https://iep.utm.edu/aristotle-politics/

### 3.6 The most-quoted factoids — verified

- **eudaimonia often rendered "flourishing":** TRUE and mainstream (not fringe).
  Users/endorsers include John Cooper, Martha Nussbaum, Roger Crisp (Cambridge NE
  translator); SEP discusses it. CALIBRATION: say "frequently/commonly," not
  "always/correctly" — Terence Irwin's translation keeps "happiness," and Julia
  Annas defends "happiness" understood in the objective whole-life sense. "Living
  well" and "well-being" also circulate. "Flourishing" itself draws criticism for
  sounding biological/vegetative (plants flourish, but eudaimonia is only for
  rational beings — SEP *Virtue Ethics*).
- **arete = excellence, not just moral virtue:** TRUE. Arete is performing a
  thing's function (*ergon*) well, and applies to non-moral, non-human things.
  Aristotle's actual examples (NE 1106a15-22, verbatim confirmed): "the excellence
  of the eye makes both the eye and its work good ... Similarly the excellence of
  the horse makes a horse both good in itself and good at running ..." CALIBRATION:
  the KNIFE is a standard textbook illustration, NOT a verbatim Aristotle example —
  if used, attribute the eye and horse (which ARE in the text) as Aristotle's, and
  present the knife as the kind of case his logic licenses.
- **"ethics" from ethos = habit/character:** TRUE. English "ethics" <- Latin
  *ethica* <- Greek *ethike* (philosophia) <- *ethikos* ("of character") <- *ethos*
  (related to *ethos*, "habit/custom"); the word traces to Aristotle's *Ta Ethika*
  (etymonline). And Aristotle makes the pun himself, NE 2.1, 1103a17-18 (Ross,
  verbatim): "moral virtue comes about as a result of habit, whence also its name
  (ἠθική) is one that is formed by a slight variation from the word ἔθος (habit)."
  PRECISION: it's a pun on two NEAR-homonyms — ἦθος (ēthos, long e, "character")
  vs ἔθος (ethos, short e, "habit/custom"); keep both spellings distinct.
