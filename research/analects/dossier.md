# The Analects of Confucius: research dossier

Supporting `analects.html` (Approach A, the translation stack). The formal
deep-research workflow run for this post was rate-limited and returned nothing
usable, so this dossier is assembled from the primary sources actually used (the
Chinese Text Project's Chinese + Legge, and the translations parsed for the
corpus) plus standard scholarship. Every keystone below was verified against the
canonical text; quotations in the post are verbatim from the editions named in
`BUILD.md`.

## What it is, in one paragraph

The Analects (Chinese *Lunyu* 論語, "edited conversations") is not a book Confucius
(Kongzi, 551-479 BCE) wrote. It is roughly 500 short fragments, most opening
*zi yue* 子曰, "the Master said," compiled by his disciples and their disciples
over a span of perhaps two centuries after his death. It reads less like scripture
than like lecture notes. It is more a work of ethics and politics than of
religion: it says almost nothing about gods, the afterlife, or metaphysics, and a
great deal about how to be a good person, a good family member, and a good ruler.
Confucius presents himself as a conservator, not an innovator (7.1: "a transmitter
and not a maker"), recovering the lost virtue of the early Zhou.

## The spine (diagnosis and prescription)

- **Diagnosis:** a society falls apart when people abandon virtue and stop
  fulfilling the duties of their roles. Rulers who are not really rulers, fathers
  who are not really fathers; the names and the realities come apart (13.3), and
  with them, order.
- **Prescription:** self-cultivation that ripples outward. A person works on their
  own character; that makes a good family member; good families make a good state.
  The root is the family (1.2), the means is ritual and virtue rather than law and
  force (2.1, 2.3).
- **Attribution caution:** the famous explicit ladder ("cultivate the self,
  regulate the family, order the state, bring peace to all under Heaven") is **not
  in the Analects.** It is the eight steps of the *Great Learning* (Daxue), a short
  later text (a chapter of the Liji) that Zhu Xi canonized in the 12th century as
  one of the Four Books. The Analects has the seed of it, not the staircase. The
  post says so in the 1.2 breakdown.

## Keystones (verified, with canonical numbering)

Numbering follows the Chinese Text Project / Legge (503 passages). A few editions
shift by one in books 10, 11, 13, 15, 16, 17; the famous keystones below are stable.

| Ref | What it is | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | The opening: pleasure of learning, friends from afar, the *junzi* unbothered by being unknown | Introduces *junzi*. No cosmology; the book opens on ordinary life. |
| 1.2 | Filial and fraternal conduct as the *root* (本) of *ren* | Said by the disciple Youzi, not Confucius. The family is where virtue starts. |
| 2.1 | Govern by *de* (virtue), "like the pole star" | Virtue as gravity; the ruler holds still and the people orient to him. |
| 2.3 | Govern by virtue + ritual, not law + punishment | Law breeds evasion without shame (恥 *chi*); ritual breeds shame and self-correction. The keystone of Confucian political thought. |
| 2.4 | "At fifteen I set my heart on learning... at seventy I followed my heart's desire without overstepping" | The arc of a life; freedom as the fruit of lifelong discipline. *tian ming* 天命 at fifty. |
| 2.15 | "Learning without thought is labour lost; thought without learning is perilous" | Both halves needed. The translators barely diverge (plain Chinese). |
| 4.8 | "If a man hears the Way in the morning, he may die content in the evening" | The one fervent line. Lau rewrites it as an epitaph; Waley as a cry. |
| 4.15 | The "one thread": Zengzi glosses it as *zhong* (loyalty / doing one's best) and *shu* (reciprocity) | Confucius's positive self-summary, twin of 15.24. The answer is given by a disciple. |
| 6.30 | The full *ren* definition: wanting to stand, help others stand | The positive golden rule; the warmest definition of goodness. |
| 7.1 | "A transmitter and not a maker" | Confucius's self-image; conservative posture. Scholars note he in fact remade the words he claimed to pass on. |
| 12.1 | *ren* as self-mastery: "overcome the self and return to ritual" (克己復禮) | The most-debated *ren* passage; five translators give five English words for *ren*. |
| 12.2 | *ren* restated to a different disciple: the negative golden rule again | Same 8 characters as 15.24, proof it is load-bearing not a one-off. |
| 13.3 | The rectification of names (正名 *zhengming*) | If words stop matching reality, everything built on them fails. "Let the ruler be a ruler..." |
| 15.24 | THE self-summary: one word for life, *shu* 恕; "do not impose on others what you do not want" | The negative golden rule. The book's own pick for its center. |
| 17.2 | "By nature close, by practice far apart" (性相近也，習相遠也) | Nearly the only line on human *nature* (性 *xing*); left open, so Mencius and Xunzi could split over it. |
| 17.19 | "Does Heaven speak? ... the four seasons turn" | The clearest window on *tian*: an impersonal, mute, reliable order, not a personal god. |

Other genuinely load-bearing passages a serious reader might expect and that the
breakdowns or stack touch: 4.16 (junzi vs profit), 12.11 ("let the ruler be a
ruler," the rectification of names in practice), 12.19 (the wind and the grass),
2.7-2.8 (filial piety is more than feeding parents). They were left to the stack
rather than given full breakdowns, to keep the keystone set tight.

## The load-bearing untranslatables

Each is a single Chinese word with no English equivalent; the stack exists to show
the spread. Renderings below are the ones used by the translators in the corpus.

- **仁 ren** (character: 人 person + 二 two; goodness as what happens *between*
  people): *perfect / true virtue* (Legge), *Goodness* (Waley, Slingerland),
  *benevolence* (Lau), *humaneness* (Watson, Chin), *authoritative conduct* (Ames
  & Rosemont), *humaneness / the humane* (Muller), sometimes *manhood / humanity*
  (Pound). The single most contested word in the book.
- **君子 junzi** (lit. "ruler's son," an aristocrat by birth, which Confucius
  redefines as noble by character): *gentleman* (Waley, Lau, Slingerland), *superior
  man / man of complete virtue* (Legge), *exemplary person* (Ames & Rosemont),
  *noble man* (Muller). The democratic move of Confucianism lives in this word.
- **禮 li** (originally the rites of sacrifice; the character carries a ritual
  vessel): *rules of propriety* (Legge), *the rites* (Lau, Waley), *ritual*
  (Slingerland), *ritual propriety* (Ames & Rosemont), often left as *li* (Eno).
- **德 de** (virtue, but also power/charisma, the pull a good person exerts): *virtue*
  (most), *moral force* (Waley). Same word as the *Te* in the Tao Te Ching.
- **天 tian** (sky / Heaven, an impersonal ordering power, not a creator god):
  *Heaven* (most), and Waley notes that at 17.19 "Nature" would nearly fit.

## Translation politics (the chorus)

- **James Legge** (1893): the Victorian missionary-scholar standard; literal,
  heavily footnoted, "perfect virtue" for *ren*. Public domain; runs the full book.
- **Arthur Waley** (1938): the literary landmark, elegant and readable; *Goodness*,
  *ritual*, *gentleman*. Often called the best English Analects.
- **D. C. Lau** (1979, Penguin): the careful scholarly standard for a generation;
  *benevolence*, *the rites*; keeps *shu* in the Chinese.
- **Ezra Pound** (1951): the poet's idiosyncratic, compressed, sometimes eccentric
  version; etymologizing, *manhood / humanity* for *ren*.
- **Simon Leys** (1997, Norton): literary and humane, with rich essayistic notes.
- **Edward Slingerland** (2003, Hackett): the modern classroom standard, paired with
  selections from the traditional Chinese commentaries; *Goodness*, *gentleman*.
- **Ames & Rosemont** (1998): the deliberately defamiliarizing "philosophical
  translation"; *authoritative conduct* (ren), *exemplary persons* (junzi),
  *ritual propriety* (li). Slingerland and others have criticized it as
  over-theorized and awkward; it is invaluable precisely because it refuses the
  comfortable English word and forces you to see *ren* as a practice, not a feeling.
- **Burton Watson** (2007, Columbia) and **Annping Chin** (2014, Penguin): clean
  modern readings, both using *humaneness* for *ren*.
- **A. Charles Muller** (rev. of 1990, free online) and **Robert Eno** (2015, free
  online teaching translation): two freely-distributed moderns; Eno leaves *Tian*,
  *li*, and *dao* untranslated on purpose.

## Provenance and stratification

The Analects was compiled over a long span; it is not a unified composition. The
Brooks & Brooks theory (*The Original Analects*, 1998) argues the books were added
in layers over roughly 230 years, with Book 4 the oldest core and the later books
(especially 16-20) the latest and least reliable. This is contested (many scholars
think the stratification cannot be dated so precisely), but the broad point is
widely accepted: the inner books (often 3-7 or 4-8) are considered closer to
Confucius, and the late books contain more legend, more system, and material that
sits oddly with the rest. The post notes this where it matters (e.g. 4.15's "one
thread" is glossed by a disciple, and 7.1's transmitter claim).

Religion vs philosophy: best framed as an ethical-political philosophy with a
religious dimension (ritual, ancestor veneration, a reverent but impersonal
*tian*). Confucius "did not speak of prodigies, force, disorder, or gods" (7.21),
and deflected questions about death ("you do not yet understand life; how can you
understand death?", 11.12).

## Steelman the uncomfortable parts

The post should not airbrush these:

- **Hierarchy and conservatism.** The system is built on ranked relationships
  (ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend) and on
  reviving an idealized past. A modern reader is right to be wary of an ethic that
  ties goodness to knowing your place.
- **Filial obedience vs. remonstrance.** Filial piety (*xiao*) can read as a charter
  for obeying authority. But Confucius explicitly required a son to remonstrate
  with a wrong father, gently and without resentment (4.18); the *Xiaojing* and
  later texts insist a minister must remonstrate with a wrong ruler. Obedience was
  never meant to be unconditional. The post makes this point at 1.2.
- **Women.** The notorious line is 17.25: "women and *xiaoren* (small/petty people)
  are hard to handle." It is genuinely sexist on its face; defenders argue about
  the scope of the terms, but the honest move is to name it as a real limit of the
  text and its age, not explain it away.
- **Political uses.** Confucianism became imperial orthodoxy and the basis of the
  civil-service examination system for ~1,300 years, which both spread literacy and
  ossified into rote orthodoxy; it has been invoked by modern states (including the
  PRC's Confucius Institutes) for legitimacy. The teaching is not responsible for
  every use, but the uses are part of the story.
