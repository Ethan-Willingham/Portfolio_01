# Meditations: Greek Terms & Attributions — fact-check reference

Scholarly reference for the Marcus Aurelius / *Meditations* post. Built for a
skeptical, well-read audience (Hacker News, people who actually read Stoicism).
Every quote here is verbatim and cited; uncertainty is flagged explicitly.

**Translations used for verbatim quotation in this file:**
- George Long (1862), public domain — the full text lives in this folder as
  `long-full.txt`; all Marcus passages below are quoted from it unless noted.
- Gregory Hays (2002, Modern Library), in copyright — only short, attributed
  keystone lines, cross-checked in `hays.md`.
- Thomas Wentworth Higginson (Epictetus *Enchiridion*), public domain, via Perseus.

**One headline correction up front (a credibility landmine in the prompt):**
the famous "it's not things that disturb us, but our judgments about them" maxim
is **Epictetus**, and the single Marcus passage closest to it (*Meditations* 8.47)
does **not** use the word ὑπόληψις (*hypolepsis*). It uses κρῖμα (*krima*) and
δόγμα (*dogma*). ὑπόληψις is the word behind Marcus's *separate* "everything is
opinion" doctrine (2.15, 4.3, 12.x). Details in the glossary entry for ὑπόληψις
and in Attribution A. Do not write "hypolepsis is the word behind 'it's not
things that disturb us.'" That conflates two different threads.

## 1. Greek term glossary

Format per term: **Greek (polytonic) — transliteration** · gloss · function in
Marcus/Stoicism and how translators render it.

Transliterations give long-vowel marks (ē, ō) where a skeptical classicist would
expect them; the barer spellings (hegemonikon, hypolepsis) are also standard.

### ἡγεμονικόν — *hēgemonikon* (also *hegemonikon*)
**The ruling / commanding faculty of the soul; the directing mind.**
In Stoic psychology the mind is monistic (no Platonic warring "parts"): the
*hēgemonikon* is the single rational center where impressions arrive, where
*assent* is granted or withheld, and from which impulse and desire issue. It is
the one faculty that can take a critical stance toward its own impressions. SEP:
"Assent is a faculty found only in ... the ruling part or commanding faculty
(*hêgemonikon*)." Marcus constantly addresses and drills his own *hēgemonikon*
as the part of himself he can actually govern. **Long** renders it "the ruling
faculty" / "ruling part"; **Hays** renders it "the directing mind" / "your mind."
[SEP Stoicism §"The Soul"]

### προαίρεσις — *prohairesis*
**Moral choice / volition / the faculty of choice.**
**Central to Epictetus, not to Marcus.** For Epictetus *prohairesis* is the
faculty of choice and assent that is uniquely "up to us" and "by nature
unimpeded" — the seat of human freedom and the thing the whole *Enchiridion*
trains. SEP-Epictetus treats it as one of his two organizing ideas and (following
A.A. Long) renders it **"volition"** (also "choice," "moral character," "will").
Marcus uses the term far less and does not build on it; his functional
equivalent is the governed *hēgemonikon* plus *dogma*/*hypolepsis*. (Contrast
with Aristotle, whose *prohairesis* is deliberate choice *of means* toward an
end; Epictetus narrows the field so that *prohairesis itself* is the only thing
up to us.) [SEP Epictetus]

### ὑπόληψις — *hypolēpsis* (also *hupolepsis*) — KEY TERM, with correction
**Judgment / opinion / supposition — the interpretive "take" you add to a bare
impression.** (Literally "a taking up," from ὑπολαμβάνω.)
This is the word behind Marcus's **"everything is opinion"** doctrine, e.g.:
- 4.3 (Greek, verbatim): **ὁ κόσμος ἀλλοίωσις, ὁ βίος ὑπόληψις** — "the universe
  is change, life is opinion."
- 2.15 (Greek, verbatim): **ὅτι πᾶν ὑπόληψις** — "that everything is opinion"
  (Marcus quoting Monimus the Cynic).
- 12.22 / 12.26: the "everything is opinion, and opinion is in your power" cluster.

**Correction to the prompt's premise.** The single passage most often quoted as
"it's not things that disturb us but our judgments about them" is **8.47**, and
there Marcus does **not** use *hypolepsis*. The Greek of 8.47 is
**"Εἰ μὲν διά τι τῶν ἐκτὸς λυπῇ, οὐκ ἐκεῖνό σοι ἐνοχλεῖ, ἀλλὰ τὸ σὸν περὶ αὐτοῦ
κρῖμα ... τίς ὁ κωλύων διορθῶσαι τὸ δόγμα;"** — i.e. it uses **κρῖμα** (*krima*,
"judgment") and **δόγμα** (*dogma*, "judgment / principle"). The near-verbatim
one-liner with that exact structure belongs to **Epictetus**, *Enchiridion* 5,
where the word is **δόγματα** (*dogmata*). κρίσις (*krisis*, "judging,
discrimination") is a related word but is **not** the one in 8.47.
Honest mapping: *hypolepsis* → Marcus's "life is opinion" doctrine; *krima* +
*dogma* → Marcus's disturbance passage (8.47); *dogmata* → the canonical
Epictetus one-liner. English translators flatten all of these to "judgment(s)"
or "opinion(s)," which is exactly why the Greek distinction disappears.
Translators render *hypolepsis* as "opinion," "judgment," or "supposition."
[Greek of 4.3 and 2.15: Wikiquote (on-page polytonic). Greek of 8.47: confirmed
across Perseus-indexed text + maurelius.app original-text + WIST/Hard.]

### τὰ ἐφ' ἡμῖν / τὰ οὐκ ἐφ' ἡμῖν — *ta eph' hēmin / ouk eph' hēmin*
**What is up to us / what is not up to us — the "dichotomy of control."**
**Foundational to Epictetus**; the literal opening of *Enchiridion* 1:
**"Τῶν ὄντων τὰ μέν ἐστιν ἐφ' ἡμῖν, τὰ δὲ οὐκ ἐφ' ἡμῖν"** ("Of things, some are
up to us and some are not"). Up to us: opinion, impulse, desire, aversion (our
own acts); not up to us: body, property, reputation, office. (The apostrophe
marks elision: ἐπί → ἐφ' before the rough breathing of ἡμῖν, with π aspirating to
φ.) Note for skeptics: **"dichotomy of control" is a modern label** (popularized
by W.B. Irvine, 2009), not an ancient phrase; "dichotomy of agency" is the more
defensible gloss, since Epictetus's point is what is and isn't our own *doing*
(*prohairesis*). [SEP Epictetus; Dickinson College Commentaries, Epictetus glossary]

### ἀπάθεια — *apatheia*
**Freedom from passion — NOT "apathy."**
It is freedom from the *pathē* (the four destructive passions: distress, fear,
appetite, pleasure), achieved by correcting the false value-judgments that
produce them. It is emphatically not absence of all feeling: the Stoic sage
still has **εὐπάθειαι** (*eupatheiai*, "good feelings" — joy, caution, rational
wish) and involuntary **προπάθειαι** (*propatheiai*, "pre-emotions," the first
flinch). SEP notes the *eupatheiai* "belie the caricature of the Sage as an
unfeeling robot." Contrast with *ataraxia*: *apatheia* = freedom from *passion*;
*ataraxia* = freedom from *disturbance*. [SEP Stoicism §"Ethics"; Wikipedia "Apatheia"]

### ἀταραξία — *ataraxia*
**Untroubledness / tranquility / freedom from disturbance.**
A **cross-school** term: it is the explicit *telos* (goal) for **Epicureans**
and for **Pyrrhonist skeptics** (who reach it by suspending judgment, *epochē*).
For the **Stoics** the *telos* is virtue / living in agreement with nature, and
*apatheia* and *ataraxia* are *byproducts* of right judgment rather than the
thing aimed at directly. Useful framing: Stoic tranquility is downstream of
virtue, not pursued for its own sake. [IEP Stoicism; Wikipedia "Ataraxia"]

### εὐδαιμονία — *eudaimonia*
**Flourishing / living well / "the good life" — better than "happiness."**
*eudaimonia* is an objective condition of a well-lived life (exercising one's
rational nature excellently), not a subjective mood, so "flourishing" or
"well-being" is the standard scholarly preference and "happiness" is widely
considered misleading. **Sourcing caveat (flag):** this is genuine consensus,
but do **not** footnote it to the SEP as if SEP declares "happiness" wrong — the
SEP *Stoicism* and *Aristotle's Ethics* entries actually use "happiness" and
"flourishing" interchangeably. Attribute the "flourishing not happiness" point
to general consensus / Britannica, or hedge it. [Britannica "eudaimonia";
cf. SEP Aristotle's Ethics, which is hedged]

### λόγος — *logos*
**Reason — both the rational ordering principle of the cosmos and the human
share in it.** In Stoic physics an immanent, divine, rational principle (*logos*,
identified with God / Zeus / "designing fire" / *pneuma* / the common law)
pervades and orders the cosmos. Each human mind holds a fragment of that same
*logos*, so "living according to nature" means attuning one's own reason to the
reason of the whole. Marcus leans on this heavily (the recurring "the reason
common to all," and *logos spermatikos*, "seminal reason"). [SEP Stoicism §"Physics"]

### φύσις — *physis*
**Nature — both cosmic nature and human (rational) nature.**
The Stoic *telos* is "to live in agreement with nature," the fuller formula being
**ὁμολογουμένως τῇ φύσει ζῆν** (*homologoumenōs tēi physei zēn*; Zeno's barer
version drops τῇ φύσει). It means **both** at once: the rational nature of the
cosmos *and* one's own rational/human nature, which coincide for Stoics because
human reason is a piece of cosmic reason. [SEP Stoicism §"Ethics"]

### οἰκείωσις — *oikeiōsis* (also *oikeiosis*)
**"Appropriation" / "affiliation" / "familiarization"** — recognizing things as
*oikeion* ("one's own," "belonging"). The Stoic developmental doctrine: every
animal begins with self-*oikeiōsis* (the instinct to preserve its own
constitution), and in rational adults this concern **widens outward** — to
offspring, family, fellow citizens, and ultimately **all humankind** (the root
of Stoic cosmopolitanism, and of Marcus's "made for cooperation" passages). The
concentric **"circles"** image (self → family → kin → townsfolk → countrymen →
all humanity, drawn inward) is specifically **Hierocles** (2nd c. CE Stoic),
preserved in Stobaeus — not from the SEP page itself. Translators: "appropriation,"
"affiliation," "orientation," "endearment." [SEP Stoicism §"Ethics"; Wikipedia
"Hierocles (Stoic)"]

### καθῆκον — *kathēkon* (plural καθήκοντα *kathēkonta*)
**Appropriate / befitting action; "proper function," loosely "duty."**
Coined by Zeno; **Cicero translated it *officium*** (hence English "duty," via
*De Officiis*). "Appropriate action" / "proper function" is the better gloss,
since "duty" imports later Kantian baggage. Contrast with **κατόρθωμα**
(*katorthōma*, "right / perfect action"): a *kathēkon* is a reasonable, fitting
act anyone can do and justify; a *katorthōma* is that same act performed by the
sage *from* virtue and complete understanding. Same outward act, different inner
state; only the sage's is fully "right." Cicero's pairing: *medium officium*
(= *kathēkon*) vs *perfectum officium / recte factum* (= *katorthōma*).
[SEP Stoicism §"Ethics"; Wikipedia "Kathekon"]

### Τὰ εἰς ἑαυτόν — *Ta eis heauton* (the title)
**"To Himself" / "Things to Himself"** — private notes addressed to oneself.
Three confirmed points: (i) *Ta eis heauton* is the **conventional Greek title**
under which the work circulates and is edited; (ii) **Marcus gave the work no
title** — it was almost certainly never meant for publication, so both *Ta eis
heauton* and the English *Meditations* are later/posthumous labels; (iii) the
striking fact is true — **a Roman emperor wrote his private philosophical journal
in Greek, not Latin.** Greek was the prestige language of philosophy in the
Greco-Roman world; his chief influence, Epictetus, taught and wrote in Greek,
and the core Stoic corpus was Greek. (Polytonic: capital tau with grave Τὰ;
rough breathing + circumflex on ἑαυτόν.) [Wikipedia "Meditations"; IEP "Marcus
Aurelius"]

## 2. Attribution corrections

### A. "Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views they take of them"
**VERDICT: EPICTETUS, not Marcus.** It is *Enchiridion* (Handbook) **chapter 5**,
one of the most misattributed lines in Stoicism. Verified against the primary
text at Perseus. The Higginson translation reads, verbatim:

> "Men are disturbed not by things, but by the views which they take of things."

(The common "of them" variant vs. "of things" is just translation drift.) Greek,
verbatim: **"ταράσσει τοὺς ἀνθρώπους οὐ τὰ πράγματα, ἀλλὰ τὰ περὶ τῶν πραγμάτων
δόγματα"** — note the word is **δόγματα** (*dogmata*). This line is the historical
seed of CBT/REBT (Albert Ellis credited Epictetus; Aaron Beck traced cognitive
therapy's "philosophical origins" to the Stoics). It predates Marcus and gets
cross-credited to him because Epictetus was Marcus's major influence.

**Marcus's own versions of the same idea** (verbatim, George Long 1862, from
`long-full.txt`):

- **4.7** — "Take away thy opinion, and then there is taken away the complaint,
  'I have been harmed.' Take away the complaint, 'I have been harmed,' and the
  harm is taken away."
  *Gist:* drop the value-judgment "I've been harmed" and the harm itself
  dissolves. (Hays renders this famous line as "Choose not to be harmed — and you
  won't feel harmed. Don't feel harmed — and you haven't been," confirmed in
  `hays.md`.)

- **8.47** — "If thou art pained by any external thing, it is not this thing that
  disturbs thee, but thy own judgment about it. And it is in thy power to wipe out
  this judgment now."
  *Gist:* the closest Marcus parallel to the Epictetus line. **Greek uses κρῖμα
  ("judgment") then δόγμα, NOT ὑπόληψις** — see the glossary correction above.

- **12.22** — "Consider that everything is opinion, and opinion is in thy power.
  Take away then, when thou choosest, thy opinion, and like a mariner who has
  doubled the promontory, thou wilt find calm, everything stable, and a waveless
  bay."
  *Gist:* everything is "opinion" (ὑπόληψις), and opinion is within your power, so
  dropping it brings calm. This is the *hypolepsis* / "everything is opinion"
  thread, not the κρῖμα disturbance line.

- **12.26** — "When thou art troubled about anything, thou hast forgotten ...
  that all things happen according to the universal nature ... that a man's
  wrongful act is nothing to thee ... [and] that everything is opinion."
  *Gist:* a checklist of the things you forgot when you let yourself be troubled,
  ending again on "everything is opinion."

So: the punchy maxim is Epictetus (*Ench.* 5); Marcus restates the idea in his own
words at 4.7, 8.47, 12.22, and 12.26. All four Marcus cites confirmed verbatim in
`long-full.txt`. [Epictetus: Perseus *Enchiridion* 5. Marcus: Long 1862.]

### B. "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."
**VERDICT: GENUINE Marcus, *Meditations* 5.20 — and the famous wording is
Gregory Hays's (2002).** Confirmed in `hays.md` (corroborated across two
independent sources that name Hays) and on Wikiquote at Book V.20. This punchy
phrasing is **Hays's**, not the ancient text: George Long's 1862 rendering of the
same passage (verbatim from `long-full.txt`) is

> "the mind converts and changes every hindrance to its activity into an aid; and
> so that which is a hindrance is made a furtherance to an act; and that which is
> an obstacle on the road helps us on this road."

Same idea, none of the slogan punch — so the meme-famous version is specifically
Hays's. **Ryan Holiday's bestseller *The Obstacle Is the Way* (2014) draws its
title and epigraph directly from this passage** (Wikipedia states this plainly).
[Hays wording: `hays.md` + Wikiquote V.20. Long wording: `long-full.txt`.
Holiday: Wikipedia "The Obstacle Is the Way."]

### C. "Waste no more time arguing about what a good man should be. Be one."
**VERDICT: GENUINE Marcus, *Meditations* 10.16 — but this exact wording is a
popular paraphrase, and it is NOT Hays.** The passage is real; George Long (1862,
`long-full.txt`) renders it:

> "No longer talk at all about the kind of man that a good man ought to be, but be
> such."

Hays's actual 10.16 is "To stop talking about what the good man is like, and just
be one." So the punchy "Waste no more time arguing ... Be one." is **neither Long
nor Hays verbatim** — it circulates widely (Daily Stoic and others) **without a
translator attribution**, and its exact source could **not** be pinned to any one
published translation. **Flag for the post:** treat it as a popularized paraphrase
of an authentic passage (10.16), not as a verbatim quotation, and do not credit it
to Hays. [Long: `long-full.txt`. Hays 10.16 + variants: Wikiquote / cross-checked.
Provenance of the punchy wording: unresolved — flagged.]

### D. Apocrypha — NOT in the Meditations

**(a) "You have power over your mind — not outside events. Realize this, and you
will find strength."**
**VERDICT: APOCRYPHAL / not in the Meditations.** Not found in any standard
translation. The most-cited candidate location — the opening of Book 5 in Hays —
is a verified dead end (Hays's 5.1 is the "getting out of bed in the morning"
passage about ants and bees, unrelated to this internal-vs-external framing). It
is best described as a modern paraphrase/mashup of genuinely Stoic themes (the
"dichotomy of control," which is more Epictetan than Marcan) that got attached to
Marcus (and sometimes to Einstein). **Origin unresolved (flag):** no first-print
source or dedicated debunk was found; it circulates uncited across post-2010 quote
sites. Confident it is not a verbatim *Meditations* quote; honest line for the post
is "no verifiable source; circulates uncited; not in the text." [Hays 5.1 text;
aggregated quote-investigation reports + r/Stoicism. No Quote Investigator entry
found — flagged.]

**(b) "Everything we hear is an opinion, not a fact. Everything we see is a
perspective, not the truth."**
**VERDICT: SPURIOUS / fake. Definitively not Marcus.** Confirmed fake by named
experts: **Donald Robertson** (*How to Think Like a Roman Emperor*) called it "a
fake quote," and **Stephen Hanselman** (co-author, *The Daily Stoic*) confirmed it
is inauthentic and reflects modern "alternative facts" preoccupations, not Marcus.
Also listed as fake by Gregory Sadler ("10 Definitely Fake Quotes Not From Marcus
Aurelius," it is #1) and Thomas Colligan (Modern Stoicism). **Likely basis:** a
loose expansion of genuine passages, usually traced to **2.15** (Marcus quoting
the Cynic **Monimus**: "everything is opinion" / **ὅτι πᾶν ὑπόληψις**) and **4.3**
("the universe is change, life is opinion"). Crucially, Marcus cites Monimus as a
*warning*, and Stoicism holds that reason *can* objectively judge whether an
opinion is correct — so the fake actively **misrepresents** Stoicism (it is also
self-refuting: if everything is opinion, so is that sentence). No reliable
pre-internet citation. [CheckYourFact 2019 (Robertson + Hanselman); Sadler "10
Definitely Fake Quotes"; Colligan, Modern Stoicism. Basis passages 2.15/4.3.]

## 3. Context facts (all confirmed; one placement caveat)

- **Reign 161–180 CE.** Marcus succeeded Antoninus Pius in 161 and reigned until
  his death in 180; he co-ruled with **Lucius Verus** (joint accession March 161)
  until Verus's death in **169**. [SEP "Marcus Aurelius"; Britannica "Lucius Verus"]

- **Written in the 170s, on the northern (Danube) frontier, during the
  Marcomannic Wars** against the Germanic **Marcomanni and Quadi**. SEP: written
  "during the last years of his military campaigns." Use "the 170s" — it is well
  supported; avoid over-precise single-year claims for individual books.
  [SEP "Marcus Aurelius"; Wikipedia "Meditations"]

- **Internal datelines exist — but edition placement differs (flag this).** The
  subscriptions are genuine: a heading naming the **Quadi / the river Granua**
  (Gran / Hron) and a heading **"At Carnuntum"** (a Roman camp on the Danube).
  *But which book each belongs to varies by edition.* Modern/editorial texts
  (Wikisource, Farquharson-style) print **"among the Quadi on the river Gran" as
  the title of Book 2** and **"At Carnuntum" as the title of Book 3** (and note
  that Carnuntum is the Book 3 title in the *editio princeps*). The Long/Gutenberg
  text instead prints the Granua note at the **end of Book 1** and the Carnuntum
  note at the **end of Book 2**. Wording also varies (Gran / Granua / the Hron;
  "among the Quadi" / "in the country of the Quadi"). **Safe phrasing for the
  post:** the subscriptions are "traditionally placed at the end of Book 1 / start
  of Book 3 and now generally taken as the headings of Books 2 and 3," and quote a
  named translator if quoting exact wording. [Wikisource Meditations Bk 2 & Bk 3
  (with editio-princeps footnote); Gutenberg Long ed.]

- **Private, never meant for publication, no addressee but himself.** Scholarly
  consensus; the Greek title *Ta eis heauton* = "to himself," and "the work has no
  official title." [SEP "Marcus Aurelius"; Wikipedia "Meditations"]

- **Rusticus introduced Marcus to Epictetus, at *Meditations* 1.7.** His Stoic
  teacher **Quintus Junius Rusticus** lent Marcus his own copy of Epictetus's
  discourses/memoirs (Arrian's record). Long 1.7 (verbatim, `long-full.txt`):
  Marcus thanks Rusticus "for being acquainted with the discourses of Epictetus,
  which he communicated to me out of his own collection." [Long 1862 / MIT
  Classics; SEP "Marcus Aurelius"; Donald Robertson]

- **First English translation: Meric (Méric) Casaubon, 1634**, titled *Marcus
  Aurelius Antoninus the Roman Emperor, his Meditations Concerning Himself*.
  [Wikipedia "Méric Casaubon"]

## Confidence & flags (for a hostile reader)

- **Highest-stakes correction (high confidence):** 8.47 uses **κρῖμα + δόγμα**,
  not ὑπόληψις; ὑπόληψις carries the separate "everything is opinion" doctrine
  (2.15 = ὅτι πᾶν ὑπόληψις, 4.3 = ὁ βίος ὑπόληψις, 12.22/12.26). Greek of 4.3 and
  2.15 confirmed on Wikiquote (polytonic on-page); Greek of 8.47 (Εἰ μὲν διά τι
  τῶν ἐκτὸς λυπῇ ... κρῖμα ... δόγμα) confirmed across the Perseus-indexed text,
  maurelius.app's original-text page, and the Hard translation (WIST). Not yet
  eyeballed against a print Loeb/Hard/Gill — rate ~95%, not the 100% a held
  primary text would give.
- **Quote C provenance:** "Waste no more time arguing ... Be one." is a paraphrase
  of a genuine passage (10.16); its exact translator is unpinnable. Don't call it
  Hays.
- **Quote D-a origin:** apocryphal/not-in-text is solid; a *first-appearance*
  source was not found. State only "no verifiable source; not in the text."
- **eudaimonia / SEP:** "flourishing, not happiness" is real consensus but SEP
  itself uses the terms interchangeably — cite Britannica/consensus, not SEP, for
  that specific claim.
- **"Dichotomy of control"** is a modern coinage (Irvine 2009), not an ancient
  phrase; "dichotomy of agency" is the more defensible gloss of *ta eph' hēmin*.
- **Hays wording** (5.20, 4.7, 10.16) is confirmed via Wikiquote + the corroborated
  `hays.md` rather than a clean read of the 2002 print text. Consistent across
  sources; for belt-and-suspenders certainty, check a physical/ebook Modern Library
  copy.
