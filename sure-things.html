Sure Things

Eighteen things we measure to impossible precision, or lean on every single day, and still cannot explain.

Drop your phone and it falls. Every time, for everyone, forever. You would call that proof, but in the strict sense there isn't any, and no one can even tell you what gravity is.

Once you notice that gap, you see it everywhere: things we have measured to impossible precision, or built our whole world on top of, that we still cannot explain or prove. Here are eighteen of the most astonishing. Every image below is real. Every fact is sourced. The phenomenon is never in doubt. Only the explanation is missing.

IThe Cosmos

The biggest, best-measured things, owed to causes we cannot see.

No. 01 · Gravity

We photographed a black hole. We still can't say what gravity is.

Einstein's equations predicted this ring of doomed light to the pixel. And when two black holes collided a billion light-years away, the ripple reached Earth and stretched the entire planet by less than a ten-thousandth the width of a proton, and our instruments felt it pass. Gravity is the best-tested idea in all of science. No one has ever explained the machinery behind it. Newton refused to even guess.

"I have not been able to discover the cause of those properties of gravity from phaenomena, and I frame no hypotheses."Isaac Newton, Principia, 1713

No. 02 · Dark Matter

Most of the universe is invisible, and we know only by its pull.

These are two galaxy clusters caught mid-collision. The pink is every atom we can see, all the stars and gas. The blue, mapped purely by how its gravity bends light, is where the mass actually is. The visible universe is the minority. The rest, 27% of everything, has never been caught, not one particle of it.

"It is a strange and mysterious universe. But that's fun."Vera Rubin, who found the evidence

No. 03 · Dark Energy

Every speck here is a galaxy. Something we can't name is blowing them apart.

This sliver of sky is as wide as a grain of sand held at arm's length, and looks empty to the eye. This long exposure found thousands of galaxies in it, and they are flying apart, faster every year, pushed by a "dark energy" that is 68% of the universe. Our best theory of its strength misses the real value by a factor of 1 followed by 120 zeros.

"...a glaring embarrassment."Sean Carroll, on the cosmological constant

No. 04 · The Big Bang

This is the oldest light there is. We can't tell you what came before it.

This is the entire sky, painted in microwaves: light set free 380,000 years after the beginning and still arriving. From its faint speckle we know the universe is 13.8 billion years old, and we read the temperature of the infant cosmos to millionths of a degree. But the theory describes only what came after. What banged, and why, it does not say.

"In its original form, the Big Bang theory never was a theory of the bang."Alan Guth, MIT

No. 05 · Cosmic Rays

A single invisible particle hit the sky with the punch of a thrown baseball.

In 1991, a detector in Utah caught one subatomic particle carrying 48 joules of energy, the wallop of a 90-mph fastball packed into something smaller than an atom. They named it the Oh-My-God particle. Decades later, there is nothing in the sky where it came from powerful enough to have thrown it.

"That's the mystery of this, what the heck is going on?"John Matthews, Telescope Array
IIThe Physical World

Everyday things, written to many decimals, working for reasons unknown.

No. 06 · Time

This clock won't lose a second in 30 billion years. We don't know what time is.

It would take more than twice the age of the universe for this machine to drift by a single second. We measure time more precisely than anything else in nature. And physics still cannot say what time actually is, why it flows in only one direction, or whether the future exists yet.

"The past is set in stone while the future can still be altered... all because of entropy."Sean Carroll, Scientific American

No. 07 · Quantum Mechanics

Reality runs on rules nobody understands. Your phone depends on them.

These colours are light interfering with itself, the same wave weirdness that, scaled down, lets one particle pass through two slits at once. Quantum theory predicts experiments to better than ten decimal places, the most accurate prediction in all of science, and it runs every microchip on Earth. And not one physicist can tell you what it means, or what happens the instant you look.

"I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics."Richard Feynman, 1965

No. 08 · Turbulence

Van Gogh painted turbulence 70 years before the math caught up.

From an asylum window in 1889, Van Gogh filled a sky with swirling eddies. A century later, physicists found that the way brightness mixes across those brushstrokes statistically matches the equations of turbulent flow. Turbulence is in every river, every jet, every cup of coffee, and a complete theory of it is still worth an unclaimed million-dollar prize.

"Nobody in physics has really been able to analyze it mathematically satisfactorily."Richard Feynman, Lectures on Physics

No. 09 · Glass

This glass has stood for 800 years. We can't fully explain that it's solid.

The stained glass of a cathedral and the fiber that carries the internet are the same strange state of matter: a liquid's jumbled, disordered structure, locked rigid in place. How a cooling liquid freezes solid without ever crystallizing into a neat pattern is, in the words of a Nobel laureate, the deepest unsolved problem in the ordinary physics of solids.

"The deepest and most interesting unsolved problem in solid state theory is probably... glass."Philip W. Anderson, Science, 1995

No. 10 · Superconductivity

We've levitated magnets on a mystery for forty years.

Chill certain materials enough and they carry electricity with exactly zero resistance, forever, and pin a magnet in the air. Every MRI machine and the Large Hadron Collider run on it. For the warmer kind discovered in 1986, there is still no agreed theory of why it happens.

"In 30 years people will still say that the theory is not understood."A.-M. Tremblay, Physics Today

No. 11 · Lightning

Lightning strikes three million times a day. We don't know how it starts.

It is the most energetic everyday event on the planet, and we have watched it for centuries. Yet the strongest electric fields ever measured inside a thundercloud are about ten times too weak to start a spark. How the bolt actually begins is unsolved.

"The most energetic process on the planet... and we have no idea how it works."Reported in Nautilus, 2024
IIIThe Living World

We can read a mind and steer by a magnetic field, and explain neither.

No. 12 · Consciousness

We can read a picture out of your brain. We can't say why it feels like anything.

These cells were drawn by hand around 1900 by Santiago Ramón y Cajal, squinting down a microscope. We have come far since: a scanner can now reconstruct the image a person is looking at from their brain activity alone. And nothing in all of neuroscience explains why any of that electrochemistry is accompanied by an experience on the inside at all.

"There is a whir of information-processing, but there is also a subjective aspect."David Chalmers, on the hard problem, 1995

No. 13 · Animal Compass

This bird sees Earth's magnetic field. We've never found the eye that does it.

A robin navigates thousands of miles by the planet's magnetic field, and the leading theory says it may literally see that field, through a quantum reaction in a protein in its eye. The behavior has been beyond doubt for half a century. The actual sensor has never been located.

"The primary sensory mechanism behind this remarkable feat is still unclear."Hore & Mouritsen, Annual Review of Biophysics, 2016

No. 14 · The Placebo Effect

Sugar pills work even when you know they're sugar.

The placebo effect is so reliable that every new drug on Earth must prove it beats it. The strangest part: in careful trials, patients told plainly that their pills are inert sugar, with no deception at all, still get measurably better, 59% versus 35% in one study. Why honesty doesn't break the spell, no one fully knows.

"The psychological mechanisms underlying... open label placebo are unclear."Kaptchuk & Miller, BMJ, 2018
IVThe Edge of Reason

Where proof itself runs out, and the machine that found these images.

No. 15 · The Bicycle

We've ridden bicycles for 200 years without knowing why they balance.

Let a moving bicycle go and it steers itself upright, recovering from a shove on its own. For a century, textbooks credited the spinning wheels' gyroscope, or the geometry of the front fork. In 2011 a bicycle built to cancel both stayed up anyway. Neither explanation was necessary, and no simple one has replaced them.

"We show that neither effect is necessary for self-stability."Kooijman et al., Science, 2011

No. 16 · Reality

Nothing you can ever observe can prove this is real.

This 1888 engraving shows a traveler reaching the edge of the sky and peering at the machinery beyond. It is the oldest worry in philosophy, and it has never been answered: from Descartes' demon to the modern simulation argument, no evidence your senses could ever gather can rule out that all of it is a flawless illusion.

"I shall suppose that some malicious, powerful, cunning demon has done all he can to deceive me."René Descartes, Meditations, 1641

No. 17 · The Limits of Proof

Some true things can never be proven. That itself was proven.

Mathematics is the homeland of certainty. Then in 1931 a 25-year-old named Kurt Gödel proved that any logical system big enough to do arithmetic must contain true statements it can never prove, including the statement that it will never contradict itself. Certainty has a hard edge, and we have an exact proof of where it lies.

Any rich-enough consistent system holds statements "which can neither be proved nor disproved" within it.on Gödel's incompleteness theorems

No. 18 · The Machine

We built a mind that works, and we can't explain how.

The neural networks behind modern AI, including the one that gathered these images and helped draft this very page, ace test after test. AlphaFold predicts the shape of proteins well enough to win a Nobel Prize. And no one, not even the people who build them, can fully explain why training them works as well as it does. We have made something like a mind, and added it to the long list of things we use every day and do not understand.

"No existing theory can guarantee that... gradient descent... can converge to the global minimum."Simon Du, Quanta, 2021

So drop your phone one more time, and watch it fall. You are looking at the most reliable, most precisely measured, least understood fact in all of science, and you have just walked past seventeen of its cousins. The edge of what we know is not some far frontier. It runs right through the middle of an ordinary day, through every clock and cathedral window and falling thing.

None of this means any of it is fake. The phenomena are as solid as facts get. It is the why, and the final proof, that runs out, and that running-out is exactly where every interesting question still lives.

A note on honesty: "we can't explain it" here means there is no scientific consensus, not that there are no ideas. Most of these have several competing theories and active research; a few, like the limits of proof or whether reality is real, are matters of logic no experiment can close. Every claim links to its source, and every quote is verbatim. Where a phenomenon is genuinely contested, the wording leans toward "no agreed explanation" rather than "no idea at all."
