Every Hour of Daylight, Visualized

From noon today to the death of the sun.

The tilt of the Earth's axis is 23.4 degrees. That one number controls the seasons, the midnight sun, polar night, and why Reykjavik and Singapore feel like different planets in December.

Drag the globe to spin it. Use the sliders to change the time of day, the day of the year, the axial tilt, and even the epoch. Hover over any point to see whether it is in sunlight or shadow.

What to look for

Set the day to late June. The entire Arctic stays sunlit as you move the hour slider through a full 24 hours. The Antarctic stays dark no matter what. At the equator, the terminator crosses at roughly the same two times regardless of the season.

Now change the day to late December. Everything inverts. The Arctic goes dark. Antarctica gets its midnight sun. Drag the slider slowly from June to December and watch the terminator tilt back and forth.

The tilt slider

This is where it gets interesting. Set the tilt to 0. The terminator runs straight north-south no matter what day it is. No seasons. Every place on Earth gets exactly 12 hours of daylight, every single day. Life would look very different.

Now crank it to 45. At the June solstice, everywhere north of 45°N gets 24-hour sunlight. At 90° tilt, the sun would circle directly overhead at the pole while the entire opposite hemisphere sits in darkness for months. Uranus actually does this; it is tilted 98°.

The epoch slider

Slide it left to rewind 4.5 billion years, back to when Earth formed. Slide it right and you pass through ice ages, mass extinctions, the rise of mammals, and eventually into a future where the sun brightens, the oceans boil, and the star itself swells into a red giant that consumes Mercury and Venus.

The epoch slider auto-adjusts the tilt to approximate what obliquity was (or will be) at that point in history. You can still override it by touching the tilt slider directly.

Some things worth stopping on:

2.4 billion years ago. The Great Oxygenation Event. Cyanobacteria pumped oxygen into the atmosphere and triggered the most extreme ice ages in Earth's history: Snowball Earth, where glaciers reached the equator.

540 million years ago. The Cambrian explosion. Complex animal life appears in a geological instant. The tilt was similar to today's.

250 million years ago. The Permian extinction. 95% of all species wiped out. Pangaea exists as a single supercontinent.

+1.1 billion years. The sun is 10% brighter. Enough to start boiling the oceans. Complex life ends.

+5.4 billion years. The sun, now a red giant, reaches its maximum radius. Its outer atmosphere may extend past Earth's orbit.

How it works

The globe is a standard HTML <canvas>. Each frame, it projects a grid of 4°×4° patches onto the sphere. Each patch is checked against simplified continent polygons to determine land or ocean, then shaded based on whether the sun is above the horizon at that point.

The day/night boundary (the terminator) is computed from the subsolar point: the spot on Earth where the sun is directly overhead. The subsolar latitude is the solar declination (a function of day-of-year and axial tilt), and the subsolar longitude depends on the time of day (15° per hour from noon). Every patch more than 90° from the subsolar point is in shadow. 

function sunlight(lat, lon, subLat, subLon) {
  // Dot product on unit sphere
  var dot = Math.sin(lat) * Math.sin(subLat) +
            Math.cos(lat) * Math.cos(subLat) *
            Math.cos(lon - subLon);
  if (dot > 0.05) return 1;  // day
  if (dot < -0.05) return 0; // night
  return (dot + 0.05) / 0.1;  // twilight
}

No WebGL, no Three.js. Just 2D canvas, some trigonometry, and a point-in-polygon test running 4,000 times per frame. It holds 60fps on any modern device.
