Every Hour of Daylight, Visualized

One number, 23.4 degrees, controls everything.

The tilt of the Earth's axis is 23.4 degrees. That single number controls the seasons, the midnight sun, polar night, and why Reykjavik and Singapore feel like different planets in December.

Things to try

Slide the hour through a full day. Watch the shadow sweep across the globe. It rotates 15 degrees every hour. On the night side, watch the city lights of Europe, India, and East Asia come into view.

Set the day to June 21. Tap on northern Norway (69°N). The daylight bar fills completely: 24 hours of continuous sun. Now tap on southern Argentina (55°S). Just 7 hours. Move the day to December 21 and the roles reverse.

Try the tilt buttons. At 22.1° (minimum obliquity), the seasons are slightly milder. At 24.5° (maximum), they are slightly more extreme. These are the real bounds of Earth's 41,000-year Milankovitch obliquity cycle. Then tap 45° and watch entire hemispheres plunge into polar night. Notice the Moon disappears: at that tilt, Earth never had a stabilizing moon.

The Milankovitch cycles.

Earth's orbit and axis wobble in three overlapping cycles. The obliquity (tilt) oscillates between 22.1° and 24.5° over 41,000 years. The eccentricity (orbit shape) varies over 100,000 and 413,000 years. The precession (axis direction) cycles every 26,000 years. Together, these drive the ice ages.

Why 23.4°?

The Moon. Without it, Earth's obliquity would wander chaotically between 0° and 85° over millions of years. Mars, which has no large moon, swings between 15° and 35°. The Moon locks Earth into a narrow, stable band.

The city lights

The night side uses NASA's Black Marble composite, built from the Suomi NPP satellite's day/night band sensor. Spin the globe at night and you can trace the outlines of India, the Nile Delta, the US Interstate system, and the dense corridors of Japan and Korea.

The brightest spots are not the biggest cities.

Some of the most intense light comes from oil and gas fields. The Bakken shale formation in North Dakota and the Siberian gas fields show up brighter than many major cities because of gas flaring.

Numbers worth knowing

Equinox (March 20 / September 22): Every point on Earth gets almost exactly 12 hours of daylight. "Equinox" literally means "equal night."

The equator: Gets 12 hours and 7 minutes of daylight every single day. Not exactly 12:00 because the sun is a disk, not a point, and atmospheric refraction bends light below the geometric horizon.

Fastest sunset on Earth: the equator.

Because the sun's path crosses the horizon nearly perpendicular, sunset takes only about 2 minutes. At the poles, a single "sunset" takes roughly two weeks.

Days used to be shorter.

620 million years ago, a day was about 21 hours long. The Moon's tidal braking adds roughly 2.3 milliseconds per century. Coral fossils from the Devonian period show about 400 daily growth bands per year, confirming shorter days.
