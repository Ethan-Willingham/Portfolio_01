Every Hour of Daylight, Visualized

One number, 23.4 degrees, controls everything.

The tilt of the Earth's axis is 23.4 degrees. That single number controls the seasons, the midnight sun, polar night, and why Reykjavik and Singapore feel like different planets in December.

Drag the globe to spin it. Scroll (or pinch) to zoom. Change the hour and day with the sliders. Hover over any point to see its daylight breakdown in the bar below.

Things to try

Slide the hour from 0 to 24. Watch the shadow sweep across the globe. It rotates 15 degrees every hour. At any given moment, roughly half the planet is in darkness.

Set the day to June 21 (summer solstice). Hover over northern Norway (69°N). The daylight bar fills completely: 24 hours of continuous sun. Now hover over southern Argentina (55°S). Just 7 hours. Move the day to December 21 and the roles reverse.

Zoom in. Scroll to zoom into the Himalayas, the Sahara, or the Amazon. The NASA Blue Marble texture holds up surprisingly well, and the bump mapping creates visible terrain relief at closer distances.

Why 23.4°?

The Moon. Without it, Earth's obliquity would wander chaotically between 0° and 85° over millions of years, driven by gravitational tugs from Jupiter and other planets. Mars, which has no large moon, swings between 15° and 35° and may have reached 60° in the past. The Moon locks Earth's tilt into a slow, gentle oscillation between 22.1° and 24.5° over a 41,000-year cycle.

The daylight bar

The gold bar below the sliders shows 24 hours for whatever latitude you are hovering over. Dark is night. Gold is day. The white line is the current hour. Hover over the equator and it is always split roughly 50/50. Hover over the Arctic in June and the bar fills entirely with gold.

Fastest sunset on Earth: the equator.

Because the sun's path crosses the horizon nearly perpendicular at the equator, sunset takes only about 2 minutes. In Scandinavia, the sun sets at a shallow angle and twilight can last over an hour. At the poles, a single "sunset" takes roughly two weeks as the sun slowly spirals below the horizon.

Numbers worth knowing

Equinox (March 20 / September 22): Every point on Earth gets almost exactly 12 hours of daylight. The terminator runs pole to pole. "Equinox" literally means "equal night."

Arctic Circle (66.6°N): Gets at least one 24-hour day of sun per year and one 24-hour day of darkness. At the pole itself, roughly 6 months of each.

The equator: Gets 12 hours and 7 minutes of daylight every single day. Not exactly 12:00 because the sun is a disk, not a point, and atmospheric refraction bends light below the geometric horizon.

Days used to be shorter.

620 million years ago, a day was about 21 hours long. The Moon's tidal braking is gradually slowing Earth's rotation, adding roughly 2.3 milliseconds per century. Coral fossils from the Devonian period show about 400 daily growth bands per year, confirming shorter days.

Solar noon is not 12:00. Due to Earth's elliptical orbit and axial tilt, solar noon drifts up to 16 minutes off clock noon depending on the time of year. This is described by the equation of time, which also explains why earliest sunset and latest sunrise do not fall on the winter solstice.

The specular glint.

Spin the globe so you are looking across a sunlit ocean at a low angle. You may see a bright spot: the sun reflecting off flat water toward the camera. This "ocean glint" is a real phenomenon that satellites use to measure wave height and wind speed. DSCOVR, a satellite at the L1 Lagrange point, photographs this glint daily from a million miles away.
