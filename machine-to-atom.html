An interactive descent

From the Machine to the Atom

The most complicated machine ever built exists to arrange atoms on a disc of sand. Fall the whole way down, from the machine to a single atom, and watch the scale collapse beneath you.

scroll to fall↓

The machine

$380 million

the price of one, from the only company on Earth that makes it

The most complicated machine ever built

Every advanced chip on the planet is printed by one kind of machine, made by one company, ASML, in the Netherlands. A High-NA EUV system costs around $380 million, ships in dozens of freight containers, and is bolted together on site over months. Its light is made by firing a laser at 50,000 droplets of molten tin a second; each hit flashes into plasma that glows at exactly the wavelength used to draw the chip.

The wafer

300 mm

one disc, sliced from a single crystal of silicon

A mirror grown from melted sand

The machine prints onto this: a 300 mm disc of silicon, sliced from one crystal grown out of melted, purified sand. The silicon is refined to about 99.9999999% pure, nine nines. Hundreds of identical chips are patterned across it at once, then sawn apart. The rainbow is real: it is light bending off features finer than its own wavelength.

The chip

~100,000,000,000

transistors, on a die the size of a fingernail

One chip, cut from the wafer

A single die. The newest carry tens of billions of transistors, each a switch flicking on and off billions of times a second. Around the rim run the connections to the outside world, thousands of contacts on the underside, with tens of thousands of microscopic bumps inside. Everything between those contacts is logic, wired together out of the switches.

The wiring

tens of km

of copper, folded into one fingernail of silicon

A city of copper in stacked floors

No photograph can follow past the surface, so from here we compute it. Between the contacts and the switches runs the wiring: copper threads in more than a dozen stacked layers, like the streets of a city seen from above. Unspooled, the wires in a single chip would run for tens of kilometers. The fat lines up top carry power; the finest, far below, are a few dozen atoms across.

The transistor

86,000,000,000

neurons in your brain, and a top chip now rivals the count

The switch, smaller than a virus

This is one transistor, the thing all of it is made of. A modern one is a few dozen nanometers across, smaller than most viruses, and it switches billions of times a second. A high-end chip packs around 100 billion of them. Your brain holds about 86 billion neurons, so a single chip now rivals it in raw count, yet each neuron wires to thousands of others, and the brain's ~100 trillion connections still dwarf anything we print.

The atom

0.5 nm

across one cell of the silicon crystal

Where the picture ends and the atoms begin

Keep falling and the material itself runs out of smallness. This is the silicon crystal, atoms fixed in a lattice that repeats about every half a nanometer. The thinnest working layers in a chip are only a handful of atoms thick. Build smaller and quantum mechanics stops cooperating. This is the floor. The whole machine, all $380 million of it, exists to place these dots, a few atoms at a time, a hundred billion times over.

4 m field of view

Start at a machine the size of a bus and end at a dot half a nanometer wide, and you have crossed about ten powers of ten without ever leaving one object.

That is the strange thing about a chip. It is the only place where the largest engineering effort humans have ever mounted and the smallest thing we can deliberately arrange meet in the same breath. A $380 million machine, a disc of nine-nines sand, a fingernail of a hundred billion switches, and underneath it all, atoms in a quiet lattice, waiting to be told what to do.
