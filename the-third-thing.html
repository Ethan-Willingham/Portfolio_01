Nature, Nurture, and One More Thing

The Third Thing

We spend forever arguing how much of a person is their genes and how much is how they were raised. Both answers leave out the part that is finally yours. A look at all three, through the lens of a very good dog.

scroll

Some of the most admirable people I can name came out of childhoods that should have wrecked them. Some of the worst came out of warm homes with every advantage. If your beginnings simply wrote your ending that should be impossible, and it keeps happening anyway.

The old argument has two sides: nature, the genes you were dealt, and nurture, the hand that raised you. I think it is missing a third, the one that is actually yours. The clearest way to see all three is a profoundly trained service dog, an animal we breed and raise on purpose. It takes us most of the way, then stops, right where the third thing begins.

01

What you're handed

Start with the genes. The cleanest evidence is identical twins separated at birth, and the Minnesota study of such pairs is uncanny: two men reunited at thirty-nine had each married a Linda, divorced, married a Betty, and named a son James Allan. Personality runs forty to fifty percent heritable, intelligence higher still, and stranger, its heritability rises with age, from about forty percent in childhood toward eighty in adulthood.

80%How much of the variation in adult intelligence tracks genes, up from about forty percent in childhood. Nature's grip tightens with age.the Wilson effect

But genes are not destiny. Heritability describes variation across a population, not a dial inside you, and the celebrated "warrior gene" predicts almost nothing alone. You can see how much weight nature really carries in what the professionals do: every serious guide-dog school breeds its own dogs, because calm nerves and a steady wish to work are substantially heritable. And yet, across thousands of sequenced dogs, breed predicts only about nine percent of an individual animal's behavior. Genes load the dice. They do not throw them.

02

What's done to you

Environment is no rounding error. The Adverse Childhood Experiences study followed seventeen thousand adults and found a clean dose-response: four or more kinds of childhood adversity made an adult about twelve times likelier to have attempted suicide. The Romanian orphanages added a twist, that timing is everything; children pulled out before age two recovered much of what later-rescued children never did. There are windows, and they do not stay open.

Then the story bends, and the bend is the hinge of this essay. When behavioral geneticists tally the causes of adult personality, the part explained by the shared family home turns out to be surprisingly small. Roughly half is left over, belonging to neither genes nor household, a residue the field grimly nicknames the "gloomy prospect," on the suspicion that it is simply random. Hold onto that leftover half.

Dogs show the nurture side just as cleanly. There is a sensitive window from about three to fourteen weeks: miss it, as puppy mills do, and you get a fearful, reactive adult; use it, with a volunteer who takes the puppy everywhere, and you build the calm that public work needs. One guide-dog study even found that the pups of indulgent mothers washed out more often than those of mothers who made them work for the milk. A frictionless start is not the gift it looks like. So a service dog is nature plus nurture, and genuinely good. But the recipe has exactly two ingredients, and for a dog that is the whole story.

03

The unexplained half

For a person, two ingredients never quite add up to a whole, and you see the gap in the lives that break the rules. Leopold and Loeb, brilliant and rich and given everything, murdered a fourteen-year-old boy in 1924 to prove they could. Frederick Douglass was born into slavery, torn from his mother, beaten weekly by a man hired to break him, and became the towering abolitionist of his century. Privilege wrote one ending and deprivation the opposite, which is the wrong way round.

The cleanest test is one household. Hermann and Albert Göring grew up in the same castles, the same comfort. Hermann became Hitler's deputy; Albert spent the war smuggling Jews to safety. "Albert was always the antithesis of myself," Hermann said, and that is the point: same blood, same house, opposite men. Something else did the deciding, and that something is the third thing.

04

The third thing

Here it is at its smallest. You cross an empty parking lot, a wrapper slips from your hand and misses the can, and no one is watching. Do you pick it up? Nothing depends on the wrapper. Everything depends on the answer, because you will face some version of it a few thousand times a week, and the answers harden, over years, into a person.

This is the oldest idea in ethics, and it is mechanical, not mystical. Aristotle said we are not handed our characters; we build them by repetition, becoming honest by telling the truth on the afternoon it costs us something, and then again. There is a line people misattribute to Freud: happiness is doing what you know is right, pleasure is doing what you want. The hard right thing costs now and pays later; the easy one pays now and bills you for the rest. The strongest witness for that learned it in the worst place on earth.

Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms, to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances.Viktor Frankl

Now the uncomfortable part. The wound was not your fault; a child does not choose the house it is born into, or the things done to it there. But there is a line adults cross without noticing, where the harm done to you becomes harm you are doing, and you keep it alive because setting it down would mean you no longer get to be the one it happened to. There comes a day to admit that some of the wreckage now has your fingerprints on it.

This is not a sermon; it is among the better-documented findings on how people change. The criminologist Shadd Maruna found that people who stopped offending and those who did not had nearly identical histories but different stories: the ones who changed owned their past, while the stuck ones blamed fate. The grimmest cycle is breakable too: roughly seventy percent of abused children never abuse their own. And of the children Emmy Werner followed on Kauai, a third of those born into adversity became thriving adults, usually thanks to one steady adult who believed in them. The window to start does not close.

70%Roughly the share of people abused as children who do not go on to abuse their own. The cycle is real, and most people refuse to pass it on.Kaufman & Zigler, 1987
05

What the dog can't do

Back to the dog, to find the edge of the parallel. Owners are sure their dog knows when it has done wrong, because of the guilty look. Alexandra Horowitz tested it, varying whether the dog had actually misbehaved and whether the owner thought so. The look tracked the scolding, not the crime; the guiltiest dogs were the innocent ones told off anyway. It is a signal aimed at us, not a conscience auditing itself.

Dogs have a sliver of fairness, but only the self-serving half: a dog sulks when another is paid and it is not, yet never objects when it gets the better deal. Frans de Waal, who argues animals carry most of the raw materials of morality, still draws the line here: they have empathy and reciprocity, but not the top floor, the stepping back to ask why and to hold yourself to a standard. As Christine Korsgaard puts it, an animal acts on its desires; a person can act on the thought that it ought to. A dog can be good; it cannot be responsible. Honest people contest this, but no study has shown a dog do the hard right thing, unwatched and unrewarded, simply because it judged that it should.

So the perfect service dog, refusing the dropped chocolate, lying still while a stranger weeps into its fur, is a masterpiece of nature and nurture, and genuinely good. But its goodness was handed to it, and that is why we can love it without complication. A person is the animal for whom that is not the whole story. You got your dice and your dealing, the same as the dog, and then the one thing the dog never gets: the wrapper in the empty lot, the grievance you could set down, the right thing with no one watching and no biscuit coming.

This is the opposite of a bleak thought. You did not choose your genes or your childhood, and if those were the only forces, many of us would be sunk. They are not the only forces. There is a third, small and constant and yours: the next ordinary choice, toward what you know is right instead of what you want, and then the one after that.

The dog cannot make that choice. You can. That is not a burden the dog has to carry. It is the one piece of you that nobody handed you, and nearly the only piece you were ever going to be judged by.
