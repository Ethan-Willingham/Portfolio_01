Nature, Nurture, and One More Thing

The Third Thing

We spend forever arguing how much of a person is their genes and how much is how they were raised. Both answers leave out the part that is finally yours. A look at all three, through the lens of a very good dog.

scroll

I keep coming back to a particular kind of unfairness. Some of the most admirable people I can name came out of childhoods that should have wrecked them. Some of the worst came out of warm houses, with two parents and a good school and every advantage. If your beginnings simply wrote your ending, that should be close to impossible. It keeps happening anyway.

The old argument about people has two sides. Nature, the genes you were dealt. Nurture, the hand that raised you. I have come to think the argument is missing a third side, and that the third side is the one that actually belongs to you. This is an essay about all three. The clearest way I have found to see them, strangely, is a dog.

Not just any dog. A profoundly trained service dog is almost a laboratory for nature and nurture, because we choose its parents on purpose and raise it on purpose, and we can watch, inside a single breed, the whole distance between a fearful animal that bites and one that will lie still under a restaurant table for two hours while its person eats. The dog will take us most of the way. Then it will run out, abruptly, at exactly the point where the third thing begins. The running out is the most human thing in this whole piece.

01

What you're handed

The genes you never chose, and how much they actually decide.

Start with the genes, because they are realer than the inspirational posters allow. The cleanest evidence comes from identical twins separated at birth and raised in different homes. The Minnesota Study of Twins Reared Apart spent two decades finding such pairs and testing them, and the results are uncanny. Jim Lewis and Jim Springer, separated at four weeks and reunited at thirty-nine, had each married a woman named Linda, divorced her, married a Betty, named a son James Allan, owned a dog named Toy, vacationed on the same short stretch of Florida beach, and suffered the same tension headaches. Another pair, Oskar and Jack, were raised on opposite sides of the Second World War, one a Hitler Youth in Germany, the other a Jew in the Caribbean, and still both flushed the toilet before using it and liked to startle people with loud sneezes in elevators.

You have to be careful with stories like these, because a long enough list of traits will always cough up coincidences. But the systematic version holds. Identical twins raised apart end up strikingly alike on the things we measure, and the measurements keep landing in the same places: the big personality traits run forty to fifty percent heritable, and intelligence climbs even higher.

One finding here is genuinely counterintuitive, and worth holding onto. The heritability of intelligence does not fade as you grow up. It rises with age, from around forty percent in childhood to as much as eighty percent in adulthood, while the imprint of the specific household you grew up in fades toward nothing. As you get older you select your own world, and you tend to select one that fits the disposition you were born with.

80%How much of the variation in adult intelligence tracks genes, up from about forty percent in childhood. Nature's grip tightens with age, not the other way around.the Wilson effect

It is just as important to say what genes do not do, because this is where the subject gets abused. Heritability is a fact about variation across a population, not a dial inside one person, so "eighty percent heritable" never means eighty percent of you was set at conception. The famous "warrior gene" turned out to predict almost nothing on its own. The most honest summary of modern gene science is that we can read a whole genome and still predict how far someone will go in school about as well as we could by asking their parents' income. Genes load the dice. They do not throw them.

02

A dog is a bet on blood

Why every serious guide-dog school in the world runs a breeding program.

Watch what the professionals actually do, and you will see how much they trust nature. The great guide and service schools do not take whatever dog turns up. They keep their own breeding colonies, and they keep them small and deliberate, because they have learned that the temperament a guide dog needs, calm nerves, low aggression, a steady wish to work with a person, is substantially heritable. The classic estimate put the heritability of guide-dog success near forty-four percent, high enough that selecting the right parents measurably improves the next generation. One school, breeding for sound hips across eight generations, lifted the share of excellent-hipped dogs from the low thirties to the nineties. You can move a bloodline. That is the whole premise of a breed.

And yet the same science carries a warning that will matter later. When researchers sequenced the genomes of more than two thousand dogs, they found that breed, the thing we treat as destiny, explains only about nine percent of the behavior of any individual dog. Heritable in the aggregate, almost unpredictable in the particular. You can stack the odds with breeding, and then you still have to meet the actual animal in front of you.

9%How much a dog's breed predicts its own individual behavior. The pattern is real across thousands of dogs, and nearly silent about any single one.Morrill et al., Science, 2022
03

What's done to you

The childhood you didn't pick, and the surprising shape of its footprint.

Now the other side. The environment is not a rounding error, and the proof is grim. The Adverse Childhood Experiences study asked seventeen thousand adults to count the kinds of harm they had lived through young, abuse, neglect, a house in chaos, and then followed their health. The relationship was a clean dose-response curve. Someone carrying four or more kinds of childhood adversity was about twelve times more likely to have attempted suicide than someone with none, and far more likely to struggle with addiction, depression, and disease decades later. The body keeps the score.

12×The odds of an adult suicide attempt for someone with four or more kinds of childhood adversity, against someone with none. Environment is not a rounding error.Felitti & Anda, 1998

The cruelest natural experiment we have are the children of the Romanian orphanages, raised in real deprivation and then adopted into ordinary families. They told us two things at once. The damage was severe, and timing was everything: children rescued before their second birthday recovered a great deal, while those who waited longer carried the marks for life. There are windows in a childhood, and they do not stay open.

But here the story bends in a way almost no one expects, and the bend is the hinge of this whole essay. When behavioral geneticists split a personality into its causes, they keep finding that the part explained by the shared family home, the values and habits and parenting style siblings have in common, is surprisingly small in adulthood. Something like half of the variation is left over, belonging neither to genes nor to the household, and the field has a bleak nickname for it: the "gloomy prospect," the worry that this leftover is unsystematic, idiosyncratic, in a word, random. Hold onto that leftover half. I do not think it is all random.

04

And a dog is raised

The window, the puppy raiser, and a small surprise about tough love.

A well-bred puppy is not yet a service dog. Between roughly three and fourteen weeks there is a sensitive window, mapped by researchers in the 1960s, when a little experience does an enormous and lasting amount of work. Miss it, and you get the dogs that come out of puppy mills, deprived of early handling and disproportionately fearful and reactive for the rest of their lives. Use it well, with a volunteer who takes the puppy everywhere, to the train and the supermarket and the vet, and you build the calm that public work demands. This is nurture, doing in a dog exactly what it does in us.

There is a lovely wrinkle in the data, too. Researchers at a guide-dog school measured how the mothers treated their litters and found that the puppies of attentive, indulgent mothers, the ones who nursed lying down so the milk came easy, were more likely to wash out. The better guides came from mothers who made their puppies work a little for it, who stood up and forced them to climb. A small early dose of effort and frustration, it seems, built the steadier adult. Even for a dog, a frictionless start is not the gift it looks like.

So a service dog is nature plus nurture, laid out as clearly as we ever get to see it. Sound bloodlines, the right window, patient training, a good match with a handler. Stack all four and you get something close to a miracle of reliability. And it is, I want to insist, genuinely good. But notice that the recipe has exactly two ingredients. For a dog, that really is the whole story.

05

The unexplained half

The lives that nature and nurture cannot finish explaining.

For a person, two ingredients do not add up to a whole. Set the genes and the upbringing on the table and there is always something left over, and you can see it most plainly in the lives that refuse to follow the rules.

Take privilege turned to ruin. Nathan Leopold and Richard Loeb were the brilliant sons of wealthy Chicago families, fluent, university-educated, given everything, and in 1924 they murdered a fourteen-year-old boy essentially to prove they were clever enough to get away with it. Hans Frank was a cultured man with a doctorate in law who became the Nazi governor-general of occupied Poland and presided over mass murder, and went to the gallows at Nuremberg for it. Neither man can be explained by deprivation, because there wasn't any.

Then take the cleanest test of all, two children of one household. Hermann and Albert Göring were raised in the same castles, by the same family, in the same comfort. Hermann became Hitler's deputy. Albert spent the war using the family name to smuggle Jews and dissidents to safety, and was vouched for afterward by dozens of the people he saved. "Albert was always the antithesis of myself," Hermann said, and they were right about that if nothing else. Or the Booth brothers, sons of one famous actor: Edwin, who once saved Abraham Lincoln's son from death under a train, and John Wilkes, who killed Lincoln.

And then the direction we love best, deprivation turned to greatness. Frederick Douglass was born into slavery, taken from his mother as an infant, and beaten week after week by a man hired to break him, and became the towering abolitionist of his century. Charles Dickens was pulled out of school at twelve to paste labels in a warehouse while his father sat in debtors' prison. Sonia Sotomayor grew up in a Bronx housing project, gave herself her own insulin injections from the age of seven, and lost an alcoholic father at nine, and sits today on the Supreme Court.

You cannot run these lives on nature and nurture alone. The same blood, the same house, the same beatings, and at the end of them, opposite human beings. Something else did work in them. The something else is the third thing.

06

The third thing

The thousands of small choices that no one makes for you.

Here is the third thing at its very smallest. You are crossing an empty parking lot, and something slips out of your hand, a wrapper, a receipt, and it misses the trash can by a foot, and there is not another soul in sight. Do you walk back and pick it up? Nothing at all depends on the wrapper. Almost everything depends on the answer, because you will face some version of that question a few thousand times before the week is out, and the answers do not evaporate. They accumulate. They harden, over years, into a person.

This is the oldest idea in moral philosophy, and it is mechanical, not mystical. Aristotle said we are not born with our characters, we build them, the way you build any skill, by repetition. You do not become honest by admiring honesty. You become honest by telling the truth on the particular afternoon it costs you something, and then again, until it is simply what you do.

We become just by doing just acts, temperate by doing temperate acts, brave by doing brave acts.Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics

William James, two thousand years later, gave the mechanism a name. Every choice is a vote, he wrote, and the nervous system keeps the tally; we are "spinning our own fates, good or evil," one small act at a time, until the thing we practiced becomes the thing we are. There is a line people like to pass around, usually pinned on Freud, who never actually wrote it: happiness is doing what you know is right, pleasure is doing what you want. He did not say it, but the distinction under it is real and very old, the same one Aristotle drew between a flourishing life and a merely pleasant one. The hard, right, unwitnessed thing tends to cost now and pay later. The easy, wanted thing pays now and quietly bills you for the rest. A life is mostly which of those trades you make by reflex.

The strongest witness for the third thing learned it in the worst classroom on earth. Viktor Frankl came out of the death camps convinced that even there, stripped of everything, a person kept one thing.

Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human freedoms, to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances, to choose one's own way.Viktor Frankl

Now the uncomfortable part, and I want to handle it with care, because it sits a hair away from cruelty. The wound was not your fault. A child does not pick the house it is born into or the things that are done to it there, and nothing in this section is permission to tell a hurt person that their hurt was a choice. But there is a line that adults cross without noticing, where the harm that was done to you becomes harm you are now doing, to yourself and to the people standing closest to you, and you keep it alive because putting it down would mean you no longer get to be the person it happened to. There comes a day when the honest thing is to admit that some of the wreckage now has your fingerprints on it, that you carried the grievance well past the point where it was protecting you and into the years where it was only a weapon, often pointed at people who never did the original wrong.

This is not a sermon. It is one of the better-documented findings in the study of how people change. The criminologist Shadd Maruna compared people who had committed crimes and then genuinely stopped against people who kept going, and found the difference was not in their histories, which were often identical, but in the story each told about himself. The ones who changed told what he called a redemption script: they took ownership of the past and cast themselves as the author of what came next. The ones who stayed stuck told a condemnation script, all fate and blame, "I had no choice." Same wreckage, different story, and the story was the thing that predicted the future. Other researchers call the move "knifing off," the deliberate act of cutting the past loose so it stops dragging.

And the grimmest cycle of all turns out to be breakable. We are often told that abused children become abusers, as if it were a law. It is not. The best estimate is that roughly seventy percent of people who were abused as children do not go on to abuse their own. The risk is real, several times the ordinary rate, and most people overcome it anyway, quietly, by deciding to be the place the inheritance stops.

70%Roughly the share of people abused as children who do not abuse their own. The cycle is real, and most people still refuse to pass it on.Kaufman & Zigler, 1987

If you want the most hopeful version, it is on a Hawaiian island. Emmy Werner followed nearly seven hundred children on Kauai for more than three decades. Of the third born into real adversity, poverty, stress, troubled homes, about one in three grew into what she called competent, confident, caring adults. The protective ingredient was rarely dramatic. Usually it was one steady adult who believed in the child, often not a parent, and the child's own reach toward that person. And some who were a mess in their twenties still turned it around in their thirties. The window to begin doing the right thing does not close. That, to me, is the quiet engine under every redemption story: there is no last train.

07

What the dog can't do

Where the most human thing shows up, by being missing.

Now back to the dog, to find the edge of the parallel. We said a trained dog shows us nature and nurture with rare clarity. Here is what it cannot show us, and the absence is the point.

Most owners are certain their dog "knows it did wrong," because of the guilty look, the ears and the slink when you come home to a mess. The cognitive scientist Alexandra Horowitz tested exactly this. She varied two things independently: whether the dog had actually broken the rule, and whether the owner believed it had. The guilty look tracked the scolding, not the crime. The dogs that looked guiltiest of all were the innocent ones who got told off anyway. The expression is an appeasement signal aimed at us. It is not a conscience auditing itself.

The rest of the evidence points the same way. Dogs have a sliver of something like fairness, they will sulk and quit when another dog is paid for a trick and they are not, but they show no sign of the other half, the half that matters: a dog never once objects when it is the one getting the better deal. That is wounded self-interest, not a principle. Their self-control is real but it is temperament, not a rule they hold themselves to when the rule is inconvenient.

The primatologist Frans de Waal, who has spent a career arguing that animals have far more of the raw materials of morality than we credit, still draws the line in the same place. Animals have the building blocks, empathy, reciprocity, even a kind of social pressure. What they do not have is the top floor: the capacity to step back, to ask why a thing is right, to hold yourself to a standard you could put into words. The philosopher Christine Korsgaard puts it most sharply. An animal acts on its desires. A human being can act on the thought that it ought to, can place a reason above an urge and be moved by the reason. A dog can be good. It cannot be responsible.

I want to be fair to the other side, because honest people dispute this. Marc Bekoff and Jessica Pierce argue there is no clean moral gap at all, and they have real evidence, rats that will free a trapped companion before taking a treat. The building blocks are not nothing, and anyone who has been loved by a dog knows it. But across all of it, no study has ever shown a dog do the hard right thing, unprompted, unrewarded, with no one watching, simply because it had judged that it should. That specific act is the one we have never found outside our own species.

So the perfect service dog, the one that picks up the dropped keys and refuses the dropped chocolate and lies still while a stranger weeps into its fur, is a masterpiece of nature and nurture, and it is genuinely, deeply good. But its goodness was handed to it. It was bred, and it was raised, and that is the entire story, and that is precisely why we can love it without complication. A person is the animal for whom that is not the entire story. You got your dice and you got your dealing, the same as the dog. Then you were given the one thing the dog never gets: the wrapper in the empty lot, the grievance you could finally set down, the harder right thing with no one watching and no biscuit coming.

I find this is the opposite of a bleak thought. You did not choose your genes, and you did not choose your childhood, and if those were the only two forces in a life, a great many of us would be sunk before we started. They are not the only two. There is a third, and it is small and constant and entirely yours: the next ordinary choice, made in the direction of the thing you know is right instead of the thing you happen to want, and then the one after that.

The dog cannot make that choice. You can. That is not a burden the dog has to carry. It is the one piece of you that nobody handed you, and very nearly the only piece you were ever going to be judged by.
