# OPTIONAL_BODY.md: The Optional Body

The canonical living doc for the shipped page `optional-body.html`. It owns the concept, the content arc, every vetted fact with its sources, the accuracy/sensitivity guardrails, and the visual craft rules that govern the CSS.

---

## 1. What the page is

A scroll-driven descent where the reader takes a human body apart piece by piece and keeps discovering it is fine. The descent starts with parts you did not know you had, sinks through organs you would miss but survive, past limbs, down to a torso wired to machines, and finally into the brain itself, until the question stops being "what can you remove?" and becomes "where did the *you* go?"

The whole piece is a subtraction game. The reader removes things; the body keeps living; a quiet status line holds green the entire way. Until the very bottom, when it does not.

**The structural through-line (the spine):** every machine in the deep tiers replaces a mechanical or filtering job: a pump (heart), a bellows (lungs), a sieve (kidneys). The two things we cannot replace are the liver and the brain, the organs that *create and decide* rather than move things. The descent is the slow reveal that "you" is not plumbing.

**The turn:** in the brain section the rule inverts. We show the body surviving astonishing structural brain loss, then land the gut-punch: a body in a persistent vegetative state can breathe, sleep, and open its eyes for years with, as far as we can tell, no one home. Remove the right thing and the body lives but the person is gone.

**The landing:** so *you* were never the parts. You are the pattern, the one item on the list nobody can locate on a scan, cleanly remove, or even agree how to define. The body is almost entirely optional. The self is the one non-negotiable, and we do not know what it is made of.

**Format:** a native site post: 780px column, forest-green `#303931` background, parchment accent `#D4C4A0`, Century Supra serif. The page background stays constant; mood rides the anatomical engravings on cream plates and a left-margin weight gauge.

## 2. Tone and sensitivity (read before writing a single line of copy)

These are real surgeries on real people. The deep tiers touch amputation, organ donation, brain injury, disorders of consciousness, and death. Get the tone wrong and the piece is ghoulish instead of awed.

- **Curious and a little awed, never mocking.** The "funny" is cosmic-absurd ("the body is a clown car of optional parts"), never cruel about a specific condition or person.
- **Real people, named with respect.** Paul Alexander (70+ years in an iron lung), Craig Lewis (first pulseless artificial heart), quadruple-amputee survivors: these are humans, not exhibits.
- **Survival is not unchanged.** Never say "perfectly fine." People living without these parts have real deficits and real maintenance. The honest framing ("you can live without this, here is the cost") is *more* interesting than a fake "no downside."
- **Bust the myths, do not repeat them.** The single biggest credibility risk is parroting pop-science. See §4 for the specific myths to name and correct.
- **Humility at the consciousness tiers.** PVS vs. minimally-conscious misdiagnosis runs ~37-43%; some "vegetative" patients show covert awareness on fMRI. Never state "no one home" as certain; state it as what we can detect.
- **Footer note:** "These are real procedures performed on real people. This is a curiosity piece, not medical advice."

## 3. The descent: tiers and stations

Each station = one body part = a short scene + one verified fact. Facts are research-round-1 verified; caveats below are load-bearing. Field format: **What it is / Removal / Survivability (+ cost) / The hook / Source(s) / Caveat.**

### Tier 0: The frame

**Spine ligament (personal hook).** Open on a microdiscectomy: a piece of the spine (ligamentum flavum, partially removed) and the surgeon says you will never miss it. He is right. That is the rule of the game: we only remove what you can live without.

> Verify the exact structure removed in a standard microdiscectomy (ligamentum flavum vs. partial laminectomy) before finalizing copy. This is the author's own surgery and can be told first-person.

### Tier 1: "You won't even notice"

The light tier. Several stations include a **do-it-yourself body check** the reader can perform right now.

- **Palmaris longus** (self-check). A thin wrist-flexor tendon. ~10-15% of people are simply born without it (range ~4% to >25%, ethnicity-dependent); surgeons harvest it for grafts because it is expendable. Zero effect on grip or pinch. Interaction: touch thumb to pinky and flex the wrist. Does a cord pop up? About 1 in 7 see nothing. Sources: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palmaris_longus_muscle), [PMC prevalence](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10708979/), [PMC grip/pinch](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3652998/). Caveat: "15%" is a textbook average; quote a range.

- **Appendix.** A finger-sized blind pouch off the large intestine. The "useless organ" cliche is wrong: the leading hypothesis (Duke, 2007) is it is a "safe house" that re-seeds gut bacteria after illness, and it has evolved independently 30+ times in mammals. Source: [NIDDK](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/digestive-diseases/appendicitis/definition-facts), [Duke Health](https://corporate.dukehealth.org/news/appendix-isnt-useless-all-its-safe-house-bacteria). Caveat: frame as "maybe not useless," not "is essential" -- a well-supported hypothesis, not settled fact.

- **Wisdom teeth.** ~22% of people globally are born missing at least one (range ~5-40%, highest in East Asians). Hook: evolution mid-act; shrinking jaws are dropping the back molars out of the gene pool (heritability 60-80%). Source: [PMC agenesis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7678444/).

- **Accessory navicular bone.** An extra foot bone present in ~10-12% of people; only ~0.1% ever have trouble with it. Hook: 1 in 10 people walk on a bonus bone and never know. Source: [PMC meta-analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11405447/).

- **Coccyx (tailbone).** 3-5 fused vertebrae, the literal remnant of a tail; removable via coccygectomy. Hook: you grew an actual tail (~10-12 vertebrae, week 5-6) and reabsorbed it by week 8. Source: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccyx). Caveat: it anchors pelvic-floor muscles and bears sitting weight; "useless" overstates it.

- **Auricular (ear) muscles.** Vestigial ear-swivel muscles; only ~10-20% can wiggle their ears. Hook: a 2025 study found these "dead" muscles still fire electrically when you strain to listen -- your brain still tries to point ears that no longer move. Source: [Frontiers 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/news/2025/01/31/ear-muscle-wiggling-ears-activates-listening-frontiers-neuroscience).

- **Darwin's tubercle.** A small point on the ear's rim. Named by Darwin himself in *The Descent of Man* as the leftover tip of a folded-over pointed ancestral ear. Source: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darwin%27s_tubercle). Caveat: prevalence figures are inconsistent; it is not a simple dominant gene (old textbook myth).

- **Plica semilunaris ("third eyelid").** The pink crescent in the eye's inner corner. It is the vestige of the nictitating membrane: the milky third eyelid visible flicking across a bird's or cat's eye. Yours cannot move. Source: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plica_semilunaris_of_conjunctiva). Caveat: it aids tear drainage; call it vestigial as a third eyelid, not entirely idle.

- **Arrector pili / goosebumps.** Tiny muscles that yank each hair upright. Hook: goosebumps are you trying to fluff a fur coat you no longer have. Source: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrector_pili_muscle). Note: the same muscle-nerve unit helps regulate hair-follicle stem cells (Hsu lab, *Cell* 2020), so not entirely pointless.

- **Tonsils and adenoids.** Immune tissue at the throat and nasal back; >500,000 tonsillectomies/yr in U.S. kids under 15. Hook: yanked out reflexively for decades, then doctors realized they are functioning immune tissue -- the surgery is far less casual now. Source: [NCBI StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK536942/). Caveat: frame as "removable and survivable," not "useless."

> **Tier-1 framing tip:** split honestly into true leftovers (palmaris, wisdom teeth, navicular, coccyx, ear muscles, tubercle, plica, arrector pili) vs. "surprise, not useless after all" (appendix, tonsils). The reversal is the better hook.

### Tier 2: "Okay, that's serious. You're still fine."

Real organs, real surgery, demonstrably full lives. Order follows a free-to-costly difficulty ramp. Cost tags: **(A)** free / no maintenance, **(B)** daily pill, **(C)** machine, **(D)** donor organ.

- **Gallbladder (A).** One of the most common abdominal operations. Hook: nothing replaces it; the liver switches from batch-delivering bile to a permanent slow drip, and you do not notice. Source: [NIH StatPearls](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448145/). Caveat: annual-volume figures disagree; say "hundreds of thousands a year," not a point number.

- **One kidney (A).** Living donors show no drop in life expectancy. Hook: ~1 in 750-1,000 people have only one kidney (renal agenesis) and most find out by accident on an unrelated scan. Source: [NIDDK](https://www.niddk.nih.gov/health-information/kidney-disease/solitary-kidney), [UCLA Health](https://www.uclahealth.org/news/article/donating-kidney-does-not-affect-life-expectancy). Caveat: ~1 in 2,000 births vs ~1 in 750-1,000 living; cite the range.

- **Breasts and nipples (A).** Mastectomy; not a survival organ. Hook: surgeons can remove all internal breast tissue through a hidden incision while keeping skin and nipple (nipple-sparing); immediate reconstruction went from ~10% (1980s) to ~90% today. Source: [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/procedures/nipple-sparing-mastectomy).

- **Spleen (B).** Liver and lymph nodes absorb most of its jobs. Hook: the lifelong threat (overwhelming post-splenectomy infection / OPSI) is rare (~5% lifetime) but terrifyingly fast -- a "normal" fever can become death within ~12-24 hours. Requires lifelong vaccines and possibly standby antibiotics. Source: [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/procedures/14614-splenectomy), [OPSI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overwhelming_post-splenectomy_infection).

- **One lung (A).** Pneumonectomy; the other lung expands to fill the space. Hook: people do not just live with one lung, they exercise with it. Capacity halves; daily life is normal. Source: [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/25003-pneumonectomy).

- **Liver -- showstopper (A partial / D full).** Partial hepatectomy takes 25-65%. Hook: the only internal organ that regrows. As little as ~10-25% can regenerate to full size in ~8-12 weeks; a living donor gives away most of a lobe and both halves grow back. Source: [UPMC](https://www.upmc.com/services/transplant/services/procedures/living-liver-donor-surgery), [PMC regeneration](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7665117/). Give this station room; it is the emotional peak of Tier 2.

- **Colon / large intestine (A, rerouted).** Total colectomy; surgeons build a new "rectum" out of the small intestine (the J-pouch) so you go normally without a bag. Hook: the J-pouch itself. Source: [Cleveland Clinic](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/21062-j-pouch-surgery).

- **Uterus / reproductive organs (A, or B if ovaries removed).** Hysterectomy, ~500-600k/yr in the U.S. Hook: by their 70s, nearly 1 in 2 U.S. women have had one (~21% overall; ~44% at ages 70-74). Keep the ovaries and hormones continue normally. Source: [PMC trends](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9529796/), [CDC NCHS](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data/series/sr_13/sr13_092.pdf). Caveat: the popular "1 in 3 by 60" is age-band-dependent; use verified figures.

- **Stomach (B).** Total gastrectomy joins the esophagus straight to the small intestine. Hook: with no holding tank, food dumps into the intestine ("dumping syndrome"), so you eat many tiny meals all day; plus lifelong B12 (the stomach produced the factor needed to absorb it). Source: [NCI](https://www.cancer.gov/news-events/cancer-currents-blog/2024/preventive-gastrectomy-long-term-consequences).

- **Thyroid -- gut-punch (B, non-negotiable).** Thyroidectomy. Hook: the first organ on the whole list you cannot live without maintenance. Survivable only with a daily levothyroxine pill, forever; stop it and severe hypothyroidism can be fatal. But the "machine" replacing an entire gland is one tiny pill each morning. Source: [American Thyroid Association](https://www.thyroid.org/thyroid-hormone-treatment/). End Tier 2 here; it is the cliff into the machine tiers.

### Tier 3: "Now we're getting weird"

Heavy visuals. The figure is now down to a torso and head.

- **Quadruple amputation.** People live full, active lives with no arms and no legs (torso, organs, brain untouched). Hook: Curley Christian, buried alive at Vimy Ridge in 1917, became the Commonwealth's only known surviving WWI quadruple amputee and supported his family selling cigars; modern survivors run businesses and train for marathons. Source: [Legion Magazine](https://legionmagazine.com/quadruple-amputee-inspired-others/), [TIME](https://time.com/4103487/quadruple-amputee-veteran-todd-nicely/). Caveat: these are notable achievers, not a typical baseline.

- **External face structures.** Nose, ears, eyes, jaw: each individually survivable, usually for cancer. Hook: the outer ear is acoustically almost optional (a passive funnel; removing it does not by itself cause deafness), and an entire jaw can be rebuilt from a leg bone (fibula free flap). Source: [Mayo Clinic](https://www.mayoclinic.org/medical-professionals/otolaryngology/news/restoring-hearing-after-ear-removal-in-patients-with-skin-cancer/mac-20591149), [OncoLink](https://www.oncolink.org/cancers/head-and-neck/treatments/surgical-treatments/mandibulectomy). Caveat: jaw removal usually means reconstruction, not living jawless long-term.

### Tier 4: "The bare minimum"

The figure is a core wired to a wall of machines. Every mechanical or filtering job can be outsourced.

- **Heart: continuous-flow LVAD / total artificial heart.** The signature beat. Hook: a conscious, talking patient on a continuous-flow device can have no detectable pulse at all; the peer-reviewed AHA journal titled its review "Living Without a Pulse." Craig Lewis (2011, Texas Heart Institute) had his native heart removed; doctors heard not a heartbeat but a steady whir. Source: [Circ Heart Fail](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29903893/), [Texas Monthly](https://www.texasmonthly.com/the-culture/houstons-pulseless-artificial-heart-revisited/). Accuracy: say "no pulse / non-pulsatile flow" for LVAD patients (the native heart still has electrical activity, not a flatline EKG). Reserve "no heartbeat" for Craig Lewis whose heart was actually removed. Do not conflate the two cases.

- **Lungs: ECMO / ventilator.** Hook: gas exchange happens entirely outside the body. People have lived 200+ days on ECMO awaiting a lung transplant (a 207-day case; a 403-day case reported). Paul Alexander lived 70+ years in an iron lung, earning a law degree and practicing as an attorney from inside it. Source: [Johns Hopkins ECMO](https://www.hopkinsmedicine.org/health/treatment-tests-and-therapies/extracorporeal-membrane-oxygenation), [Guinness](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/news/2023/3/meet-longest-surviving-iron-lung-patient-kept-alive-by-a-machine-for-70-years-741881).

- **Kidneys: dialysis.** Hook: "average" dialysis survival is quoted at 5-10 years, yet record-holders have lived ~43-47 years on it. Source: [Guinness](https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/longest-time-having-kidney-dialysis-(haemodialysis)).

- **The wall: what cannot be replaced.** There is no machine that replaces the liver (its toxins are protein-bound; "artificial liver" devices only bridge temporarily) and none for the brain. Structural through-line landing: machines replace pumps, bellows, and sieves. The liver and the brain create and decide. That is the doorway into the final tier.

### Tier 5: "What is the *you* that's left?"

The body tolerates astonishing structural brain loss.

- **Hemispherectomy.** Half the cerebrum removed or disconnected, usually in children with catastrophic epilepsy. Hook: in the Johns Hopkins series of 71 children, IQ changed less than 15 points in 34 of 53, 8 improved, and 65% became seizure-free. Language and personality often intact thanks to childhood neuroplasticity. Source: [Pulsifer et al., *Epilepsia* 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15009226/). Caveat: true only for early surgery on an already-damaged hemisphere; adult outcomes are far worse.

- **The Feuillet 2007 *Lancet* case.** A 44-year-old French civil servant whose skull was largely filled with cerebrospinal fluid (chronic hydrocephalus), brain compressed to a thin rim. Married, two kids, employed, IQ ~75. Source: [*Lancet* 370:262](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(07)61127-1). Accuracy -- bust the myth loudly: the viral "90% of his brain missing" is misleading. The tissue was compressed, not absent; "90%" is an eyeballed ventricle estimate; and he was not unimpaired (IQ 75). Honest version: "most of his cranial volume was fluid, his brain squeezed thin -- and he lived an ordinary life anyway."

- **Split-brain.** Corpus callosotomy severs the ~200 million-fiber bridge between hemispheres; each disconnected half can respond to things the other denies seeing (Sperry won the 1981 Nobel). Hook: a patient's left hand could pick out an object his speaking self insisted he never saw. Source: [PMC review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7305066/). Accuracy: the strong "two consciousnesses" reading is no longer consensus; Pinto et al. (2017) found unified response control even when perception splits. Present as an open mystery, not "two minds." Honest verdict: the evidence is insufficient to answer this question.

- **Cerebellar agenesis.** Born without a cerebellum (which holds ~80% of the brain's neurons). Hook: a woman reached adulthood -- walking unsteadily, speaking, married -- before a scan revealed her entire cerebellum was simply missing. Only ~a dozen living cases are documented. Source: [*Brain* 2015](https://academic.oup.com/brain/article/138/6/e353/269537). Caveat: real motor and cognitive deficits. Astonishing survival and compensation, not "unaffected."

### Tier 6: The turn and the ending

- **Brain death vs. PVS vs. MCS -- defined accurately.** Brain death = irreversible loss of all brain function including brainstem (legally and medically dead; machines only). Persistent vegetative state = wakefulness without awareness (brainstem intact: sleep-wake cycles, breathing, eye-opening for years). Minimally conscious state = intermittent, reproducible awareness. Hook: the arc made literal. In PVS a body can breathe, sleep, wake, and open its eyes for years with no one detectably home. Source: [Merck Manual](https://www.merckmanuals.com/professional/neurologic-disorders/coma-and-impaired-consciousness/vegetative-state-and-minimally-conscious-state). Sensitivity: never use brain-dead / vegetative / coma interchangeably. PVS vs. MCS misdiagnosis ~37-43%; "no one home" must stay humble.

- **The landing: where the knife can't reach.** We mapped, cut, drained, and halved the body and the brain, and the one thing the journey was about -- the felt experience of being *you* -- is the one thing no scan can point to. The hard problem of consciousness (Chalmers): why is any of this felt at all? Integrated Information Theory (Tononi) is a leading but contested hypothesis (publicly challenged in 2023; flag it, do not present it as proven). Source: [IEP hard problem](https://iep.utm.edu/hard-problem-of-conciousness/), [SEP neuroscience of consciousness](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consciousness-neuroscience/). Honest bottom line (this is the ending, not a cop-out): consciousness currently cannot be located, cleanly removed, or even agreed-upon as defined.

- **Final beat.** The status line shifts from "Still alive" (green, checkmark) to "Still you?" with no checkmark. The last thing on screen is the reader.

## 4. Accuracy guardrails (myth-busting checklist -- never ship without this)

1. **Appendix and tonsils are not "useless."** The real story is the reversal: science called them junk, then found their jobs.
2. **Feuillet: not "90% of his brain missing."** Compressed, not absent; eyeballed estimate; IQ 75.
3. **Split-brain: not "two consciousnesses."** Contested; recent work favors more unity. An open question.
4. **No-pulse (LVAD) vs. no-heartbeat (Craig Lewis's removed heart) are different claims.** Keep them distinct.
5. **Brain-dead does not equal vegetative does not equal coma.** Brain death is death. PVS vs. MCS misdiagnosis ~37-43%; state awareness humbly.
6. **IIT is a contested hypothesis, not established science** (2023 open-letter controversy).
7. **Darwin's tubercle is not a simple dominant gene** (old textbook myth).
8. **Phrase as ranges, not points:** palmaris prevalence, gallbladder annual volume, single-kidney prevalence, hysterectomy prevalence, liver percentage that regrows.
9. **"Survivable," never "no downside."** Every removal carries real cost; say it.

## 5. Sourcing to-do before publish

- **Swap pop-medical links for clinical bodies.** Audit all links for any non-authoritative source; prefer NIDDK / NCI / CDC NCHS / NIH StatPearls / Cleveland Clinic / Mayo / Johns Hopkins / peer-reviewed journals.
- **Verify the author's own surgery detail** (microdiscectomy: ligamentum flavum vs. partial laminectomy) for the Tier 0 first-person frame.
- **Re-confirm every headline number** against its primary source at copy-freeze.
- **Double-check record figures** (iron lung "70+ yrs," dialysis "~43-47 yrs," ECMO "207/403 days"). Guinness and peer-reviewed citations are safest; these drift.

## 6. Visual craft rules (the shipped CSS)

These rules describe what is literally in the page CSS. They govern every future edit to the page's look.

### Depth from light, not weight

- Raise elements by lightening the surface plus a 1px top highlight, not by stacking black shadow. Build a surface ramp off the green: base `#303931`, raised `~#283129`, overlay lighter.
- **Layered, hue-matched shadow stack.** Never one shadow, never pure black. Stack 3 to 5 layers with doubling blur and offset, tinted toward the background hue (e.g., `hsl(150 32% 4% / a)`). As elevation rises, increase offset and blur, decrease opacity. One light source page-wide.
- **Hairlines, not borders.** On dark, use a translucent light line (e.g., `rgba(232,226,214,0.11)`) plus an inset top highlight to fake a bevel. Render at 1 device pixel; never pure `#000` or `#fff`.

### Type

- **One modular scale** (1.25 major third), 19px base, defined as tokens. Derive sizes by ratio, then trust the eye and nudge.
- **Measure:** 60-75 characters for body, 45-55 for captions. Leading ~1.55 for body, tighter as size grows.
- **Display tracking:** negative on large serif headings (-0.015em to -0.02em, never past -0.03em), zero on body, never tighten small text. Use `font-optical-sizing: auto`.
- **Figures:** old-style figures in running prose (`font-variant-numeric: oldstyle-nums`), tabular lining figures in data and the gauge (`tabular-nums`) so counts do not jitter.
- **`text-wrap: balance`** on headings and captions; **`text-wrap: pretty`** on body. Bind stubborn widows with a non-breaking space.
- **Dark-mode type:** never pure white (parchment `#D4C4A0` is correct). Dim body slightly and reserve full parchment for headings so hierarchy comes partly from luminance. Go one weight lighter on dark than on white.

### Motion

- **Transform and opacity only** (compositor). Never animate width, height, top, or margin per frame. `will-change` only while animating, then remove.
- **Default to ease-out.** Use `cubic-bezier(0.23, 1, 0.32, 1)` for entrances, reveals, and the gauge. Reserve ease-in for exits, ease-in-out for on-screen movement.
- **Durations:** micro 100-160ms, small 125-200ms, medium 200-350ms, large 350-600ms. For a contemplative piece sit at the slow end, but never use a sluggish curve.
- **Cross-fade:** overlap the fades so density stays constant, with a subtle settling scale (1-3%) on the incoming plate. For premium feel, map the fade to scroll position so scrubbing reverses cleanly.
- **The weight gauge:** animate the bar with `transform: scaleX()` (origin at the depleting edge), synced to the same duration and curve as the counting number, with tabular figures so digits do not shift. Count via requestAnimationFrame, not a CSS property.
- **Reduced motion:** keep opacity fades, remove transform-based motion and parallax, snap the count to its final value. Motion is enhancement, never load-bearing.

### Scrollytelling craft

- **One image, one beat, one idea.** Each scroll step does exactly one thing.
- **Never jack the scroll.** Map motion to native scroll position so fast and slow readers both feel right. This is the single biggest premium-vs.-amateur tell.
- **Earn the first scroll with stillness.** Open on one quiet treated plate, a small parchment title, and negative space. No autoplay, no instruction. The image and the silence are the invitation.
- **Captions are labels, not paragraphs.** Aim near a 280-character ceiling. The plate is the protagonist; the words are the wall label.
- **Motion only where it deepens.** Make the cross-fade itself the craft (a plate dissolving into the next like a memory). Reserve larger motion for one or two narrative peaks.
- **Atmosphere through consistency.** One green, one parchment, one accent. Treat every plate identically (same duotone, grain, vignette) so they read as one recovered atlas. Let large fields of dark green do nothing; that emptiness is the luxury.
- **Land the ending.** Pull back from the last detail to a final held image and a closing line in parchment, then quiet. The descent must arrive somewhere, not run out of scroll.
